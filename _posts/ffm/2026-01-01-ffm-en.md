## History of Frankfurt am Main
![FFM Altstadt]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Altstadt.jpg)
### ***The Dawn of the City***

There are two cities in Germany named Frankfurt. One is Frankfurt (Oder), located east of Berlin on the border between Germany and Poland. The other is the protagonist of this series: Frankfurt am Main, situated 500 kilometers away. Many travelers are familiar with Frankfurt am Main, as its airport serves as the starting point for most journeys into Germany. For convenience, we will simply refer to it as Frankfurt.

The earliest area of human activity in Frankfurt was the Cathedral Hill (Domhügel), located between the City Hall (Römer) and the Cathedral. Celts settled here as early as 450 BC. By the beginning of the Christian era, the Germanic Chatti tribe migrated to this region; the name "Hesse" is derived from this tribe.

Around 70 AD, the Roman Empire expanded into the Rhine-Main region and established a military base on Cathedral Hill. Today, remains of Roman baths can still be seen in the Archaeological Garden in front of the Cathedral. As Roman influence deepened, an administrative center was established in Nida (modern-day Heddernheim). To the north, the Upper Germanic-Rhaetian Limes was constructed to protect the territory.

By the mid-3rd century, under increasing pressure from Germanic tribes, the Romans withdrew. The right bank of the Rhine was occupied by the Alamanni. Around 500 AD, the Franks defeated the Alamanni, turning the lower Main region into a base for eastward expansion. They built a royal palace here to control the strategically important river.

### ***A Stronghold of the Empire***

Frankfurt first appeared in documents in 794 AD. In a deed of gift issued by Charlemagne, it was called Franconofurd, meaning "Ford of the Franks." That same year, he convened a major church synod and imperial assembly here. While buildings from this era no longer stand, the Archaeological Garden preserves Carolingian palace ruins, including the aula regia built by Louis the Pious.

In 855, the Frankfurt Cathedral (Kaiserdom St. Bartholomäus) held its first election for a Frankish king, choosing Lothar II. By the 12th-century Hohenstaufen dynasty, Frankfurt had become the fixed site for royal elections. The Golden Bull of 1356 formally designated Frankfurt as the legal location for imperial elections. Between 855 and 1792, a total of 36 kings were elected here, 10 of whom were crowned Holy Roman Emperors in the city—starting with Maximilian II (1562) and ending with Francis II (1792).

### ***Medieval Trade Hub and Urban Expansion***

Frankfurt’s urbanization was closely tied to its flourishing trade. Originally a fortified royal residence managed by a Vogt (advocate), it grew rapidly during the Hohenstaufen period (1138–1254). Long-distance trade boomed, and management shifted to a Schultheiß (mayor). The Staufen Wall (Staufenmauer) and the Saalhof Chapel (Saalhofkapelle) remain as witnesses to this prosperity.

In 1254, Frankfurt moved toward autonomy and joined the Rhenish League of Cities. By 1311, the city gained limited self-governance. Emperors like Ludwig the Bavarian granted the city numerous privileges to ensure the safety of its markets. The autumn and spring fairs evolved into vital hubs for international trade, earning Frankfurt the nickname "the department store of Germany."

Around 1480, book fairs became a staple of the Frankfurt markets. Following the rise of printing around 1530, the city became a core hub for the European book trade—a tradition that continues today with the Frankfurter Buchmesse, the world's largest book fair.

### ***Religious Conflict and the Reformation***

Between the 13th and 14th centuries, religious fanaticism swept Europe, leading to the persecution of Jewish communities. Between 1461 and 1463, under imperial orders, Frankfurt's Jews were forced into a ghetto built outside the Staufen Wall.

The Reformation gained support in Frankfurt starting in 1520; Martin Luther even stayed here en route to the Diet of Worms. In 1525, an uprising led by guilds combined religious reformation with social protest against the conservative clergy and nobility. By 1533, the City Council officially moved forward with the Reformation. Following the Peace of Augsburg in 1555, Frankfurt became a religiously mixed city, welcoming Protestant refugees from the Netherlands who significantly impacted the local economy.

### ***Autonomy and Development (14th–18th Century)***

As the middle class grew, so did the demand for self-governance. However, power remained concentrated among the patricians and wealthy guilds. This led to civil unrest in 1350, 1366, and notably in 1612–1614. The latter conflict, though originally targeting the council's mismanagement, tragically turned into a pogrom against the Jewish community.

The 18th century saw a burgeoning culture of civic patronage. Dr. Johann Christian Senckenberg founded medical and scientific institutions in 1763 for the public good. During this period, Frankfurt's most famous citizen, Johann Wolfgang von Goethe, was born (1749), laying the foundation for modern German literature and culture.

### ***Grand Duchy, Nationalism, and the German Confederation***

In 1806, Napoleon established the Confederation of the Rhine, and the Holy Roman Empire dissolved. Frankfurt briefly became the Grand Duchy of Frankfurt (1810) under Karl von Dalberg, an enlightened ruler who abolished serfdom and reformed education. After Napoleon’s defeat in 1813, the Duchy ended.

The 1815 Congress of Vienna designated Frankfurt a Free City and the seat of the Bundestag (Federal Diet) of the German Confederation. This status attracted diplomats and fueled cultural growth, leading to the founding of the Städel Art Institute. In 1848, the wave of revolution led to the first German National Assembly at St. Paul’s Church (Paulskirche), where representatives drafted a constitution for a united Germany, though the movement ultimately failed.

### ***From Free City to Modern Metropolis***

In 1866, Prussia defeated Austria, and Frankfurt was annexed by Prussia. By 1871, the Treaty of Frankfurt ended the Franco-Prussian War, and the city entered a period of rapid modernization. Between 1891 and 1912, it transformed into an international industrial center.

The economy flourished with companies like Adlerwerke (bicycles and cars). In 1914, the University of Frankfurt—the first foundation-based university in Germany—was established. Banking remained the city's backbone, with roots dating back to the 1400s. The Rothschild family established a global financial network here, and the Frankfurt Stock Exchange became a central pillar of European finance.

### ***The World Wars and Today***

During WWII, Allied air raids in 1944 leveled the historic old town. In March 1945, US forces arrived, ending Nazi control. Post-war, Frankfurt became the administrative heart of the combined occupation zones and the seat of the Deutsche Bundesbank.

Today, Frankfurt is Germany's fifth-largest city and a premier global financial hub, housing nearly 260 credit institutions. Its skyline is a symbol of trade and finance. It boasts one of Europe's busiest airports and a major cultural landscape, including the Museumsufer (Museum Embankment). With its high quality of life and international atmosphere, this historic metropolis continues to thrive.

## Frankfurt Cathedral (Kaiserdom St. Bartholomäus)
![FFM Dom]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_St_Bartholomäus.jpg)
Frankfurt Cathedral, with its 95-meter-high steeple, stands tall in the heart of Frankfurt's city center. Strictly speaking, it is not a "cathedral" in the traditional sense, as it never served as the seat of a bishop. However, it is one of the few churches to bear the title "Imperial Cathedral" (Kaiserdom). According to the Golden Bull of 1356 issued by Charles IV, this site was designated as the place for the election of German kings of the Holy Roman Empire. Furthermore, between 1562 and 1792, a total of ten German kings were crowned Holy Roman Emperors here.

The church we see today is the fifth known structure on this site. Before 680 AD, there was a small Merovingian chapel and a Carolingian hall church. In 852, the Salvatorbasilika was built as part of the Carolingian Royal Palace. In 1239, the late-Romanesque Bartholomew Choir (Bartholomäuschor) was inaugurated to honor the Apostle Saint Bartholomew (the cathedral's most precious relic is the cap of his skull). The Gothic nave and aisles visible today were constructed starting in 1260, while the magnificent West Tower began construction in 1415.

In 1867, the cathedral was severely damaged in a major fire. Subsequently, Franz Joseph Denzinger rebuilt the cathedral in a Neo-Gothic style based on the original blueprints by the 1415 cathedral architect, Madern Gerthener. For the first time, a spire was added to the bell tower, allowing it to pierce the city skyline at a height of 95 meters.

After World War II, the cathedral underwent years of restoration to return to its current state. Entering the church, the first thing that catches the eye is the Baroque Maria-Himmelfahrt-Altar (Altar of the Assumption). Passing through the vestibule into the nave, you will see vibrant red walls, the result of repainting during renovations from 1992 to 1994. On the south side of the cathedral lies the Wahlkapelle (Election Chapel), where royal elections took place during the Middle Ages.

For those interested in learning more about Frankfurt Cathedral, the adjacent museum houses many treasures from the cathedral's treasury. Visitors with good stamina can also challenge the 324 steps to climb the tower between April and October each year to enjoy a panoramic view of Frankfurt.

## St. Paul's Church (Paulskirche)
![FFM Paulskirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Paulskirche.jpg)
As early as 1270, a Gothic Franciscan monastery stood on what is now Paulsplatz. This building was expanded over time and became Frankfurt's largest Evangelical church in the 16th century during the city's rapid growth. Due to decay and lack of space, the church was demolished in 1786-1787. Construction of a new church, primarily using red Main sandstone, began in 1789. However, the process was far from smooth; financial difficulties caused by the Napoleonic Wars interrupted work for nearly thirty years, and it was not completed until 1833. The new Neoclassical church, designed with an oval floor plan, was officially opened as the main Evangelical church and named Paulskirche in honor of the Apostle Paul.

Following the outbreak of the French Revolution in 1848, national consciousness across Europe surged, and calls for unity and freedom grew rapidly within the German Confederation. Although Vienna and Berlin were undoubtedly the political power centers of the Confederation, those within the movement for German unity and freedom never doubted that the future German National Assembly should be held in Frankfurt.

On March 5, 1848, at the Heidelberg Assembly (Heidelberger Versammlung), 51 politicians gathered to discuss the future political direction. They decided to form a "Committee of Seven" (Siebenerausschuss) to prepare for the upcoming Frankfurt Preliminary Parliament (Vorparlament). The Preliminary Parliament, consisting of 573 members including regional representatives, assembled in Frankfurt on March 31, 1848. Although originally intended for the Kaisersaal (Imperial Hall) in Frankfurt City Hall, the meeting was moved to St. Paul's Church due to the limited space and the enthusiastic crowds of Frankfurt citizens. The session lasted until April 4, primarily discussing the election methods, organizational structure, and powers of the future National Assembly. Ultimately, participants decided to elect the National Assembly through universal suffrage and planned to formally convene in Frankfurt on May 1. They also established a "Committee of Fifty" to oversee and push forward election preparations alongside state parliament representatives.

Due to organizational difficulties, the National Assembly did not officially open until May 18, 1848. The opening ceremony was originally planned for the Kaisersaal, but for the same reasons, 382 deputies eventually walked to St. Paul's Church to begin their first working session. Subsequently, the assembly decided to make St. Paul's Church its permanent venue until May 30, 1849 (with a brief interruption during the winter of 1848-49).

To meet the needs of the parliament, St. Paul's Church underwent several modifications. These included the installation of gas lighting, the addition of wooden galleries to improve acoustics, and the installation of Germany's first central heating system in the winter of 1848-49. During the heating installation, the assembly temporarily moved to the German Reformed Church at Kornmarkt (which no longer exists) from November 6, 1848, to January 9, 1849.

On December 20, 1848, the National Assembly passed the "Declaration of the Basic Rights of the German People" (Grundrechte des Deutschen Volkes). After ten months of intense debate, the "Constitution of the German Empire" (Verfassung des Deutschen Reiches) was finally passed on March 27, 1849, and officially promulgated the following day. This constitution aimed to establish a federal, unified German state. Except for Austria, all other 38 states of the German Confederation were to be members. Under the constitution, the head of Germany was to be a hereditary emperor with the power to appoint the government. The Imperial Diet (Reichstag) would consist of the House of States (Staatenhaus) representing the states and the House of the People (Volkshaus) elected by the public.

Unexpectedly, King Frederick William IV of Prussia refused the imperial crown, citing the concept of divine right. As conservative forces pushed back, the National Assembly's power began to disintegrate. Representatives from Austria and Prussia were recalled by their governments, and deputies supporting the constitutional monarchy resigned. On May 30, 1849, the remaining 135 deputies—mostly from the left—decided to move the assembly to Stuttgart to seek support from the Württemberg government. Ultimately, the remaining members were forcibly dispersed by the Württemberg army on June 18. The constitution and the efforts to unify Germany under the National Assembly ended in failure.

Because St. Paul's Church holds significant symbolic meaning as the "Cradle of German Democracy," it was among the first buildings in Frankfurt to be rebuilt after World War II. On May 18, 1948, on the centenary of the first National Assembly, the church was reopened as a political memorial and a venue for important ceremonies and awards. To this day, St. Paul's Church remains a symbol of the German people's tireless pursuit of national unity, democracy, and freedom.

## Frankfurt City Walls
![FFM Eschenheimerturm]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Eschenheimerturm.jpg)
When people mention Frankfurt's city walls, most might only think of a few remaining towers or the "Wallanlagen," the green belt of parks surrounding the city. However, this is far from the full picture. In Frankfurt's history, there were actually three different walls built at different times, traces of which can still be found in various corners of the city.

The earliest wall dates back to the 9th century, originally built to protect the Carolingian Royal Palace. It is estimated that this wall, about two to three meters high, existed around 1000 AD. While its exact location is debated, it is certain that the area it covered was very small. Its remains were only discovered during the development of the Old Town in the early 20th century and post-war reconstruction.

The second wall was built during the Hohenstaufen era in the 12th century (around 1180). Standing seven meters high, it surrounded the entire Old Town and had three main gates: the Bornheimer Pforte at the north end of Fahrgasse, the Katharinenpforte next to St. Catherine's Church, and the Guldenpforte at the west end of Weißfrauengasse. Information about this wall is abundant; parts of the ruins are still visible today, and many street names are related to it—for instance, streets ending in "-graben" refer to the moats outside the wall. Additionally, a street named "An der Staufenmauer" preserves a section of the wall with 15 arches. For a long time, it was thought to be the original Staufen wall, though it was later proven to be a reconstruction following a fire in 1719.

The third wall was built in 1333, following Emperor Ludwig IV's approval for city expansion. It enclosed the main urban area north of the Main River and also covered Sachsenhausen on the south bank. This stage of the wall not only expanded the range but also significantly upgraded defensive designs, featuring moats up to 10 meters wide and bastions for artillery.

The wall had several gates, the most critical being the Galgentor on the west side, leading to Mainz. Although most of these gates no longer exist, their names remain in city place names, such as Eschenheimer Tor, Allerheiligentor, and Friedberger Tor.

Among the remaining defensive towers of this wall, the most famous is the Eschenheimer Turm, built in the early 15th century. It is not only a landmark of Frankfurt but also the oldest and most representative tower. Additionally, several original watchtowers have been preserved, such as the Galluswarte, Bockenheimer Warte, Friedberger Warte, and Sachsenhäuser Warte.

The Staufen wall was not immediately demolished after the third wall was completed. In the eastern part of the city, it served as the boundary separating the Jewish Quarter (Judengasse) from the new city. It was only from 1582 onwards that the Staufen wall began to be gradually dismantled, though it remained intact within the Jewish Quarter. The third wall was also demolished during the Napoleonic era after losing its military function, replaced by today's Wallanlagen. Walking along this green belt today, one can still feel the historical context of Frankfurt's walls and the city's transformation.

## Römer
![FFM Römer]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Römer.jpg)
For over six centuries, the destiny of Frankfurt has been closely intertwined with the Römer. In 1405, the Frankfurt City Council purchased the buildings on the square for 800 gold coins (Gulden), and it has served as the site of Frankfurt City Hall ever since. Over the following years, nine more buildings and several inner courtyards were added, eventually forming the Römer complex we see today.

The significance of the Römer surpassed the city itself from the very beginning. The magnificent Imperial Hall (Kaisersaal) was once the meeting place where Prince-electors held deliberations before the election of German kings, earning it the title "House of the Empire" (hus des riches). The hall houses 52 portraits of German kings and Holy Roman Emperors and is open to the public, making it a must-visit destination for history enthusiasts.

Frankfurt City Hall suffered severe damage during World War II. Reconstruction began in 1945, and it was not until 1975 that the famous three-gabled facades of the Römer were restored to their former glory.

## Fountain of Justice (Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen)
![FFM Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen.jpg)
The Fountain of Justice on the Römerberg is arguably the most photogenic spot in Frankfurt. Originally built in 1543, this fountain is a concrete manifestation of the civic identity and legal values of Frankfurt's citizens. The octagonal fountain and the statue of Lady Justice we see today were crafted from red sandstone by sculptor Johann Hocheisen in 1611. It has been preserved to this day and remains the oldest surviving public fountain in Frankfurt.

Did you know, however, that since the coronation of Emperor Matthias in 1612, water has flowed from the breasts of the four nymphs at the corners of the base, symbolizing fertility and prosperity? During imperial coronations, wine even flowed from the fountain. Excited Frankfurt citizens would swarm the fountain in such a heated atmosphere that immediate repairs were often required afterward.

Looking at the crowds of tourists here today, it’s not hard to imagine the scene back then!

## Senckenberg Society for Nature Research (Senckenberg Gesellschaft für Naturforschung)
![FFM_Senkenberg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Senkenberg.jpg)
The Senckenberg Society for Nature Research (SGN) aims to promote natural science research and share scientific knowledge with the public. It is not affiliated with the hospitals, research institutes, museums, and foundations (Dr. Senckenbergische Stiftung) established by Johann Christian Senckenberg in 1763. Instead, it was founded spontaneously in 1817 by a group of citizens passionate about natural sciences.

Among the museums under the SGN, the most famous is the Senckenberg Nature Museum Frankfurt. It showcases a wide variety of dinosaur fossils and precious prehistoric remains unearthed from the Messel Pit. Another highlight is the deep-sea research section, which introduces various deep-sea creatures and the essential instruments used for deep-sea exploration. A tour here will definitely broaden your understanding of the vast ocean.

## Bockenheimer Warte
![Bockenheimer Warte]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Brokenheimerwarte.jpg)
Followers who have played "Age of Empires" are likely familiar with watchtowers or sentry towers. The Bockenheimer Warte, located on the west side of Frankfurt, is a classic real-world example. Built around 1435, it served as the boundary between Frankfurt and the town of Bockenheim at the time.

However, I personally find the nearby subway entrance more interesting than the tower itself. From a distance, it looks like a train carriage bursting through the ground and getting stuck. The meaning is unclear, but it is a very creative design.

## Palais Frankfurt (Palais Thurn und Taxis)
![FFM Palais]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Palais.jpg)
As one of Frankfurt's largest shopping centers, the western entrance of MyZeil connects to the Palais Frankfurt, which originally belonged to the Thurn und Taxis family.

The Thurn und Taxis family originated from the Lombardy region of Italy. At the end of the 13th century, Omodeo Tasso and his family members founded a courier company (Compagnia dei Corrieri), establishing a postal route from Milan through Venice to Rome, which is considered one of the earliest modern postal systems in Europe. Due to their royal authorization and the successful construction of a high-efficiency postal network, the Thurn und Taxis family gradually gained a monopoly over European postal services. From 1489 onwards, they were appointed as the Imperial Postmasters (Kaiserliche Reichspost) of the Holy Roman Empire, responsible for operating postal services throughout the Empire and Spain.

In the 18th century, the residence of the Thurn und Taxis family was originally located in Brussels, but they later planned to move to Frankfurt. Despite opposition from various parties, construction of this late Baroque-style building finally began at the end of 1731 and was completed eight years later, named after the patron, Prince Anselm Franz von Thurn und Taxis. The Imperial Post Office operated by the family subsequently moved into the palace. Between 1805 and 1813, it served as the residence of Karl Theodor von Dalberg, the Grand Duke of Frankfurt. From 1816 to 1866, it also served as the meeting location for the German Confederation.

In 1895, the palace came under the full ownership of the Imperial Post (Reichspost). Ten years later, the City of Frankfurt took over the building and established an ethnological museum there. During the air raids at the end of World War II, the palace was severely damaged. Although parts of the building structure remained well-preserved, the building was ultimately completely demolished during the construction of a telecommunications tower. In the early 2000s, through the reconstruction project of the Palais Quartier, the Palais Frankfurt was given the opportunity to be rebuilt according to its original appearance. Today, the palace houses restaurants, office and retail spaces, and an event center.

## Frankfurt Central Station (Frankfurt Hbf)
![FFM Hbf]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/FFM/FFM_Hbf.jpg)
Frankfurt Central Station is one of Germany's busiest railway hubs. Although it is very close to the city center, this area was merely farmland during the Middle Ages. With the wave of industrialization in the 19th century, railways became the arteries of economic and population movement. To cope with rapidly growing transportation demands, the city government selected this open area as the site for the new central station. Construction began in 1883, designed by architect Hermann Eggert, and it officially opened on August 18, 1888.

The newly completed station features a Neo-Renaissance style. If you look closely at the roof, you can see a statue of Atlas supporting the Earth, with personifications of Electricity and Steam beside him, symbolizing the progress of the city and technology. At the time of its opening, this new station was the largest railway station in Europe until the completion of Leipzig Central Station in 1915. Setting aside the chaotic atmosphere in front of the station, it is actually a very magnificent place.

In April 2025, the station square was named Emilie-und-Oskar-Schindler-Platz to commemorate their efforts (the protagonists of Schindler's List) in saving Jews from the tyranny of the Nazi regime. I must say, I only learned through research that Oskar Schindler used to live near Frankfurt Central Station (Am Hauptbahnhof 4), basically right next to where the Malaysian restaurant is located. Interested friends on Threads can take a special look.

## Berlin Airlift Memorial (Luftbrückendenkmal)
Aviation fans have definitely visited the "Airplane Alley" near Songshan Airport. In Frankfurt, there is a similar place located right next to the Berlin Airlift Memorial (Luftbrückendenkmal). Here, you can not only watch planes take off and land but also get a full view of the traffic flowing on the highway.

If you arrive at the airport too early, you can consider walking over or taking a bus. It is also very convenient to get there from other places; take the S-Bahn to Zeppelinheim station and walk for a few minutes, or simply drive there.

In fact, the Airlift Memorial at Frankfurt Airport (and the one near Celle/Hanover) is just a replica. The original is located at the Platz der Luftbrücke in Berlin (see [Berlin City Introduction](https://joedventure.github.io/adventure_in_de/en/berlin/berlin-city/#spot-10) for details), used to commemorate the aerial operation that supplied West Berlin residents during the Berlin Blockade.

After World War II, Berlin was divided into East and West Berlin. Since West Berlin did not share a border with any other Western occupation zones, West Berlin's supplies were cut off after the Soviet Union blocked all rail and road traffic between West Berlin and the Western occupation zones on June 24, 1948. Until May 12, 1949, all raw materials such as food, medicine, and coal could only be transported into West Berlin by Allied aircraft. Some American planes became known as "Raisin Bombers" (Rosinenbomber) because they dropped candy for children.

During the blockade, the Allies mainly used three air corridors for the West Berlin supply operation: the northern route to Hamburg, the southern route to Frankfurt, and the western route to Hanover. Most Allied planes took off and landed at Tempelhof Airport, handling up to about a thousand take-offs and landings per day. Therefore, the Airlift Memorial is designed with three vertical ribs, symbolizing the three air corridors that connected West Germany and West Berlin. In this arduous operation, a total of 39 Britons, 33 Americans, and 5 German workers lost their lives, and a bronze strip on the base of the memorial commemorates these victims.

The Airlift Memorial in Frankfurt also has two transport aircraft parked there: a Douglas C-47 Skytrain and a Douglas C-54 Skymaster with the Rosinenbomber livery.

## The Golden Book (Goldenes Buch)
Beyond its famous landmarks, Frankfurt is home to two books of profound historical significance.

When Frankfurt welcomes distinguished guests, they are met not only with a warm reception but also with a highly ceremonial task: signing the Golden Book. Since 1904, this book has served as a place where countless key historical figures have left their mark. From emperors to chancellors, sports stars to top scholars, their signatures are more than just mementos—they are witnesses to the intersection of the city’s history with that of the world.

The ledger itself is a masterpiece of art. It is usually kept securely in a vault at the City Hall, managed by the municipal government, and is only carefully brought out for special occasions. Weighing 20 kilograms and measuring about half a meter in length, it requires a specially designed cart for transport. As its name suggests, the cover was crafted in gold leaf by the Italian artisan Augusto Varnesi in the early 20th century, featuring an engraving of Charlemagne at the center, surrounded by various coats of arms and precious stones. This design evokes the Council of Frankfurt in 794 AD, which marked the city's first appearance in historical documents.

## The Golden Bull (Goldene Bulle)
Another historically significant document preserved in Frankfurt is the Golden Bull (Goldene Bulle). Those who follow my updates likely have some familiarity with it, but it certainly deserves a proper introduction here.

The Golden Bull is the most important constitutional document of the Holy Roman Empire. This unassuming-looking booklet holds fundamental significance for both the Empire and the city. Spanning 44 pages, it records the imperial laws promulgated in 1356 regarding royal elections: the King of Germany was to be elected by seven Prince-electors based on a majority rule. Frankfurt was designated as the site of election, following Aachen as the site of coronation, while the new king was required to hold his first Imperial Diet in Nuremberg. To emphasize the importance of this new law, the document was sealed with a golden seal (Latin: bulla) rather than the traditional wax. It wasn't until around 1400 that the term "Golden Bull" came to refer to the entire decree.

Initially, only five of the seven Prince-electors received a copy. Later, the Imperial Cities of Frankfurt and Nuremberg applied for their own official, legally sealed copies. In 1366, the Frankfurt City Council obtained its copy from the Imperial Chancery. This Frankfurt copy was repeatedly referenced during every royal election, as there was no single, unified official version for the entire Empire.

With the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire in 1806, the Golden Bull lost its legal force. Although it was once dismissively called a useless piece of parchment, its historical allure has never faded over the next 200 years. The Nazi-led Frankfurt government originally intended to present the Golden Bull to Hitler during his visit in 1938; however, Hitler only received a 15th-century German translation—which remains missing to this day—while the original fortunately escaped the same fate.

Due to the threat of air raids in 1942, the Golden Bull was moved from the Kaisersaal (Emperor's Hall) to a secure location. Since 1969, it has been carefully preserved in the charter archives of the Institute for City History. In 2013, the seven copies of the Golden Bull, along with King Wenceslaus's luxury manuscript from 1400 (housed in the National Library in Vienna), were included in the UNESCO Memory of the World Register.

## Frankfurt Gastronomy
***Apple Wine (Apfelwein)***

In Frankfurt's restaurants, you will often see "Apfelwein" on the menu, and you can even find hot mulled apple wine at the Christmas markets. As Frankfurt's national drink, this is perhaps not surprising at all.

When you taste it for the first time, the unique tartness of apple wine might feel a bit "hardcore." However, bravely trying a second glass is definitely worth it. Especially in summer, this refreshing and thirst-quenching drink is the perfect choice (for adults only 😆).

Frankfurt's apple wine culture dates back to around 1600. Generally, brewing begins in October, and the wine develops different flavors depending on the length of fermentation. For instance, the fresh juice pressed in autumn is cloudy, contains no alcohol, and ferments quickly; this stage is called "Rauscher" and can be drunk directly. Only after a longer fermentation process in wooden barrels does it become true apple wine.

If you want to try the most authentic way of drinking, follow the tradition: serve the apple wine from a "Bembel" (a blue-gray ceramic jug) and drink it from a "Geripptes" (a traditional diamond-patterned glass). This sense of ritual truly enhances the apple wine experience!

***Frankfurter Würstchen***

Did you know that the hot dogs we see so often actually originated in Frankfurt?

The German sausages we frequently find in convenience stores have a history much longer than you might imagine. As early as the Middle Ages, Frankfurter sausages were highly prized for their unique aroma. They even appeared as a side dish for grilled meats during the coronation ceremony of Emperor Maximilian II.

To craft an authentic Frankfurter, pure pork is first stuffed into sheep's small intestines and then smoked at a low temperature using beechwood for one hour. Only through this process can the sausage acquire its golden hue and rich fragrance. In the 19th century, Johann Georg Lahner, a master butcher from Frankfurt, brought this craft to Vienna. By adding beef to the original recipe, he created what is known as the Vienna sausage (Wiener Würstchen). This sausage later took Europe by storm; it is said that even the Austrian Emperor loved pairing them with beer for breakfast.

The birth of the hot dog was simply a creative invention by American immigrants who placed Frankfurter or Vienna sausages inside a bun. Because the sausage's long, thin shape resembled a badger dog, the dish was originally called the "Dachshund sausage."

It is fascinating how a single sausage can connect the world—truly, gourmet food knows no borders.

***Beef Sausage (Rindswurst)***

In addition to the classic Frankfurter, another sausage made from beef is equally famous. The way to enjoy it is the same as the Frankfurter: simply heat it up in hot water. Fragrant and crunchy with a deep meaty flavor, it is an absolute perfect match when eaten with yellow mustard and potato salad!

This sausage also boasts a century of history, dating back to 1894 when it was first created by butcher Karl Gref and his wife Wilhelmine Völsing. Since it was made with pure beef, it became highly popular among Jewish customers at the time. To this day, this family business continues to operate in Frankfurt's East End.

***Green Sauce (Grüne Soße)***

Green Sauce (Grüne Soße) is a quintessential Frankfurt spring dish and is said to have been Goethe's favorite food. This sauce is made from sour cream and seven fresh herbs: parsley (Petersilie), chives (Schnittlauch), chervil (Kerbel), watercress (Kresse), salad burnet (Pimpernelle), sorrel (Sauerampfer), and borage (Borretsch). It is typically enjoyed with potatoes and boiled eggs, though it is also frequently served with fish, asparagus, or boiled beef. These herbs are each believed to have health-promoting properties, such as boosting metabolism and being rich in iron.

The preparation of Green Sauce is quite simple: the herbs are finely chopped by hand and mixed with sour cream, salt, pepper, vinegar, and mustard. Diced boiled eggs are then stirred into the sauce, and finally, a bit of horseradish (Meerrettich) is added according to personal taste.

Green Sauce is so popular that there is even a dedicated Green Sauce Festival held annually to celebrate this delicacy. Have any of you "Threads" friends tried it yet?

***Mit Musik***

When dining in Hesse, you will sometimes see the option "Mit Musik" on the menu. I remember being quite confused at first; my friend wouldn't help translate and even kept egging me on to order it...

As it turns out, "Mit Musik" is a special way of eating in Hesse and a classic appetizer found in local restaurants. It is generally served as "Handkäse mit Musik" (Hand cheese with music). Essentially, matured hand-made cheese is marinated in a mixture of vinegar, oil, apple wine, diced onions, caraway seeds, salt, and pepper. It is enjoyed alongside apple wine and buttered bread. One legend says that true Frankfurters only use a knife to eat this dish. As for the "music," it refers to the sounds produced by the stomach after digesting the onion marinade. As for what that means—I’ll leave that to your imagination.

Personally, I find Handkäse quite acceptable. There is also Harzer Käse from the Harz region, which has a similar texture but an even stronger and more intense flavor. I wonder if any friends have tried that?

Regarding the "Mit Musik" style, my personal favorite is the Odenwald specialty, Kochkäse/Brennkäse mit Musik. Whether spread on bread or topped on a schnitzel, the rich, smooth cheese combined with the slight pungency of raw onions achieves a delicate balance—it is truly a great delight.
