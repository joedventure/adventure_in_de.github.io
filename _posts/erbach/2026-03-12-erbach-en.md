## The History of Erbach
Just a short distance south of Michelstadt lies the town of Erbach. The two towns are so close that a merger referendum was held in 2007. However, the proposal did not pass; otherwise, they would be a single municipality today.

The origins of Erbach date back to 1095, appearing in the Lorsch Codex as "Ertbach." While records of its early history are limited, a castle serving as the residence for the Counts of Erbach (Grafen von Erbach) already existed in the Mümling valley around 1180. This castle was first mentioned in official documents in 1303, marking Erbach's importance as a noble estate.

In 1532, Holy Roman Emperor Charles V elevated the Schenken von Erbach family to Imperial Counts, further consolidating local aristocratic rule. Subsequently, the Erbach Town Hall was completed in 1545, and the settlement was granted town rights in 1560. However, the Black Death and the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648) dealt heavy blows to Erbach, causing its development to stagnate for a time.

It was not until the 18th century that the city entered a new era of prosperity. In 1775, Count Franz I (Graf Franz I. zu Erbach-Erbach) ascended to power. In 1783, he introduced the art of ivory carving to the region, ushering in a new age of artistic craftsmanship. This craft reached its peak in the 19th century; at the 1873 Vienna World's Fair, the "Erbach Rose" (Erbacher Rose), an ivory masterpiece by local carvers, won a bronze medal, bringing international fame to Erbach's ivory artistry.

In the 20th century, Erbach focused on cultural and tourism development. The Ivory Museum opened in 1966 and was renamed the German Ivory Museum (Deutsches Elfenbeinmuseum Erbach) in 1971, becoming one of the few museums in Europe dedicated to ivory art. In 2005, Erbach Castle was acquired by the State of Hesse and reopened in 2006, allowing the public to visit the Count's collections and private apartments.
