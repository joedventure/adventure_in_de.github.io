## History of Berlin
Human activity in the area including Berlin dates back to the Last Glacial Period around 10,000 BC. Germanic tribes began migrating here in 600 BC, but by 200 BC, they gradually moved south and were replaced by Slavic tribes. They established numerous settlements and towns along the Spree and Havel rivers, such as Spandau and Köpenick. This continued until 1157, when the Slavic Duke Jaxa von Köpenick was displaced by the powerful Germanic Ascanian family, specifically Albert the Bear (Albrecht der Bär), the Margrave of Brandenburg. This vast territory subsequently became part of the Holy Roman Empire.

The history of Berlin itself can be traced back to around the 12th century. Situated between Spandau and Köpenick, the area developed into two adjacent cities with the rise of trade: Cölln, first documented in 1237, and Berlin, first recorded in 1244. These two towns united in 1307 to form a city union, and 1237 is regarded as the official founding year of Berlin. Due to the early Slavic rule, the name "Berlin" originates from the Slavic language: a combination of the root Berl- (meaning swamp) and the Slavic suffix -in, reflecting the geographical characteristics of the local terrain.

Berlin became a member of the Hanseatic League in 1360 (until 1518), although its status was less prominent than other core members. However, the Golden Bull of 1356 promoted the Margrave of Brandenburg to one of the seven Prince-electors of the Holy Roman Empire, increasing Berlin's importance. Subsequently, Berlin became the center of rule for the House of Hohenzollern and gained even greater significance as the capital of the Kingdom of Prussia after its establishment in 1701.

Between 1806 and 1808, Berlin was occupied by French forces led by Napoleon, and Prussian remained under French control until 1814. For the next nearly 60 years, Prussia continued to contend with France, Russia, and Austria, eventually achieving German unification in 1871 and establishing the German Empire (Deutsches Reich), with Berlin naturally becoming its capital.

The events that followed are well-known. The wars of the first half of the 20th century brought immense trauma to Germany and Berlin. After the war in 1945, Berlin was divided into East and West, becoming a focal point of the Cold War. This situation remained until the fall of the Berlin Wall on November 9, 1989, and after German reunification on October 3, 1990, Berlin officially became the capital of Germany as it remains today.

## Old Berlin City Wall
![Berlin alte Stadtmauer]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Alte Stadtmauer.jpg)
By the end of the Thirty Years' War, the Berlin city walls had almost completely collapsed, with only a few remains existing around Littenstraße and Waisenstraße. To strengthen urban defense, the Great Elector Friedrich Wilhelm decided in 1658 to build a sturdy fortress for the city. He entrusted this major responsibility to the talented engineer Johann Gregor Memhardt, who designed a star-shaped fortress comprising 13 bastions, 6 major outer fortifications, and 6 city gates. The eastern fortifications were completed between 1658 and 1662, while the western side was not finished until 1683; however, due to the terrain, the height of the western wall was far below the original design.

In the 18th century, due to damage to the walls and urban expansion, the Berlin Fortress was eventually demolished and replaced by the new Berlin Customs Wall (Berliner Zoll- und Akzisemauer). Today, the Berlin Fortress no longer exists, and its former presence can only be glimpsed through the curved streets and small triangular squares in the city. For example, the S-Bahn route from Hackescher Markt to Jannowitzbrücke was built roughly along the former fortress walls, while the small triangular square at Hausvogteiplatz marks the site of one of the original bastions.

## Brandenburg Gate (Brandenburger Tor)
![Brandenburger Tor]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Brandenburgertor.jpg)
The Brandenburg Gate is a famous landmark in Berlin, Germany, having witnessed many significant historical events.

Originally built in 1735, it was merely a simple stone gate supported by two massive stone pillars. In 1788, Friedrich Wilhelm II ordered its reconstruction, which was completed in 1791, symbolizing the height of the Kingdom of Prussia. The Quadriga statue (a chariot drawn by four horses driven by the Goddess of Victory) atop the gate was seized by Napoleon in 1806 and returned to Berlin in 1814.

During World War II, the statue suffered severe damage and was recast after the war. Following the fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989, the Brandenburg Gate became a symbol of German unity and is now a major tourist attraction and site for celebrations.

## Notable churches in Berlin
### St. Nicholas Church (Nikolaikirche)
In Berlin's old city center, there are three notable churches: St. Nicholas Church (Nikolaikirche), St. Mary's Church (Marienkirche), and the ruins of the Franciscan Monastery Church (Franziskaner-Klosterkirche). The oldest among them is St. Nicholas Church, located next to Berlin's oldest market, Molkenmarkt.

The history of St. Nicholas Church dates back to around 1230, when it was built as a Romanesque church for local fishermen and merchants and named after St. Nicholas (the patron saint of sailors and travelers). As Berlin grew, the church underwent multiple reconstructions and expansions, eventually becoming a brick Gothic building with its characteristic twin towers.

However, during World War II, St. Nicholas Church was severely bombed and almost completely destroyed. It wasn't until the 1980s, in commemoration of Berlin's 750th anniversary, that the East German government decided to rebuild it.

Today, the reconstructed St. Nicholas Church serves as a museum, showcasing Berlin's religious history, urban development, and cultural heritage related to the church. The surrounding Nicholas Quarter (Nikolaiviertel) was also restored, becoming a historic tourist destination that allows visitors to experience the medieval atmosphere of Berlin.

### Franciscan Monastery Church Ruins (Franziskaner-Klosterkirche)
![Klosterkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Franziskaner-Klosterkirche.jpg)
Hidden in the heart of old Berlin, the ruins of the Franciscan Monastery are considered the last remaining traces of medieval monastic culture in Berlin. This all-brick structure marks the beginning of Brick Gothic (Backsteingotik) architecture in the region.

At the end of the 13th century, Franciscan monks established a monastery here. Following the Reformation and the dissolution of the monastery, the personal physician and alchemist to the Elector of Brandenburg founded Berlin's first printing workshop and scientific laboratory on the site, where he also kept exotic animals and grew medicinal plants. Later, in 1574, Berlin's first grammar school, the "Grey Monastery" (Grauen Kloster), was established here; its famous alumni include the later Prussian Chancellor Otto von Bismarck.

After numerous historical restorations and the scars of war, the Franciscan Monastery ruins were reconstructed in the 1980s for Berlin's 750th anniversary, with further renovations taking place between 2002 and 2005. Today, it serves as a venue for various cultural events.

### St. Mary's Church (Marienkirche)
![Marienkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Marienkirche.jpg)
St. Mary's Church in Berlin is the city's oldest church still used for religious services. Its exact construction date is unknown, but the earliest documentation dates back to 1292, suggesting the church likely existed before then. Originally built in the Gothic style, it was one of the few large stone buildings in Berlin at the time.

With the rise of the Reformation in Germany in the 16th century, Martin Luther's reformist ideas spread rapidly, leading St. Mary's Church to convert from Catholicism to Protestantism—a major turning point in Berlin's religious history. Since then, the church has played a vital role as a center for the Protestant community.

The church's most distinctive interior feature is the 15th-century fresco "Dance of Death" (Totentanz). This 22-meter-long mural depicts representatives of religious and secular classes appearing in pairs, led by a skeletal personification of Death, symbolizing the inescapable nature of mortality. The verses on the mural are written in Middle Low German, and the two-thirds of the surviving verses are well-preserved, regarded as some of Berlin's earliest existing literary works and showcasing late medieval European art.

After World War II, the damaged St. Mary's Church was restored to its original appearance by the East German government, followed by a comprehensive renovation in the 1970s to protect its historical architecture and artistic heritage. Today, it is not only a place of worship but also an open cultural space. Its long history and diverse architectural styles reflect Berlin's religious development and cultural diversity, making it a historical gem in the city center.

### Holy Spirit Chapel (Heilig-Geist-Kapelle)
The Holy Spirit Chapel is one of the oldest surviving buildings in Berlin, first mentioned in documents in 1313. The chapel is a single-story red brick structure, with its star-shaped vault completed around 1520 being its most prominent feature—a significant witness to the architectural and artistic history of medieval Berlin.

In 1906, the Holy Spirit Chapel was incorporated into the campus of the then-Berlin Business School, and later belonged to the Faculty of Economics at Humboldt University. The chapel, which survived World War II intact, underwent a full restoration in 2005 and was reopened on May 21 of the same year. Today, it serves as a ballroom for Humboldt University, used for various special events. Are there any students from Humboldt University who have visited it in person?

### St. Peter's Church (Petrikirche)
On the Spree Island (Spreeinsel), in the former district of old Cölln, once stood one of Berlin's oldest churches: St. Peter's Church. It first appeared in documents in 1237. Over the years, Petrikirche underwent several stylistic changes, evolving from Romanesque to Gothic, with Baroque interior decorations added later.

The church suffered severe damage during World War II and was eventually demolished in 1964. Incidentally, the old Cölln Town Hall (Cöllner Rathaus) was located next to St. Peter's Church, forming the core area of ancient Cölln. Today, these buildings no longer exist. A modern hotel (Capri Hotel) now stands on the site, featuring a glass floor that allows visitors to glimpse the remains of the old town hall—a place that bridges the past and the present.

### Berlin Cathedral (Berliner Dom)
![Dom]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Dom.jpg)
The church that definitely cannot be missed on Spree Island is the Berlin Cathedral.

As early as 1450, a Gothic chapel stood here as part of the Royal Palace of the Hohenzollerns. In 1747, Frederick the Great commissioned architect Johann Boumann to build a Baroque-style church with a dome. About 70 years later, Karl Friedrich Schinkel remodeled the interior and exterior in a Neoclassical style. After several small-scale renovations, Emperor Wilhelm II deemed the original church insufficiently grand; thus, in 1894, he decided to demolish the old church and build a new cathedral as a counterpart to St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, completed between 1894 and 1905.

During World War II, the Berlin Cathedral could not escape the devastation of war and was severely damaged. Restoration work did not begin until more than 30 years after the war; the exterior was completed in 1983, and the interior restoration was officially finished in 2002 with the unveiling of the final dome mosaic. Interested visitors should explore the cathedral's magnificent interior.

### Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church (Kaiser-Wilhelm-Gedächtniskirche)
![Gedächtniskirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Kaiser-Wilhelm-Gedächtniskirche.jpg)
The Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church is not located within the boundaries of old Berlin, but it holds special significance for Berlin and the world.

This church was built by Emperor Wilhelm II in memory of his grandfather, Wilhelm I, with the foundation stone laid in 1891 and completion in 1895. The designer was the famous Cologne-born architect Franz Schwechten, who drew inspiration from the Romanesque churches of his home Rhineland region to create this Neo-Romanesque structure. The church featured five bell towers, with the main tower reaching 113 meters, making it Berlin's highest landmark at the time. If you look closely, you will find many similarities to the Bonn Minster and the Church of St. Mary in Gelnhausen.

However, Allied bombing in November 1943 severely damaged the church. After the war, a proposal by architect Egon Eiermann for a new church building was selected, which included the complete demolition of the ruins. However, this plan met with strong opposition from Berlin citizens. Eventually, a compromise was reached: Eiermann built four modern buildings around the site, while the ruins of the old church were preserved as a memorial and warning against war.

## Airlift Square (Platz der Luftbrücke)
![Tempelhof_Luftbrückendenkmal]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Tempelhof_Luftbrückendenkmal.jpg)
After World War II, Berlin was divided into East and West. Since West Berlin did not share a border with other Western occupation zones, the Soviet Union's blockade of all rail and road traffic between West Berlin and the Western zones on June 24, 1948, immediately cut off supplies. Until May 12, 1949, raw materials such as food, medicine, and coal could only be transported into West Berlin by the Allies via aircraft. Some American planes became known as "Candy Bombers" or "Raisin Bombers" (Rosinenbomber) because they dropped sweets for children.

During the blockade, the Allies primarily used three air corridors to supply West Berlin: North to Hamburg, South to Frankfurt, and West to Hanover. Most Allied aircraft took off and landed at Tempelhof Airport, handling up to a thousand operations a day. Consequently, the Airlift Memorial features three vertical ribs symbolizing the three air corridors that connected West Germany to West Berlin. In this arduous operation, a total of 39 British, 33 American, and 5 German workers lost their lives, and a bronze strip on the memorial's base commemorates these victims.

## Tempelhof Airport (Flughafen Tempelhof)
![Flughafen Tempelhof]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Tempelhof.jpg)
Since we mentioned Tempelhof Airport, let's briefly discuss its history.

Located south of Berlin, Tempelhof was first mentioned in a document in 1351, though its history goes back much further. In the 12th century, the Knights Templar (Templerorden) established their headquarters here, engaging in land development and colonization. When the order was dissolved in 1312, its property was transferred to the Order of St. John (Johanniterorden), which eventually sold the land to the city of Berlin in 1435. From then on, it belonged to the Dukes of Brandenburg and later Prussia. Note: The Order of St. John still exists today; I may write about it in the future.

In the 19th century, Tempelhof transitioned into a major military site. From 1827, it served as a parade ground for the Berlin garrison, and a large number of military facilities, including hospitals, barracks, and prisons, were built in the late 19th century. One of the most controversial was the military detention center completed in 1896, later the notorious Columbia-Haus, which served as a Gestapo prison and concentration camp during the Nazi era.

However, during this period, with the support of the Prussian military, Tempelhof also became an important testing ground for hot-air balloons and airships. For instance, airships designed by Hermann Wölfer and David Schwarz, though their test flights ultimately failed, greatly inspired Ferdinand Graf von Zeppelin. His LZ-6 airship flew over Tempelhof in 1909, becoming a symbol of technology at the time.

Starting in 1936, the Nazi regime launched the construction of the airport at Tempelhof, commissioning architect Ernst Sagebiel to design what was intended to be the world's most modern airport—a symbol of aerial supremacy. However, with the outbreak of World War II, the airport was quickly requisitioned as a military industrial base for aircraft production, including the famous Ju-87 Stuka. Thousands of forced laborers and concentration camp prisoners were compelled to perform high-intensity labor here as part of the Nazi war machine.

After the war, Tempelhof became a US Air Force base and played a crucial role in the Berlin Airlift of 1948–1949. The airport resumed civil aviation in 1951, until flights ceased with the opening of Tegel Airport in 1975. Although it briefly reopened for small and business flights in 1985, it officially closed in 2008.

In 2011, the airport was honored as a historic landmark of German engineering and architecture and is now an open urban park. You can walk, play basketball, or fly kites here—a perfect spot for weekend relaxation.

## Berlin Thai Park
Friends in Berlin have likely heard of "Thai Park" located in Preußenpark. Its story reportedly began in 1992 when four German-Thai families had a picnic in the park. Through word of mouth, the group grew rapidly, and people from Thailand and neighboring countries began gathering regularly for picnics, forming a community. Everyone cooked their best dishes at home to share, gradually evolving into today's food market. For those who want to try authentic Southeast Asian cuisine, a weekend visit is a must—you're bound to find something you love! 🤤

Because Thai Park has long been held on park grounds, local residents have voiced opposition. In 2024, the local council decided to move the Thai market off the park grounds to the adjacent street (currently on Württembergische Straße). It is expected to officially relocate to Barstraße near Fehrbelliner Platz in 2026. Since it's all nearby, it won't be hard to find—just follow the aroma!

## Hohenschönhausen Memorial
![Hohenschönhausen]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Hohenschönhausen.jpg)
The site of the Hohenschönhausen Memorial was originally a large kitchen. After World War II, the Soviet secret service converted it into a detention center, and in 1951, it was handed over to the East German Ministry for State Security (Stasi). Subsequently, the Stasi built a new prison here, which served as the central remand center for political prisoners awaiting investigation between 1960 and 1989. After the fall of the Berlin Wall, former inmates campaigned for the site to become a memorial, which opened in 1994.

As in many such places, those imprisoned were often presumed guilty before being convicted. Interestingly, guides mention that the kitchen workers in the basement didn't know a prison was upstairs. I suspect some did know but couldn't speak out given the presence of Stasi secret police. Overall, the place feels very oppressive, especially during evening tours, serving as a reminder of the preciousness of freedom.

## Berlin Museum of Natural History (Museum für Naturkunde Berlin)
![Naturkunde]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Museum Naturkunde.jpg)
Berlin's "Long Night of Museums" originated in 1997. During this annual event, enthusiasts can use a single ticket to visit different museums and art exhibitions from 6 PM until midnight, enjoying the beauty of Berlin at night while soaking in culture.

I had long wanted to visit the Natural History Museum to see the dinosaur and Archaeopteryx fossils, and this was the perfect opportunity. Seeing the Giraffatitan in person was truly staggering—it felt like stepping into the Jurassic period! Furthermore, the museum houses various fossils, specimens, and a mineral collection covering nearly one-third of the museum area. It was a feast for the eyes! 🤤

In addition to the exhibits, there are many extra lectures that night. I was lucky enough to catch a talk about amber specimens containing leaves—it was a very rewarding evening!

## Humboldt Forum
![Naturkunde]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Berlin/BER_Humboldtforum.jpg)
The Humboldt Forum is one of Berlin's most iconic museums. It is named after the Humboldt brothers: the elder was a Prussian educator, while the younger is known as the father of modern geography. The name implies a fusion of natural and human sciences.

Historically, the site of the Humboldt Forum was the Berlin City Palace (Stadtschloss Berlin), which began construction in 1443 as a Renaissance-style palace for the House of Hohenzollern. After the Thirty Years' War, Elector Friedrich Wilhelm remodeled the palace into a Baroque style. The palace remained largely unchanged until World War II.

In the 1950s, the East German government demolished the remains of the palace, viewing it as a symbol of Prussian militarism. Twenty years later, the Palace of the Republic (Palast der Republik), the seat of the East German parliament, was built here. It wasn't until the 1990s, due to asbestos contamination, that the building's future was debated again.

Ultimately, the German federal government decided to rebuild the Baroque Berlin City Palace on the original site (now the Humboldt Forum). Construction began in 2013, and it officially opened at the end of 2020. Today, we can engage in dialogue between cutting-edge research and the public in the Humboldt Lab, and explore precious collections in the Ethnological Museum and the Museum of Asian Art. For those who want an overlook of Berlin, don't forget to visit the museum's roof!

## The Dark History of Berlin
### Führerbunker
What appears to be an ordinary parking lot today was once the site where World War II in Europe reached its end: the Führerbunker. Located beneath the gardens of the old Reich Chancellery, it was here that Adolf Hitler committed suicide on April 30, 1945, followed the next day by Joseph Goebbels. Although the Third Reich had not yet formally surrendered, the regime effectively ceased to exist at the moment its core power collapsed. 

Even though no traces remain on the surface, the bunker’s structure was so massive that repeated demolition attempts failed to destroy it completely. Its remains are still sealed deep underground today.

### Reich Chancellery (Reichskanzlei)
The area where Wilhelmstraße meets Voßstraße was once the site of the Reich Chancellery complex, which included the Old Chancellery and the New Chancellery.

The Old Chancellery: Originally a Baroque palace built in 1738-39 for Count Anton Ludwig von Schulenburg. Under Bismarck, the German government purchased the building at Wilhelmstraße 77 in 1875, where it served as his office and official residence.

The New Chancellery: Commissioned by Hitler and designed by chief architect Albert Speer to display the regime’s expanding ambition. Opened in early 1939 at Voßstraße 6, its design focused on intimidation. Through gargantuan proportions, it aimed to make guests feel small and instill a sense of awe and fear. The scale was staggering: a facade over 420 meters long, a Marble Gallery twice the length of the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles, and a Führer’s office spanning nearly 400 square meters with a 10-meter ceiling.

By the end of the war, the Old Chancellery was in ruins, while the New Chancellery suffered only minor damage. From 1949 onwards, the entire complex was demolished; today, part of the site is occupied by residential housing.

### Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe (Holocaust-Mahnmal)
Located near the Brandenburg Gate, this memorial was born from discussions starting in the late 1980s. Designed by New York architect Peter Eisenman, it was inaugurated on May 10, 2005. The design breaks away from traditional forms, consisting of 2,711 concrete slabs of varying heights arranged in a wave-like maze across 19,000 square meters. 

Visitors walking through the uneven ground and towering pillars often experience disorientation and unease. This abstract approach offers an individual space for reflection. 

Beneath the surface, the Information Centre documents the history of the Holocaust through diaries, letters, and biographies, restoring the victims' identities from cold statistics to human stories.

### Flak Towers (Flaktürme)
Between 1940 and 1942, three pairs of flak towers were built in Berlin (Tiergarten, Friedrichshain, and Humboldthain). Each pair consisted of a command tower (L-Turm) and a combat tower (G-Turm) equipped with heavy anti-aircraft guns.

The Humboldthain Flak Tower is the most iconic remaining example. Built in just six months by 3,200 workers—including forced laborers and POWs—it featured 2.5-meter-thick walls and a 3.8-meter-thick roof. It served as a bunker, field hospital, and storage for museum treasures. After the war, the Tiergarten tower was demolished, while the others were partially blasted into ruins. Today, thanks to the efforts of the Berliner Unterwelten e.V., the interior of the Humboldthain tower can be explored via guided tours.
