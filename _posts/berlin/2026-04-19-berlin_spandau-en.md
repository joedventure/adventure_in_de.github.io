## History of Berlin-Spandau
Spandau boasts a rich history that predates Berlin itself. Before its incorporation into Greater Berlin in 1920, it was an independent city with a strong sense of self-identity. To this day, many lifelong residents still prefer to identify as "Spandauers" rather than Berliners.

The origins of Spandau date back to the early 8th century, when Slavs established a settlement at the confluence of the Havel and Spree rivers. In 1157, Albert the Bear (Albrecht der Bär), the Margrave of Brandenburg, took control of the area and appointed a governor to ensure military security. By the late 12th century, merchants began settling around St. Nicholas Church, gradually forming a marketplace that was granted city rights in 1232.

Spandau has long played a vital military role. In 1559, Elector Joachim II ordered the construction of the Spandau Citadel to protect the neighboring city of Berlin. During the Thirty Years' War, Spandau became a garrison town, and by the late 18th century, military personnel accounted for half of the city's population.

Following the establishment of a rifle factory by King Frederick William I in 1722, Spandau evolved into a major center for the German Empire's arms industry, with gunpowder plants, artillery foundries, and ammunition factories being established. However, the strict building restrictions imposed on fortress cities severely hindered Spandau’s urban expansion. It was not until its fortress status was lifted in 1903 that private enterprises could move in, leading to the development of Siemensstadt.

Following Germany's defeat in World War I, the Treaty of Versailles prohibited arms production, dealing a devastating blow to Spandau’s industry and causing mass unemployment. Administratively, Spandau was incorporated into Greater Berlin in 1920, officially losing its municipal autonomy. Due to severe unemployment and structural economic issues, the Nazi Party expanded rapidly here. During World War II, munitions production resumed, and up to 40 forced labor camps were established in the district, leading to heavy bombing of the Old Town. On May 1, 1945, the commander of the Spandau Citadel surrendered to the Allies, saving the lives of many soldiers and civilians.

After the war, Spandau was assigned to the British occupation zone. Its economy shifted from armaments to consumer manufacturing, attracting many small and medium-sized private enterprises as well as essential public facilities like power plants and sewage treatment works. As a result, Spandau remains a premier industrial hub of Berlin today.

## The Behnitz Quarter of Spandau
Behnitz (also known as Kolk) is the oldest quarter within Spandau’s Old Town. It has been a site of human settlement since the Stone Age. In the 12th century, it served as a Slavic settlement tied to a nearby castle, where residents performed forced labor; this castle eventually evolved into the famous Spandau Citadel. The area was officially incorporated into the Spandau urban district in 1240.

Today, Behnitz retains its historical charm. Its narrow cobblestone streets and ancient timber-framed houses allow visitors to experience a distinct medieval atmosphere. In addition to the preserved remains of the old city walls, the quarter is home to the historic St. Mary’s Church (St. Marien am Behnitz), the second oldest Catholic church in the Greater Berlin area.

## St. Mary’s Church (St. Marien am Behnitz)
Beginning in the 18th century, the influx of Catholic workers and soldiers into Spandau created a growing need for a new place of worship. Consequently, St. Mary’s Church was designed by August Soller—a student of the renowned architect Karl Friedrich Schinkel. The foundation stone was laid in 1847, and the church was consecrated in 1848.

The church was twice requisitioned as a garrison church (Garnisonkirche): from 1910 to 1920 and again from 1936 to 1945. Although it suffered severe damage during World War II, it remained in use until 1952 before falling into disrepair. In a shocking turn of events during a 1964 renovation, precious interior frescoes were covered with plaster and the windows were bricked up. While the Archdiocese of Berlin purchased the church in 1995, they were unable to afford the high costs of restoration and decided to sell it. In late 2001, a couple from Neukölln purchased the building and undertook a thorough restoration using entirely private funds. They drained years of accumulated moisture from the walls, reopened the windows, and restored the magnificent interior decorations based on historical documents.

Today, the church interior is dominated by traditional Marian colors—blue, red, and gold—with marble-textured walls and a sanctuary niche painted with a beautiful starry sky and tapestry-like patterns. In 2003, the owners also commissioned Schuke, a traditional organ builder from Potsdam, to craft a brand-new organ specifically for the church.

St. Mary’s Church remains privately owned today, but visitors can appreciate its beauty during the masses held twice a week.

## St. Nicholas Church (St. Nikolai Kirche)
During the Middle Ages, Spandau was a vital trading hub at the confluence of the Spree and Havel rivers. Consequently, St. Nicholas Church in the Old Town was dedicated to Saint Nicholas, the patron saint of sailors and merchants. The church first appeared in records in the 13th century. The main structure seen today dates primarily from the 15th century—a late Gothic brick hall church featuring the oldest surviving Gothic church roof in the Brandenburg region.

Historically, St. Nicholas Church played a pivotal role in the Reformation. Elector Joachim II, who had been sympathetic to the movement since the 1520s, officially converted to Protestantism on November 1, 1539, making this church the center of the Brandenburg Reformation. A painting by the 19th-century artist Carl Röhling in the south aisle vividly depicts the scene of Joachim II receiving Communion. Furthermore, the church is located at Reformationsplatz (Reformation Square), with a statue of Joachim II standing at the main entrance to commemorate this historic milestone.

On the north side outside the church, visitors can find a monument designed by the famous architect Karl Friedrich Schinkel. It is the oldest monument in Spandau, erected to honor the citizens who fell during the Wars of Liberation (1813–1815).

## Spandau Citadel (Zitadelle Spandau)
The Spandau Citadel originated as an 11th-century Slavic fortification and evolved into a stone castle by the 12th century. With the advancement of firearms, the old castle lost its defensive efficacy. Between 1560 and 1594, Elector Joachim II commissioned Italian architects to transform it into a modern fortress. The symmetrical four-pointed bastion design eliminated defensive blind spots, ensuring that defenders could spot and engage enemies from any angle.

The citadel suffered severe damage during the Wars of Liberation against Napoleon. French troops occupied the castle, and during a Prussian bombardment, a fire spread to the gunpowder magazine, causing the Queen's Bastion (Bastion Königin) to explode. It was not fully rebuilt until the 19th century.

Starting in 1935, the Wehrmacht requisitioned the castle as a laboratory for the research and production of chemical weapons, including animal testing. Consequently, the interior structures of several buildings were significantly altered or damaged.

After WWII, British forces took over the site from the Soviets and returned it to German authorities in 1948. Since then, the citadel's military use has ended, and it has transitioned into an important historical and cultural center for Berlin.

The citadel is vast and full of highlights. The most famous is the Julius Tower (Juliusturm), one of the oldest surviving structures in Berlin, with a base dating back to the 13th century. It has served various purposes over the centuries, including as a residence, a defense tower, and a prison. After the Franco-Prussian War, it also housed the Imperial War Treasury to store French reparations.

The four bastions are equally renowned: King (König), Queen (Königin), Crown Prince (Kronprinz), and Brandenburg. Defensive tunnels remain preserved beneath the King and Queen bastions and can be visited via guided tours. The Crown Prince Bastion, once used to store highly toxic chemicals during the Nazi era, now serves as an art school and exhibition space. The Brandenburg Bastion is known as the "Italian Courtyard" because its vaulted basement resembles Venetian architecture; it once served as a prison and a Nazi-era gas laboratory, and today it is the citadel’s largest event venue.

Additionally, the Spandau City History Museum in the Armory (Zeughaus) and the Drill Hall (Exerzierhalle), which houses ancient cannons and military artifacts, are must-see attractions.

## Spandau Prison (Kriegsverbrechergefängnis Spandau)
There is a shopping mall in Spandau that looks like an ordinary supermarket at first glance, yet this site was once the location of the Spandau Prison.

Built in 1877 as a military prison, it became a site for incarcerating political opponents after the Nazi government took power in 1933. Following WWII, the Allies took control of the facility to house the seven major Nazi war criminals sentenced during the Nuremberg Trials, including Albert Speer, Karl Dönitz, and Rudolf Heß—Hitler’s steadfast deputy who was arrested after his secret flight to Scotland.

The prison's operation during the Cold War was unique, jointly managed by the UK, France, the US, and the Soviet Union. Jurisdiction rotated monthly, with the responsible nation raising its flag in front of the facility. This highly symbolic "Four-Power" administration led to extremely cumbersome daily operations. The massive prison was staffed by numerous soldiers, civilian guards from each nation, four governors and their deputies, and four doctors and translators. The changing of the guard ceremony even became a popular public spectacle. Within the harsh isolation, prisoners showed remarkable ingenuity, inventing a "knock alphabet" (Klopfalphabet) to communicate secretly. As the other six war criminals were gradually released, Heß became the sole inmate, held in solitary confinement within the heavily fortified building for over twenty years.

After Heß committed suicide in 1987, the Allies decided to completely demolish the prison to prevent the site from becoming a pilgrimage site for neo-Nazis. No monument was erected even for the victims of the Nazi era. To fill this gap in history, the Spandau Citadel recently hosted a special exhibition. Through historical photographs, unpublished documents, comic illustrations, and an interactive "knock alphabet" experience, the exhibition re-examined the prison's history from the German Empire to the post-war Allied administration. It explored the complex relationship between perpetrators and victims, forgetting and remembering, and sparked broad reflection on the social purpose of incarceration and how to preserve historical memory.
