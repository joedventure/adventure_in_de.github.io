## History of Michelstadt
Michelstadt is considered the cultural and economic heart of the Odenwald region. As early as 800 to 500 BC, the Celts had already settled here. In the 1st century AD, the Romans occupied the area, constructing military camps and the Upper Germanic-Rhaetian Limes to protect their territory. In the Vielbrunn district of Michelstadt, a reconstructed Roman watchtower remains visible today.

The name Michelstadt first appeared in 741 AD as "Michlinstat" in a donation document to Burkhard, the Bishop of Würzburg. After the bishop's death, the area likely reverted to the Frankish royal court and was granted by Charlemagne around 815 AD to his close confidant and biographer, Einhard. Today, we can still visit the Einhard Basilica (Steinbacher Basilika), which stands as one of the best-preserved Carolingian buildings north of the Alps.

Following Einhard's death, Lorsch Abbey became the lord of Michelstadt. With the decline of Lorsch Abbey, Michelstadt passed to the Archbishopric of Mainz. However, prolonged conflicts with the Electoral Palatinate led to the city's total destruction in 1307. Amidst these wars, the House of Erbach rose to power and began rebuilding the city gates and walls in 1390.

In the 19th century, all the city gates were demolished as they were viewed as obstacles to traffic. Had they been preserved, Michelstadt might be known today as the "Rothenburg of the Odenwald." Nevertheless, the city's ring walls and several towers still exist, surrounded by a well-preserved double moat structure.

Over time, Michelstadt was granted market rights, wall rights, and self-governing status, evolving into a middle-class city centered on handicrafts, commerce, and administration. Its historic architecture, particularly in the Old Town, continues to define the city's unique character and remains a must-visit destination in the Odenwald region.

## Michelstadt City Hall (Altes Rathaus)
![Michelstadt Rathaus]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Michelstadt/Michelstadt.jpg)
If there were a contest for Germany’s most beautiful city hall, the Michelstadt City Hall would undoubtedly be a top contender. With its pointed turrets, timber-framed beams, and exquisite municipal coat of arms, the building looks like something straight out of a fairy tale.

It was constructed in 1484 by an unknown architect. Upon closer inspection, you can find the year of construction marked in Gothic numerals on the pillars. For a long time, this town hall served as much more than a venue for council meetings; it functioned as a banquet hall, a Catholic church, a temporary hospital, school classrooms, a polling station, and even a wedding registry. It remained the seat of city administration until 1920, and the city council continued to meet here until 1973, with the ringing of the bells serving as their "start of work" signal 🔔.

Anyone who loves the classic German town style should not miss this iconic landmark of Michelstadt.

## Einhard Basilica (Einhardsbasilika)
Located just northwest of Michelstadt is the Einhard Basilica. This thousand-year-old church is named after its builder, Einhard, who was one of Charlemagne’s most important confidants and the author of the biography Vita Karoli Magni (Life of Charlemagne).

Between 815 and 827 AD, Einhard built what he described as a "magnificent church suitable for religious ceremonies." Although this three-aisled structure has undergone many changes throughout history, much of its core remains in the Carolingian style, making it an extremely rare and precious architectural heritage site.

After Einhard’s death in 840 AD, ownership of the church passed to Lorsch Abbey. However, with the progress of the Reformation, the Hesse region became Protestant territory, and the former abbey property was subsequently purchased by the rising House of Erbach.

Following this, the once-grand Einhard Basilica ceased to function as a religious site and was repurposed as a storage facility for hunting gear and timber. Because the roof had to be kept in perfect condition to ensure the timber remained dry, this change in use unexpectedly protected the historic structure, allowing us today to better understand the architectural art of the Early Middle Ages in Europe.
