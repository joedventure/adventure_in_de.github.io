## History of Idstein
![Idstein_Altstadt]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Idstein/Idstein_Altstadt.jpg)
The town of Idstein, located in the state of Hesse, is situated approximately 48 kilometers from Frankfurt and only 24 kilometers from Wiesbaden. Although it is a small town, it boasts a long and rich history. It was first recorded in 1102 under the name "Etichenstein" and was granted city and market rights by King Rudolf of the Habsburg dynasty in 1287, leading to its gradual prosperity.

From 1102 to 1721, Idstein served as one of the residences for the House of Nassau. It is worth noting that Count Adolf of Nassau was elected Holy Roman Emperor in 1292. Furthermore, the current Dutch Royal Family and the Grand Duchy of Luxembourg also descend from a branch of the House of Nassau—the House of Orange-Nassau.

In 1806, Napoleon incorporated Idstein into the Duchy of Nassau. Later, following the Austro-Prussian War in 1866, the region was annexed by Prussia.

From the late 18th century to the mid-20th century, Idstein became a major hub for the leather industry. Fortunately, the town escaped significant damage during World War II, allowing many well-preserved half-timbered buildings to remain today, maintaining the unique charm of this medieval town. The oldest house in town is said to have been built in 1410 and still stands to this day. If you have the chance to visit Frankfurt, consider taking a side trip to Idstein to escape the hustle and bustle of the big city.

## The Crooked House (Schiefes Haus)
![Haus]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Idstein/Idstein_Haus.jpg)
Idstein features many colorful half-timbered buildings, primarily concentrated around the lively König-Adolf-Platz. As you step into the alleys of the city center or look back from the Town Hall, you can take in the view of many famous old houses!

The most famous of these in the city center is undoubtedly the "Crooked House." This rare four-story timber-framed building was constructed in 1727 by Johann Jacob Nicolay, the commander of the local militia. From 1736 to 1745, the left side of the building served as a pharmacy, and it was later used for commercial and residential purposes. After comprehensive renovations between 1995 and 1997, this leaning house became part of the Idstein Town Hall. Its slanted appearance was caused by structural issues during its original construction, but it has now become one of the town's most popular attractions.

## Castle District and the Witches' Tower (Hexenturm)
![Idstein_Hexenturm]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Idstein/Idstein_Hexenturm.jpg)
Idstein Castle is perched on a rocky ridge within the Old Town. While the full history of the castle's construction is not entirely clear, the main keep, known as the Witches' Tower (Hexenturm), was built around 1170 and has since become the symbol of Idstein. The top of this 42-meter-tall tower offers the best vantage point to overlook the entire Idstein city center. Although it is quite a climb, the scenery is well worth the effort, and admission is free! 🤣

One section of the castle walls features the coat of arms of the House of Nassau, along with the title of one of the Counts of Nassau: BGZN—Balthasar Graf zu Nassau, who was the Count of Nassau-Wiesbaden-Idstein from 1566 to 1568. ## Union Church (Unionskirche)
The Union Church in Idstein is a member of the Evangelical Church in Germany. Records suggest that a church existed on this site as early as the 13th century, undergoing multiple renovations to reach its current form.

While its exterior resembles many other German churches, the interior walls and ceiling are decorated with 38 oil paintings. This is a particularly unique style for a Protestant church.

It is worth noting that the church was originally known simply as the Stadtkirche (City Church). It was renamed the Unionskirche in 1917 to commemorate the 100th anniversary of the Nassau Union (Nassauische Union), which established the united church of the Duchy of Nassau in 1817.
