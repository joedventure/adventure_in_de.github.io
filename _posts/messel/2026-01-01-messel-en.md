## History of Messel
Unlike other places previously introduced, Messel does not boast grand castles or exquisite palaces, yet it is, in my opinion, one of the most worthwhile places to visit.

Messel belonged to the Roman Empire between 50 BC and 260 AD, and it was first mentioned in historical records in 800 AD. At that time, the land was donated by its owner to the famous Lorsch Abbey. Following the dissolution of the abbey in 1232, all its properties were transferred to the Archbishopric of Mainz. However, long before this, the Counts of Katzenelnbogen seemed to have acquired certain privileges and, from the 14th century, granted Messel as a sub-fief to the von Groschlag family (Freiherren von Groschlag), who originated from neighboring Dieburg.

In 1799, the von Groschlag line became extinct, and Messel, along with nearby Dieburg, was transferred to the Albini family. Finally, in 1806, Messel was officially incorporated into the newly formed Grand Duchy of Hesse. As for Dieburg and the von Groschlag family, that is another story for another time.

In 1859, iron ore and oil shale (Ölschiefer) mining began around Messel. Unexpectedly, during the early stages of excavation, an incredibly well-preserved alligator fossil was discovered! This astounding find marked the beginning of Messel's journey as a paleontological treasure trove. As mining operations ceased, more precious fossils were unearthed, revealing the profound scientific and cultural potential hidden within this land.

Since 1975, the Senckenberg Nature Research Society in Frankfurt has been authorized to participate in official excavations at the Messel Pit, taking charge of long-term research and discovery. In 1976, paleontologist Jens Lorenz Franzen published an article emphasizing the immense paleontological significance of the Messel fossils.

However, in 1977, the local government planned to convert Messel into a landfill, a decision that sparked strong protests from the scientific community and the public. After a decade of litigation and social movements, the plan was finally canceled in 1988. In 1991, the State of Hesse acquired the mining area and entrusted its protection and scientific research to the Senckenberg Society. In 1995, it became Germany's first UNESCO World Natural Heritage site.

Since then, local institutions have continued to study and protect the site, making the Messel Pit not only a vital site for paleontology but also a symbol of the balance between natural resources and cultural heritage preservation.

## Messel Pit (Grube Messel)
![Messel_Grube]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Messel/Messel_Grube.jpg)
The Messel Pit contains Eocene fossils dating back 48 million years. At that time, Europe was closer to the equator and the world was much hotter than it is today. Due to constant tectonic plate movement, the Alps were slowly forming during this period, causing the Black Forest and Odenwald regions to rise, accompanied by significant volcanic activity. According to a 2001 scientific survey, the Messel area was once a volcanic center. Between 48.49 and 47.89 million years ago, underground magma met groundwater, triggering a massive explosion less than 100 meters below the surface, creating a crater over 700 meters deep.

After the volcanic activity ended, the bottom of this crater was filled with debris and volcanic ash, and eventually flooded by water, forming a natural lake. Due to the warm climate, minimal seasonal variation, and the depth of the lake, there was very little convection in the water. As a result, the bottom of the lake became oxygen-depleted and rich in sulfides. This environment provided ideal conditions for preserving the remains of plants and animals that sank to the bottom. Over millions of years, this mud continuously compacted and mixed with organic matter, eventually becoming the oil shale we see today.

The fossil finds at the Messel Pit are incredibly rich, including invertebrates and vertebrates alongside plants. Plant fossils alone account for over 75 families and more than 200 species, covering both micro and macro fossils. Among vertebrates, over 130 taxa have been identified, including more than 30 genera and 40 species of mammals. Furthermore, the preservation of fossils in the oil shale is exceptional; in vertebrates, stomach contents and soft tissues are often visible, while insects may retain wing venation or nearly original shell colors. These rare clues allow us a glimpse into the ancient world. The most famous discoveries at the Messel Pit are the early horse Eurohippus messelensis (providing evidence of the evolution from multi-toed to single-hoofed) and the primate Darwinius masillae (Ida, the most complete primate fossil ever found).

If you have the chance to visit the Messel Pit, joining a guided tour is the best choice. Along the way, guides explain the ancient climate and lead you step-by-step to the center of the pit, visiting various archaeological stations scattered throughout. Finally, you can experience the fun of finding fossils in oil shale yourself—how cool is that!

## Messel Museum (Fossilien- und Heimatmuseum Messel)
![Messel_Museum]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Messel/Messel_Museum.jpg)
After exploring the fossil pit, if you have time, do not miss the museum in the center of Messel town. Here, you can see more spectacular archaeological results and more graphic displays documenting Messel's journey from a mine to the archaeological heritage site it is today.
