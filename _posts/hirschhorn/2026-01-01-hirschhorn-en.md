## City History of Hirschhorn
Located at the southern edge of the Odenwald forest along the banks of the Neckar River, Hirschhorn is a destination well worth visiting.

The name Hirschhorn literally translates to "Stag's Horn," a fitting title for this tranquil town. In addition to its thousand-year-old historic center, the town offers a stunning overlook of the Neckar River's "meander neck" (Schleife).

The history of Hirschhorn actually began in Ersheim, located on the opposite bank of the Neckar. This settlement was first recorded in the Lorsch Codex in 773 AD, making it one of the oldest settlements along the river.

Around 1200, the Lords of Hirschhorn (Herren von Hirschhorn) chose the hillside across the river as their new stronghold and began constructing Hirschhorn Castle. Following the dissolution of the Lorsch Abbey in 1232, sovereignty over Ersheim and the surrounding lands passed to the Electorate of Mainz.

The mid-14th to early 15th centuries marked the most critical period in the town's development. Between 1336 and 1361, Lord Engelhard I von Hirschhorn achieved unparalleled financial success by acquiring key private estates and fiefs, significantly boosting the family's wealth. Although his son, Engelhard II, lost some property during wartime, his grandson, Hans V, not only successfully revived the family business but also further expanded their influence. It was during this period that the expansion of Hirschhorn Castle began.

In 1391, Hans V was granted the right by King Wenzel (reigned 1376–1400) to elevate the emerging settlement below the castle to the status of a city. Furthermore, as an advisor to King Ruprecht (reigned 1400–1410), he solidified ties with the neighboring House of the Palatinate. In 1413, he was appointed to the hereditary office of Seneschal of the Palatinate (Erbtruchsessenamt) by Elector Ludwig III. This position remained with the Hirschhorn family until the lineage ended in 1632. Due to the protection offered by the city walls, Hirschhorn attracted a large influx of residents, leading to the eventual abandonment of Ersheim and surrounding villages.

However, the Thirty Years' War changed everything. In 1632, Friedrich III, the last Lord of Hirschhorn, fled to Heilbronn due to the conflict and passed away in September. With his death, the Hirschhorn family exited the stage of history, and the town returned to the Electorate of Mainz. Over the next two centuries, control of the town shifted between Westphalia and Mainz until it was finally incorporated into the Grand Duchy of Hesse in 1806.

## Hirschhorn Castle (Schloss Hirschhorn)
![Hirschhorn View]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Hirschhorn/Hirschhorn_view.jpg)
Hirschhorn Castle is so vast that it is difficult to realize its core area actually accounts for only one-twelfth of the total size. The heart of the castle was built in the mid-13th century, consisting primarily of the main tower (keep), modest residential and economic buildings, and sturdy curtain walls. This was a transitional period between Romanesque and Gothic styles; thus, remnants of rounded Romanesque windows and later-added pointed Gothic windows can still be seen within the castle.

Like most castles of that era, Hirschhorn was a self-sufficient world. It housed not only the lords and their families but also gate and tower guards, hunters, stable hands, shepherds, maids, servants, castle administrators, and even a clergyman. To meet daily needs, the grounds included stables, barns, a brewhouse, a laundry, a bakery, a blacksmith shop, and specialized food storage buildings.

In the 16th century, the castle underwent major expansions, gradually taking on a palatial grandeur. Notable additions included terraces overlooking the Neckar, gardens, a garden house, and the "Hatzfeldbau," an exquisite Renaissance-style building.

With the decline of the Hirschhorn family and the town's frequent change of hands, the castle's fate reached a low point. Preliminary repairs began in the late 19th century to prevent further decay. Around 1950, the castle was converted into a hotel and underwent comprehensive historical restoration. This allowed the facade of the Hatzfeldbau to be faithfully recreated and the Renaissance architectural paintings inside to regain their former glory.

The castle is not only a symbol of Hirschhorn but also a significant landmark on the border between the modern states of Hesse and Baden-Württemberg.

## Hirschhorn Dam (Staustufe Hirschhorn)
![Hirschhorn_Staustufe]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Hirschhorn/Hirschhorn_Staustufe.jpg)
The dam facilities in Hirschhorn, completed in 1933, still stand strong today. From time to time, you can still see ships passing through the locks.

## Neckar River Meander (Neckarschleife)
![Hirschhorn_Neckarschleife]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Hirschhorn/Hirschhorn Schleife.jpg)
Another classic sight in Hirschhorn is the "Neckarschleife" (Neckar meander). There is a small hill near the apex of the curve that offers a panoramic view of the entire landscape, with views of Ersheim visible in the distance along the way.
