## History of Würzburg
![Würzburg Altstadt]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Würzburg/Würzburg Altstadt.jpg)
As the starting point of the Romantic Road, Würzburg is a city steeped in history. In ancient times, Celtic peoples had already settled in the area where Marienberg Fortress (Festung Marienberg) stands today. During the Migration Period in the 4th and 5th centuries AD, the Alemanni moved into the region. By the 6th and 7th centuries, the Franks began their eastward expansion, incorporating this land into the Merovingian dynasty.

Since the Franks had already converted to Christianity (specifically Catholicism) at that time, the Würzburg area was exposed to the faith quite early. Around 686, the Hiberno-Scottish missionary St. Kilian and his companions preached in the region, successfully converting the Frankish Duke Gosbert and many of his subjects. However, the process was not without conflict; Kilian and his companions Kolonat and Totnan eventually suffered martyrdom for their faith in 689.

Nevertheless, the earliest official record of Würzburg dates back to 704, mentioned by the Frankish-Thuringian Duke Hedan II in a deed for Bishop Willibrord. In 741, the Diocese of Würzburg was formally established under the influence of Saint Boniface, the Archbishop of Mainz. In 1030, Emperor Conrad II granted the Bishop of Würzburg the rights to mint coins, hold markets, and exercise jurisdiction over citizens, leading to the city's gradual prosperity. In 1168, at the Imperial Diet held in Würzburg, Emperor Frederick I (Barbarossa) bestowed the ducal title and secular power upon Bishop Herold. With this privilege, known as the "Golden Liberty" (Güldene Freiheit), the bishops of Würzburg could henceforth style themselves Prince-Bishops (Fürstbischof) and hold the title of Duke of Franconia (Herzog in Franken).

During the Thirty Years' War, Würzburg briefly fell to Swedish forces between 1631 and 1635. Despite this, the post-war era brought a golden age to the city. The bishops began large-scale construction of magnificent Baroque buildings, the most famous being the Würzburg Residence (Würzburger Residenz). It was designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1981 and remains a must-visit attraction in the city.

With the outbreak of the French Revolution and the Napoleonic Wars, Würzburg's fate fluctuated once more. In 1803, the city was secularized, ending its status as a Prince-Bishopric and joining the Electorate of Bavaria. Between 1806 and 1814, Würzburg briefly served as the capital of the Grand Duchy of Würzburg (Großherzogtum Würzburg) under Ferdinand III of Tuscany, before ultimately being permanently incorporated into the Kingdom of Bavaria.

## Marienberg Fortress (Festung Marienberg)
![Würzburg Altstadt]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Würzburg/Würzburg_Marienburg.jpg)
A visit to Würzburg is incomplete without exploring Marienberg Fortress. Records indicate that around 700 AD, the Frankish Duke Hedan II built a convent for his daughter, Immina. She likely served as the abbess until the Diocese of Würzburg was officially established around 742. From the 13th century onwards, the site was transformed into a fortress and served as the residence of the Prince-Bishops of Würzburg from 1253 to 1719.

## Würzburg Residence (Würzburger Residenz)
Until 1719, the Prince-Bishops of Würzburg resided at Marienberg Fortress. However, as their power grew, the original residence could no longer adequately reflect their prestige and wealth. Consequently, Bishop Johann Philipp Franz von Schönborn decided to build a new palace—the Würzburg Residence—on the site of the former Rennweger Schlösschen. Construction began in 1720, the main structure was completed in 1744, and the final touches were finished in 1780. In 1981, the Residence was officially added to the World Heritage list, becoming the second German landmark to be included after Aachen Cathedral.
The Würzburg Residence is not only grand in its exterior but also incredibly ornate inside, featuring exquisite Baroque and Rococo chapels and imperial halls that are absolutely worth seeing!

## Röntgen Memorial Site (Röntgen-Gedächtnisstätte)
I’m not sure if any of you have had the experience of getting an X-ray in Germany. I remember the first time I needed an examination here; the doctor simply said "Röntgen," which left me completely confused. I later realized that doctors in Germany refer to it as "Röntgen" (Röntgen rays) rather than "X-ray," which is what we are typically used to. 😅

Anyway, I’m sure many of you remember learning about Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen during your school years. In 1895, while serving as a professor in the Department of Physics at the University of Würzburg, Röntgen proved the existence of X-rays through a series of experiments. The most famous image is likely the very first X-ray he took of his wife's hand—legend has it she was absolutely stunned when she saw her own bones. In December of that same year, Röntgen published his paper titled "On a New Kind of Rays." This groundbreaking discovery led to him being awarded the first-ever Nobel Prize in Physics in 1901.

If you are interested in this piece of history, you can visit Röntgen’s former laboratory in Würzburg to see what those early scientific instruments actually looked like.

## Würzburg Wineries
Beyond exploring historical sites, a visit to Würzburg wouldn't be complete without experiencing the wines of the Franconia region. Among the many famous estates, Juliusspital Weingut Würzburg stands as the second-largest winery in Germany (the largest being in the state of Hesse). It was established in 1576 by the Prince-Bishop Julius Echter von Mespelbrunn. Since its founding, the site has functioned not only as a winery but also as a hospital and a center for elderly care. If you want to see where the magic happens, look back at the hillside vineyards from Würzburg Main Station—a portion of those vines belongs to Juliusspital.

While I am not yet a wine expert, I find it fascinating that Franconian wines are traditionally bottled in a flat, ellipsoidal flask called a "Bocksbeutel." It is said that this shape was originally used for ease of carrying and transport, and it now serves as a unique way to differentiate their products from wines of other regions.

## Würzburg Cuisine
In Würzburg, besides the local wines, you should also try the Würzburger Bratwurst mit Brötchen (Würzburg sausage with bread roll).

It is said that Würzburg sausages are flavored with wine, a concept similar to Taiwan's Kaoliang liquor sausages. However, in reality, this might not be the case—if anyone has tried them, feel free to share your experience! 😂

Another unique feature is the Fränkischer Kipf, a bread common in the Franconia region. Compared to the standard round Brötchen, its ends are more pointed, and it is sometimes seasoned with caraway seeds (Kümmel). If you are interested, it is definitely worth a try.

In fact, grilled sausage with bread is one of the most common street foods in Germany. Different regions feature different types of sausages, and I look forward to introducing more of them to you in the future! 🌭🌭

## The Starting Point of the Romantic Road
After World War II, Germany urgently needed to rebuild its international image and attract tourists from around the world. Thus, in 1950, the Romantic Road (Romantische Straße) was established. This route not only showcases Germany's rich heritage in history, art, culture, and architecture but has also become a dream journey for travelers.

The Romantic Road stretches over 400 kilometers, starting from Würzburg in northern Bavaria and extending all the way to Füssen in the south. The starting point, Würzburg, is renowned for its magnificent Residence palace. It is followed by towns full of medieval charm such as Rothenburg ob der Tauber, Nördlingen, and Dinkelsbühl. In Augsburg, visitors can feel the profound atmosphere of an ancient historical city. Meanwhile, the famous Rococo-style Pilgrimage Church of Wies (Wieskirche) leaves visitors in awe of its exquisite artistic achievement. Finally, the terminus at Füssen features Neuschwanstein Castle—a fairytale-like dream castle that leaves every visitor lingering with wonder.

For cycling enthusiasts, you can also choose to challenge the D-Route 9. This cycling path not only winds along the Romantic Road but also allows you to ride all the way north to Bremen. 🚲
