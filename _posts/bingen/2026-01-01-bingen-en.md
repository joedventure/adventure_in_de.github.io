## Historical Background of Bingen
***Origins of the Ancient Roman City***

Bingen (Bingen am Rhein) is located at the confluence of the Rhine and Nahe rivers in the state of Rhineland-Palatinate, Germany. Along with Rüdesheim, this town serves as the gateway to the Upper Middle Rhine Valley and has been an important settlement since prehistoric times.

The history of Bingen began in the Roman era. They arrived around the beginning of the Christian era and named the settlement Bingium, which was first recorded by the historian Tacitus in 107 AD. Upon their arrival, the Romans built a large fortress as a military base to guard the confluence of the two rivers and connected Bingen to Trier via the military road known as the Via Ausonia. The city walls of Bingen were constructed around 359 AD to protect residents from the eastward expansion of Germanic tribes. However, as history tells us, the Roman Empire eventually fell, and the Franks inherited the Roman legacy.

***The Era of the Prince-Archbishops in the Middle Ages***

Between 500 and 1000 AD, parts of Bingen belonged to various feudal lords. According to a document issued by Holy Roman Emperor Otto II in 983 AD (the "Veroneser Schenkung"), it was established that Bingen fell under the sphere of influence of the Archbishop of Mainz. As the power of the bishops stabilized, the city began to flourish. Landmarks such as the Basilica of St. Martin (Basilika St. Martin) and the Drusus Bridge (Drususbrücke) were built during this period. Klopp Castle (Burg Klopp) was first mentioned in documents in 1282, serving as the administrative center and military stronghold for the Archbishops of Mainz.

However, the Thirty Years' War and the subsequent War of the Palatine Succession caused severe damage to Bingen, particularly in 1689 when retreating French troops burned the city to the ground. In October 1792, French revolutionary forces attacked Bingen again, occupying the area from 1794 to 1814. After 1816, following the Congress of Vienna, Bingen no longer belonged to Mainz but was annexed to the Grand Duchy of Hesse.

***An Ancient City in the New Era***

The rise of railway and steamboat transport in the 19th century transformed Bingen into an important transportation hub and freight marshalling yard in western Germany. During World War II, Bingen became a key logistical node for transporting supplies to the Western Front. Starting in the autumn of 1944, as German forces prepared for the Ardennes Offensive (Ardennenoffensive), as many as 2,400 wagons passed through the area daily, making Bingen a primary target for Allied bombing raids.

By then, however, the German army was at its breaking point. In a final, futile attempt to stall the Allied advance, all of Bingen's bridges were blown up on March 16 and 17, 1945. World War II officially ended for Bingen on March 18, when American forces began their occupation of the city.

Today, Bingen strikes a perfect balance between history and modernity, preserving its rich heritage while thriving as a tourist destination. Annual events such as "Rhine in Flames" and the "Bingen Swingt" jazz festival showcase the renewed vitality of this historic city.

## Saint Hildegard (St. Hildegard)
Who exactly was Saint Hildegard? Born as Hildegard von Bingen in 1098 to a noble family in Niederhosenbach (some suggest Bermersheim), she was the youngest of ten children. She possessed extraordinary gifts from a young age, experiencing mystical "visions" as early as three to five years old. At the age of eight, her parents sent her to the Benedictine monastery at Disibodenberg to be raised by the nun Jutta von Sponheim, under whose guidance she studied Latin, scriptures, and liturgy. At fifteen, Hildegard took her monastic vows, and following Jutta's death in 1136, she was unanimously elected by her fellow nuns as the leader of the community.

The year 1141 marked a turning point in her life. She experienced a powerful vision and felt a divine calling to record everything she saw. Out of humility, she initially refused the call and subsequently fell ill. Ultimately, she submitted to the divine will and began writing her book of revelations. Her work received official recognition from Pope Eugene III at the Synod of Trier in 1148. From that moment, Saint Hildegard became the first officially recognized female prophet in Catholic history.

As her monastic community grew, she moved the convent to Rupertsberg in Bingen around 1150 to seek greater autonomy, and in 1165, she established a second daughter house in Eibingen.

Saint Hildegard’s creative output was incredibly vast, spanning theology, medicine (especially herbalism), natural history, and music. She maintained frequent correspondence with popes, monarchs, and powerful figures of the time, demonstrating her extraordinary influence in medieval Europe. Furthermore, she personally embarked on four public preaching tours across Germany—an exceptionally rare feat for a woman at the time.

Saint Hildegard passed away in 1179. While she was venerated as a saint by the faithful for centuries, she was officially canonized by Pope Benedict XVI in 2012, making her one of the few women to receive such an honor.

## Museum am Strom (Museum by the River)
For those who wish to learn more about Saint Hildegard, the Museum am Strom in Bingen is an absolute must-visit.

The museum is housed in a former power station completed in 1898. After a century, the decommissioned plant was reopened as a museum in 1998. It primarily showcases the history of Bingen, the Roman era and ancient medicine, Rhine Romanticism, and the life of Saint Hildegard.

Adjacent to the museum is the Saint Hildegard Garden. In addition to being beautifully maintained, the garden features many herbs from medieval Germany. Their properties are documented in Hildegard's work "Physica"—one might even call this book the German equivalent of the "Compendium of Materia Medica."
