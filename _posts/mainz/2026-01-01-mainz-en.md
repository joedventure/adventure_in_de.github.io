When people mention Mainz, many first think of the vibrant Carnival (Fastnacht) or the BioNTech headquarters, which gained global fame for developing the COVID-19 vaccine. However, this city on the left bank of the Rhine, the capital of Rhineland-Palatinate (Rheinland-Pfalz), holds over two thousand years of history waiting to be discovered.

## History of Mainz
### ***Origins of the City***

The Celts were the first to settle here, but most historical records begin with the Roman Empire. The founding of Mainz is generally attributed to the Roman general Drusus around 13 to 12 BC, who established a massive military camp capable of housing up to 12,000 soldiers. Shortly after, a settlement developed around the camp and was named Mogontiacum after the local Celtic deity Mogon, quickly becoming a key city of the Roman Empire in the region.

Although the remains from the Roman period are limited today, excavations in recent years allow us to reconstruct the appearance of Roman Mainz, including late Roman city gates, a sacred temple district, a Roman theater built around the 1st century AD, and Roman ships unearthed in the early 1980s.

Roman rule in this region ended in the mid-5th century AD with the eastward expansion of the Franks.

### ***Archbishops and Prince-Electors***

After Christianity (Christentum) was recognized within the Roman Empire in the early 4th century, the first Christian community appeared in Mainz, and it became an episcopal see. A key figure in the ecclesiastical and political development was the Missionary Archbishop (Missionserzbischof) Saint Boniface (Bonifatius). He served as the Archbishop of Mainz from 746 to 754 AD. After this, the title of Archbishop became firmly tied to Mainz.

The Archbishop of Mainz wielded unimaginable power at the time. He was both the archbishop and the ruler of the city, controlling vital rights such as markets, coinage, taxation, and land ownership, while overseeing ever-expanding territories. Unlike other episcopal cities, the Archbishop of Mainz successfully suppressed emerging civic groups. By around 900 AD, he had become the undisputed lord of the city, a role he maintained until the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire in 1800. Furthermore, in the Golden Bull of 1356, the Archbishop of Mainz was officially established as one of the seven Prince-Electors and served as the Arch-Chancellor of the Holy Roman Empire (Reichserzkanzler), playing a central role in the medieval ruling structure.

By the 18th century, Mainz was known as the "Central Hub of the Empire" (Centralort des Reiches), highlighting its status. To reflect this, the Archbishop of Mainz built the magnificent Electoral Palace (Kurfürstliches Schloss) on the banks of the Rhine as a residence and symbol of power. Many prominent figures served as Archbishops of Mainz, such as Johann Philipp von Schönborn, one of the most influential European politicians of his time.

### ***Social Change Under the Noble Court***

The city of Mainz benefited greatly from being the residence of the Archbishop-Elector. Strolling through the city, one cannot help but notice the many Baroque noble palaces, especially around Schillerplatz, which hosts grand residences such as the Erthaler Hof, Schönborner Hof, Bassenheimer Hof, and Osteiner Hof. For regional noble families, maintaining a presence in Mainz was crucial, as the Elector's court distributed many important offices.

Compared to the spacious and grand noble palaces, the residential areas of the merchant class consisted of narrow alleys and half-timbered houses. Despite being an important commercial city, the nobility and guilds were never able to challenge the Archbishop's rule, so large civic mansions are rarely seen in the city. Nonetheless, the city offered abundant employment opportunities, and trade flourished at various markets. In 1477, under the initiative of Archbishop Diether von Isenburg, the University of Mainz was established, greatly enhancing the city's status as a cultural center.

However, history was not always smooth. Between 1632 and 1635, Swedish troops occupied the city twice, and the ensuing plague took the lives of nearly half the inhabitants, severely impacting the city's development and population.

### ***Democracy Budding in a Fortress City***

After the French Revolution, the French captured Mainz in 1792. The following year, the Rhenish-German National Convention (Rheinisch-deutsche Nationalkonvent) was held at the Deutschordenshaus (now the seat of the Rhineland-Palatinate State Parliament), establishing the Republic of Mainz (Mainzer Republik). Although the republic lasted less than a year before ending under the siege of Prussian coalition forces, this convention remains a landmark event as the first democratically elected representative body in German history.

In the following years, power changed hands several times until the left bank of the Rhine was officially ceded to France in 1797. Until 1814, Mainz served as the capital of the French department of Mont Tonnerre (Département du Mont Tonnerre).

With the defeat of Napoleon, French rule in Germany ended, and Mainz became part of the German Confederation (Deutschen Bund), incorporated into the Grand Duchy of Hesse in 1816. As a Fortress of the German Confederation (Festung des Deutschen Bundes), Mainz hosted a large permanent garrison, with soldiers once accounting for 20% of the population. For the next century, life in Mainz was deeply influenced by the fortress system. Civil authorities had to obey the Fortress Government (Festungsgouvernement), resulting in the police and security apparatus being controlled by Prussian and Austrian troops. Due to its status as a fortress, industrialization and population growth were slow, and the city only began to expand after the city walls were dismantled at the end of the 19th century.

From the 1830s onward, economic pressure, political frustration, and resentment toward long-term military suppression led to growing tension between the government and citizens. The middle-class reform movement spread to Mainz in the 1840s; democrats met at the Frankfurter Hof (completed in 1841), eventually leading to an uprising in 1848, which was quickly suppressed by the military.

The Carnival as we know it today also began during this period. The traditional pre-Lenten celebrations (Fastnacht or Karneval) were reorganized by the middle class. In 1838, the first Rose Monday (Rosenmontag) parade was held in the city. Shortly after, the first Carnival associations were founded (there are now over fifty). At that time, Carnival was much more political than it is today, with the speaker's podium serving as a stage for powerful citizens to present themselves and criticize contemporary politics.

### ***A Vibrant Modern City***

After Germany became a republic in 1918, Mainz was incorporated into the newly formed People's State of Hesse (Volksstaat Hessen). Due to deep-rooted Catholic traditions and the influence of the labor movement, the Nazis initially found it difficult to gain a foothold. However, as in other parts of Germany, they quickly took control after 1933. This was a disaster for the city. The thousand-year-old Jewish community was completely eradicated. Massive Allied air raids during WWII also leveled Mainz, with 80% of the city center destroyed.

After WWII, Mainz was placed in the French occupation zone. Although the population generally felt the oppression of foreign troops, the French laid key foundations for the city's future during this period. In 1946, the state of Rhineland-Palatinate was established, with Mainz designated as its capital. That same year, the University of Mainz was reopened under French initiative.

Reconstruction in Mainz progressed slowly at first, but by the 1960s, the city began to develop rapidly. The 2000th-anniversary celebration of the city's founding in 1962 provided even more momentum. Today, Mainz has a population of about 200,000 and is the most populous city in Rhineland-Palatinate. With over 30,000 students, the University of Mainz consolidates the city's position as an academic center. Many well-known companies have also established headquarters here, such as ZDF (Zweites Deutsches Fernsehen), the optical glass manufacturer Schott, and BioNTech.

Of course, the famous Carnival cannot be missed. The representative Rose Monday (Rosenmontag) parade attracts over 500,000 people to the streets every year to share in the festive fever. Additionally, the Johannisnacht (Johannisfest) in summer and the Wine Market (Weinmarkt) in autumn each have their own unique characteristics, making this city vibrant all year round.

## Mainz Cathedral (Dom St. Martin)
![Mainzer Dom]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_Dom.jpg)
As early as the Roman period, Mainz (then known as Moguntiacum) was already a seat for bishops. The original cathedral was likely located to the west of the current structure, where St. Johannis Church stands today. Around 900 AD, Archbishop Hatto rebuilt the original cathedral, and parts of that structure can still be seen within St. Johannis today.

During the tenure of Archbishop Willigis, the Archdiocese of Mainz became a highly influential religious and political center within the Holy Roman Empire. Having close ties to the Ottonian royal family, Willigis decided to build a more magnificent new cathedral to manifest his lofty status in both the Empire and the Church. Departing from traditional designs, the high altar of this new cathedral was placed on the west side, emulating the configuration of the papal altar in St. Peter's Basilica in Rome. After construction began in 975 AD, the old cathedral gradually lost its significance.

At the end of the 18th century, the fires of the French Revolution reached the Rhine valley. Mainz became a strategic point contested by Prussia and France, leading to severe damage to the cathedral. From 1798, French forces used the damaged church as a granary and even considered demolishing it. Precious interior decorations were auctioned off, leaving almost nothing behind except the altar and some monuments. It was not until 1802 that the new bishop, Joseph Ludwig Colmar, successfully reclaimed the church and began restoration. On August 15, 1804, the cathedral was reconsecrated. However, in 1813, following the French retreat after the Battle of Leipzig, the cathedral was temporarily converted into a field hospital, and a large amount of wooden equipment was stripped for firewood. Mass was not held again until November 1814.

Mainz Cathedral underwent large-scale renovations between 1856 and 1879. From 1909 to 1928, new concrete foundations were added to prevent collapse. During World War II, the cathedral was heavily damaged and was not fully restored until 1960.

As a vital religious and political core of the Holy Roman Empire, Mainz Cathedral was the site of many major historical events. Several emperors held their coronations here, including Henry II (1002), Conrad II (1024), and Frederick II (1212). It also hosted the Imperial Diet (Reichstag) numerous times; for instance, in 1188, Emperor Frederick I Barbarossa announced his participation in the Third Crusade here.

In the modern era, the cathedral continues to play a significant role. In 1848, the first German Catholic Convention (Katholikentag) was held here, with anniversaries celebrated in 1948 and 1998. In November 1980, during his visit to Germany, Pope John Paul II visited Mainz Cathedral to engage with representatives of the German Evangelical Church and the Jewish community, initiating a profound interfaith dialogue.

Naturally, Mainz Cathedral is also closely linked to the origins of the Reformation.

## The Reformation and Mainz
Martin Luther, indulgences, the Ninety-five Theses, and the Reformation are historical events familiar to many. The fuse for these events can be traced back to Albert of Mainz (Albrecht von Brandenburg).

Albert came from the House of Hohenzollern and was born in 1490. As the son of Johann "Cicero," Elector of Brandenburg, he enjoyed immense political resources and ecclesiastical connections from a young age. At 23, he simultaneously became the Archbishop of Magdeburg and the Apostolic Administrator of Halberstadt. The following year (1514), he was elected Archbishop of Mainz, attaining the status of Prince-Elector and serving as the Archchancellor (Erzkanzler) of the Holy Roman Empire. At the time, his election was partly motivated by the hope of using the power of the Elector of Brandenburg to protect Erfurt—a city under the Mainz diocese—from the aggressive expansion of the Electorate of Saxony.

However, his rapid ascent was illegal. According to canon law at the time, one could not be a bishop before the age of 30, let alone without a corresponding academic degree. Holding multiple bishoprics was also prohibited. To legitimize this, he obtained a study dispensation in 1513 and borrowed money from the Fugger family—the wealthiest family in Europe at the time—to pay the confirmation fees to the Roman Curia.

This financial transaction drew the ire of the Archbishop of Salzburg, who protested Albert's pluralism. Consequently, Pope Leo X demanded even higher confirmation fees. On the advice of his counselors, Albert reached an agreement with the Pope to sell indulgences in German territories. Half of the proceeds would go toward the reconstruction of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, while the other half would be used to repay his bank debts.

In 1517, Albert authorized the priest Johann Tetzel to promote indulgences throughout Germany, claiming that purchasing them could remit sins and shorten time in Purgatory. This commercialized promotion sparked widespread resentment and enraged Martin Luther. On October 31, 1517, Luther posted his Ninety-five Theses, denouncing the theological absurdity and abuse of power regarding indulgences, and sent a copy to Albert, pleading with him to stop the trade. Unexpectedly, Albert did not respond to Luther but instead forwarded the theses to Rome. The Papacy subsequently launched a heresy investigation, a reaction that directly triggered the outbreak of the Reformation and transformed Luther from a scholar into a reformer challenging church authority.

Despite being at the eye of the storm, Albert initially took a "wait-and-see" approach toward the Protestant movement. In 1520, he hired Luther's friend Wolfgang Capito as a cathedral preacher, followed by Kaspar Hedio the next year. Both were moderates who advocated for church reform centered on the Bible, and their sermons gained wide support among Mainz University students and the clergy.

However, as the reformers' influence grew, Albert's stance hardened between 1523 and 1524. He decided to oppose the Reformation entirely, expelling Wolfgang Capito and Kaspar Hedio from the city and siding with the Emperor and the Pope to suppress the reform movement.

## St. Augustine's Church (Augustinerkirche)
![Augustinerkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_Augustinerkirche.jpg)
Strolling along Augustinerstraße, the most quaint street in Mainz, one arrives at a Baroque-style church. This St. Augustine's Church was originally a Gothic building, first constructed around 1260 when the Augustinian Order established a foothold in the city. Construction of the new church began in 1768 and was completed three years later. The entrance features a facade made of red sandstone, with a statue of the Virgin Mary at the center, flanked by St. Augustine and his mother, St. Monica.

Upon entering, the magnificent decorations are sure to inspire awe, especially the ceiling frescoes painted by Johann Baptist Enderle. The theme of these frescoes revolves around the faith and mission of St. Augustine. The center depicts the Virgin Mary enthroned with the Christ Child, while St. Augustine offers his burning heart, symbolizing love and faith. Latin inscriptions around the scene mark his achievements in various roles:

-The east side presents him as the "Doctor of the Church" (DOCTOR ECCLESIAE), studying and writing in his study.

-The west side depicts him drafting the monastic rule, with patron Hugo Franz Karl Graf von Eltz among the listeners, symbolizing the "Patriarch of Religious Orders" (PATRIARCHA RELIGIOSORUM).

-The south side portrays him debating heretics, demonstrating the power of the "Hammer of Heretics" (MALLEUS HAERETICORUM).

-The north side shows his spirit of pursuing truth in a serene posture, echoing the title "Disciple of Truth" (DISCIPULUS VERITATIS).

The monastery was abolished due to secularization in 1803 and later became a seminary. Fortunately, it suffered only minor damage during World War II. After the war, it was the only church in Mainz that could hold Mass immediately after temporarily boarding up its windows. The original decorations remain largely intact to this day, and the ceiling frescoes have been fully restored.

## Roman Theatre (Römisches Theater)
![Römisches Theater]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_Römisches Theater.jpg)
Just like Roman baths, theatres played a vital role in the daily life of Romans. At the Mainz Roman Theatre station, there once stood the largest Roman stage theatre north of the Alps at the time. Measuring 116 meters wide with a 42-meter-long stage, it could accommodate 10,000 spectators simultaneously—a scale that remains impressive even by modern standards.

The construction of the theatre dates back to the early 1st century AD, likely originally used for ceremonies honoring General Drusus. At that time, representatives from sixty districts in Gaul would gather annually in Mogontiacum, the capital of the province of Germania Superior, to commemorate their ties with Rome. This annual memorial event granted the city the status of a political pilgrimage site.

However, entering the Middle Ages, the theatre's stones were gradually hauled away to be used as materials for new buildings. During the construction of the railway in 1884, Roman walls were rediscovered but were quickly demolished to lay tracks. It wasn't until the early 20th century, when roadworks revealed the ruins again, that people began to realize the importance of this discovery. Nevertheless, the outbreak of the two World Wars forced the suspension of planned full excavations, and the theatre once again faded into oblivion.

Systematic excavations finally began in 1997, with many people participating by hand. In addition to archaeologists and students, many volunteers were involved. Today, when visiting the Roman Theatre station, travelers can clearly see the remaining walls and partially restored seating behind the glass partitions of the platform. If you observe closely, you will notice that the platform surface is divided into gray and light-colored sections; the light-colored stones mark the presumed location of the ancient stage wall, the height of which likely aligned with the highest point of the original spectator stands.

## Mainz Citadel (Zitadelle Jakobsberg)
![Zitadelle Jakobsberg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_Zitadelle Jakobsberg.jpg)
The Jakobsberg, where the citadel stands today, housed only a monastery during the Middle Ages and was not included within the city walls. From 1329 onwards, Jakobsberg had only simple defensive earthworks. This left a strategic gap, as the city walls turned at this point, allowing attackers to use the hill to invade Mainz or shell the city. Between 1620 and 1629, an irregular pentagonal fortification temporarily closed this gap, connecting the hill to the city's defense system.

Around 1655, Prince-Elector Johann Philipp von Schönborn ordered the encirclement of all of Mainz with bastions according to French fortification styles. During this project, the fortress on Jakobsberg was converted into a regular quadrilateral, which is the form we see today. Notably, the original monastery and the Drususstein (Drusus Stone), erected by the Romans to honor Drusus, were kept intact and incorporated into the fortifications.

In 1696, a commander’s building (today’s Building A) was constructed above the gate facing the city. Following the victory over France in 1816, Mainz became a fortress of the German Confederation. Prussian and Austrian troops moved into the citadel, using it as barracks. To this end, the Austrians built the bomb-proof Citadel Barracks (today’s Building C) in 1861, with smaller buildings serving as a clubhouse and kitchen (today’s Building D). During the city's expansion at the end of the 19th century, the citadel was preserved, and an additional double-regiment barracks (today’s Building E) was built in 1914. For this purpose, the remains of the original monastery were demolished. During World War I, the citadel was used as a prisoner-of-war camp.

With the entry into force of the Treaty of Versailles and the dismantling of fortifications inside and outside Mainz, the military history of the Mainz Citadel came to an end. However, during World War II, it was again used as a POW camp, mainly housing French officers and some British officers. Toward the end of the war, Mainz citizens took refuge in the underground tunnels converted into air-raid shelters. After the war, French occupation forces requisitioned the site until 1955 and constructed some new buildings, though they had no military significance. Today, the citadel belongs to the City of Mainz and houses numerous municipal offices.

## Gutenberg Monument and Museum (Gutenberg Denkmal, Museum)
![Gutenberg Denkmal]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_Gutenberg.jpg)
As many know, Johannes Gensfleisch zur Laden zum Gutenberg was the first person in the Western world to invent movable type printing. A native of Mainz, his invention enabled the mass production of books, fueling the Reformation, Humanism, and the Scientific Revolution.

On the 400th anniversary of the advent of movable type printing, a 3.45-meter-tall statue of Gutenberg was unveiled in 1837. Holding the medieval bestseller, the Bible, it was one of the early public statues dedicated to a common hero. During World War II, the statue was buried in the ground next to its pedestal to prevent destruction. In 2008, it was removed for restoration due to damage and returned to the square two years later.

Every summer, Mainz hosts the four-day Johannisnacht (St. John's Night) festival. The book market surrounding the statue, the traditional "Gautschen" (a baptismal ritual for printers where apprentices are splashed or dunked in water by masters or colleagues after completing training), and the ancient game "Preisquadräteln" all pay tribute to Gutenberg. In autumn, the Science Market (Wissenschaftsmarkt) invites everyone to explore science firsthand. During the Carnival season, parade participants even place a jester's hat on the statue. Regardless of the season, this world-famous inventor continues to enjoy the festivities in Mainz, remaining a central part of the city's culture and life.

To explore movable type printing in depth, you can visit the Gutenberg Museum, which exhibits the Gutenberg Bible, movable type printing machines, and various tools. It is an experience that will surely leave you enriched.

## Osteiner Hof
![Osteiner Hof]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_Osteiner Hof.jpg)
The Osteiner Hof was built between 1747 and 1752 by Prince-Elector Johann Friedrich Karl von Ostein and is one of the aristocratic mansions of Mainz. In the 19th century, the mansion served as the residence of the military governor (Das Gouvernement). To this day, the term "Das Gouvernement" is still used by many locals. In 1914, the news of the outbreak of World War I was announced to the public from the Osteiner Hof by General Hugo von Kathen.

During World War II, the Osteiner Hof was completely destroyed by fire but was rebuilt between 1947 and 1948 under the impetus of the French occupation authorities. It served as the site command for the German Federal Armed Forces (Bundeswehr) until 2014. Today, it has been transformed into a mixed-use residential and commercial building. There are many high-end apartments available for booking inside; it looks very luxurious, and one might wonder if any social media friends have stayed there to share their experiences!

According to tradition, the Carnival season is officially inaugurated on November 11th at 11:11 AM from the balcony of the Osteiner Hof. The celebration lasts for several months, reaching its climax with the Rose Monday (Rosenmontag) parade the following year. On that day, various giant floats appear on the streets, using exaggerated humor to satirize political and social issues. Parade participants constantly toss candy, and both adults and children excitedly run to collect it—just be careful not to get hit! The scene is lively and joyful, much like traditional festive parades, and is well worth experiencing at least once.

## Carnival Fountain (Fastnachtbrunnen)
![Fastnachtbrunnen]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_Fastnachtbrunnen.jpg)
Mainz's crazy "Fifth Season" (die fünfte Jahreszeit) is famous far and wide, yet for a long time, the city lacked a sculpture that truly echoed the spirit of Carnival. It wasn't until late autumn in 1963 that city officials proposed an idea to a small group of entrepreneurs to establish a Carnival fountain at the southern end of Schillerplatz. They hoped to add beauty to the city and showcase the unique joie de vivre of the people of Mainz. Sponsored generously by the famous beverage company Eckes, the design by Munich professor Blasius Spreng and local architect Helmut Gräf was ultimately chosen. After three years of effort, the fountain named the "Tower of Fools" (Narrenturm) was unveiled on January 14, 1967, instantly becoming a new landmark of the city.

In the center of the fountain is a basin made of red sandstone, from which rises a copper tower nearly nine meters high, crowded with over 200 distinctive copper statues. These figures are mysterious, funny, grotesque, and familiar all at once, including Father Rhine, Till Eulenspiegel, the city goddess Mogontia, monks, money launderers, rigid rule-followers, and a man with a wooden board on his head. They not only showcase the diversity of Carnival but also perfectly convey the humorous spirit of the city of Mainz.

Every year on November 11th, when Carnival opens, the balcony of the Osteiner Hof opposite grandly announces, "The Carnival has begun!" Although relatively young, the Carnival Fountain (Fastnachtbrunnen) at Schillerplatz has become an indispensable part of the Mainz cityscape.

## St. Stephen's Church (St. Stephan)
![St. Stephan]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_St. Stephan.jpg)
The history of St. Stephen's Church dates back to 990 AD, founded by Willigis, the Archbishop of Mainz and Archchancellor of the Holy Roman Empire. The Gothic structure we see today was constructed between 1290 and 1335, built upon the foundations of an earlier Pre-Romanesque church from the Ottonian period.

In 1857, the church suffered severe damage due to the explosion of the nearby Powder Tower (Lustheim). It was heavily struck again during World War II, with a massive crack appearing in the tower, which was not repaired until 1947. In 1962, during the celebration of the 2,000th anniversary of the founding of Mainz, the church's new dome and lantern tower were completed. Notably, the main tower once housed a unique residence; from 1559 until 1911, tower guards were stationed there to watch for fires and protect the city skyline.

However, what truly made St. Stephen's world-famous are the blue stained-glass windows created by the artist Marc Chagall. When sunlight filters through these windows, the entire space is bathed in a mystical blue light, with angels and biblical figures appearing to float within, offering a truly transcendent experience. Statistics show that over 200,000 tourists visit annually, many specifically to experience this unique atmosphere.

## Sanctuary of Isis and Mater Magna (Isis- und Mater Magna Heiligtum)
As a significant city of the Roman Empire, it is perhaps not so surprising to encounter ancient Roman ruins within a modern shopping mall.

During the early construction phases of the Römerpassage shopping center, archaeologists unexpectedly discovered the remains of a sanctuary dedicated to the Egyptian goddess Isis and the Great Mother goddess Mater Magna. During the Roman period, this site was located right next to the main road connecting the legionary camp to the bridge over the Rhine.

According to recovered inscriptions, the sanctuary dates back to the 1st century AD. Archaeological excavations between 1999 and 2001 unearthed a wealth of rare artifacts, including votive inscriptions, various offerings, fire altars, clay and bronze sacrificial figurines, and curse tablets (voodoo dolls). Additionally, a female burial from the Hallstatt period (approx. 8th to 6th century BC) was discovered.

Originally, it was assumed that only a residential area for merchants and craftsmen would be found here; instead, these precious finds revealed the reality of daily Roman religious practices. Thanks to the professional efforts of archaeologists and the support of the Initiative Römisches Mainz and local citizens, this long-buried history was finally brought back to light for the world to see.

## Leichhof Fountain (Leichhofbrunnen)
![Leichhofbrunnen]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mainz/Mainz_Leichhofbrunnen.jpg)
The Leichhof Fountain is located in the Leichhof square in the heart of Mainz. Created in 1980 by the German sculptor Heinz Müller-Olm, this modern fountain commemorates five important figures in the history of Mainz:

1. A Roman legionary holding a model of the Jupiter Column (Jupitersäule) and a temple, marking the city's Roman origins.

2. A bishop holding a model of a church, honoring Archbishop Willigis, who founded the cathedral in 975.

3. A noble citizen holding a large key, with the Wheel of Mainz (Mainzer Rad) at his feet.

4. An architect holding blueprints and a house model, commemorating the urban development under Prince-Elector Johann Philipp von Schönborn.

5. A man holding a model of city walls, representing the civic guard (Landwehr). Beside him is a stack of books and a building facade, commemorating the founding of the University of Mainz in 1477.

## History of Mainz-Kastel
Mainz-Kastel is one of the few settlements that still retains its original founding name. Between approximately 12 and 9 BC, General Drusus established the Roman Castellum Mattiacorum (Fortress of the Mattiaci; no archaeological remains exist today) on the right bank of the Rhine in the territory of the Mattiaci. This included a temporary pontoon bridge across the Rhine and an archway. From this point on, Mainz and the bridgehead on the right bank held immense strategic importance, allowing for the monitoring of the river and crossing points, as well as providing waterborne access to territories not yet occupied.

During the reign of Emperor Vespasian (71 AD), the wooden pontoon bridge connecting Mainz to the right bank was replaced by a permanent arch bridge supported by stone pillars, which likely remained in use until the 4th century. Simultaneously, the fortress was reinforced with stone walls during Emperor Domitian's Chattan Wars (83–88 AD) and garrisoned by two Roman legions. Subsequently, a military civilian settlement (vicus) rapidly formed around the fort, flourishing during the peaceful period of the 2nd century into a wealthy town, as evidenced by the remains of large thermal baths. However, the Romans appeared to have dismantled the fortifications of Kastel by 122 AD, as the Upper Germanic Limes had been completed, providing sufficient security for the town.

With the internal and external crises facing the Roman Empire in the 3rd century, the Romans gradually abandoned the borders on the right bank of the Rhine and redefined the Rhine as the imperial frontier, making Mainz a border city once again. Nevertheless, the bridgehead on the right bank remained under Roman control. To counter the threat of the Alamanni and secure the Rhine defense line, Kastel was fortified again and surrounded by stone walls.

According to a hoard of treasure unearthed in 1962 dating back to the early 5th century, these gold and silver coins along with high-quality military equipment likely belonged to a Roman unit and its officers. These funds were buried due to dire circumstances and never retrieved. This suggests that Roman control here may have lasted until the end of the 5th century, after which the fate of Kastel remains unaccounted for.

After the Roman withdrawal, it was not until a document from 757 AD that a settlement named "Kastel" was mentioned again. During the reign of Charlemagne, Kastel belonged to the royal estates, and a new bridge was constructed between 803 and 813 AD to connect the banks of the Main, but it was destroyed by fire shortly after completion. It would be another thousand years before a permanent bridge was built here again.

The coat of arms of Kastel features a scallop shell, dating back to the 12th century. Since 1127, the local Brotherhood of Saint James was established to provide hospitality to pilgrims traveling to Santiago de Compostela.

From the second half of the 10th century, Kastel gradually came under the influence of the Archdiocese of Mainz. The village was walled and was regarded as an Imperial City in the 13th century. However, the power that took root here did not stem from the Archbishopric of Mainz, but from the Lords of Hohenfels of the House of Bolanden.

In the spring of 1242, Archbishop Siegfried III von Eppstein of Mainz launched a siege on Kastel. The conflict arose because Philipp I von Hohenfels (1220–1277), who was loyal to the Staufer dynasty, had established a customs station here. This severely impacted commercial traffic on the Rhine and Main rivers, particularly blocking trade routes from Mainz to Frankfurt.

The Archbishop of Mainz first led his army across the river and set up siege engines. Upon hearing this, the citizens of Worms, also loyal to the Staufer dynasty, sent armored warships to assist. The Archbishop was forced to retreat and burned his siege engines to prevent them from falling into enemy hands. As night fell, the crisis seemed temporarily averted. However, through deception, the Archbishop bribed the gatekeeper of Kastel and captured the town under the cover of darkness and thick fog. It was not until the following autumn that King Conrad IV personally led an expedition to retake Kastel after a short siege, promptly reinstalling the customs station.

In 1244, Archbishop Siegfried promised the citizens of Mainz that he would destroy Kastel and guaranteed that no further fortifications would be permitted within a one-mile radius of the town. At the end of that year, he captured Kastel again and, according to historical records, completely destroyed the city. However, specific details regarding the extent of the destruction were not recorded.

At the beginning of the 14th century, shipping and rafting industries in Kastel began to flourish, and internal affairs became relatively stable. When French troops occupied the Rhine region in 1689, Kastel was burned down; it remained under the Archdiocese of Mainz until the fall of the Electorate. In 1792/93, French troops occupied and fortified Kastel once again. Following the Reichsdeputationshauptschluss of 1803, Kastel was assigned to the Principality of Nassau-Usingen until it was handed over to France in 1806.

After the French withdrawal, Kastel, along with Mainz, was designated as a fortress of the German Confederation in 1816. In 1840, the Taunus Railway (Taunusbahn), the second railway in Germany, opened between Wiesbaden and Kastel, later extending to Frankfurt. In 1908, Kastel was incorporated into Mainz.

During World War II (1939–1945), Kastel was almost entirely destroyed. During the post-war occupation, US General Eisenhower declared the Rhine as the boundary between American and French forces. This led to the right-bank suburbs of Mainz being separated; Kastel, Amöneburg, and Kostheim were placed under the jurisdiction of Wiesbaden, where they remain today.
