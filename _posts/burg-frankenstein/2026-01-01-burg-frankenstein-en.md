The Odenwald is located at the crossroads of Hesse, Bavaria, and Baden-Württemberg. Compared to the world-famous Black Forest, it might lack a few fairytale-like fantasy castles, but for enthusiasts of medieval fortresses and ruins, this is undoubtedly a destination that cannot be missed.

According to local folklore, the Odenwald is the very place where Siegfried was betrayed and met his tragic end in the "Nibelungenlied" (The Ring of the Nibelung). For those seeking the "hardcore" side of German fairytales, the Odenwald might just be the premier choice.

## Frankenstein Castle
![Burg Frankenstein]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Burg Frankenstein/Burg_Frankenstein.jpg)
Among all the castles in Hesse, Frankenstein Castle (Burg Frankenstein) is arguably the most legendary. Located south of Darmstadt, it stands as the northernmost castle of the Odenwald forest. Records first mention the castle in 1252, and its construction is attributed to Konrad Reiz von Breuberg and his wife, Elisabeth von Weiterstadt. Here, they established their own territory and founded the House of Frankenstein.

Around 1400, the castle was expanded to the north, which sparked disputes between the Frankenstein family and the House of Hesse-Darmstadt over religious beliefs and territorial sovereignty. Ultimately, in 1662, the Frankenstein family sold the castle and its lands to Landgrave Ludwig VI. They used the proceeds to purchase another estate in Ullstadt, where their descendants still reside today. Visitors can still see the earliest parts of the castle, such as the main tower and the original palace. These structures reveal a cramped original layout, illustrating how medieval castle architecture prioritized defense over comfort.

The fame of Burg Frankenstein is largely owed to the novel "Frankenstein" by English author Mary Shelley. In this classic tale, Dr. Victor Frankenstein creates an artificial lifeform that ultimately becomes a symbol of horror. After World War II, American soldiers stationed in and around Darmstadt seemed particularly fascinated by the castle. An AFN (American Forces Network) live broadcast once described the castle as "the real home of the monster," and in 1976, the first Halloween festival was held at Burg Frankenstein. Until major renovation work began in 2023, the castle became a pilgrimage site for horror fans every autumn.

However, historians now consider the claim that Mary Shelley visited the castle during her 1814 trip along the Rhine and drew inspiration for her novel to be pure fiction. Nevertheless, this has not diminished the castle's romantic charm. The Late Gothic castle chapel, restored in 1850, remains a popular venue for weddings today.
