## History of Wiesbaden
I must admit, for a long time I thought the capital of Hesse was Frankfurt. It wasn't until I had the chance to explore the region that I discovered the capital of Hesse is actually Wiesbaden 😅

### ***From Roman Thermal Outpost to Medieval Power Struggles***

Wiesbaden was first mentioned in historical documents in 122 BC as Aquae Mattiacorum, named after the abundant local thermal springs and the Mattiaci, a Germanic tribe that settled there.

At the end of the 3rd century, the Alamanni invaded the area, destroying most Roman settlements. To defend against foreign enemies, the Romans built a defensive wall in Wiesbaden, known today as the Heathen's Wall (Heidenmauer), which is still visible. In the late Western Roman Empire, some Alamanni even allied with Rome, becoming tribes that assisted in defending the borders.

With the decline of the Western Roman Empire in the 6th century, the Franks gradually displaced the Alamanni and occupied the area, establishing long-term rule. During the Merovingian dynasty, Wiesbaden was the site of a royal estate known as Wisabada, meaning "meadow bath."

Entering the Middle Ages, Wiesbaden's history moved into a new phase dominated by the struggle between the nobility and royal power. In 1170, the House of Nassau gained control over the Wiesbaden region, leading the city's development for centuries. In 1232, Wiesbaden was elevated to an Imperial City, gaining higher political status. Unfortunately, this prosperity did not last. The elevation of Wiesbaden posed a great threat to the rival Archbishop of Mainz. Consequently, the city was attacked by Mainz in 1242 and destroyed. It never fully recovered from this blow, becoming an obscure small town and ending its era as an "Imperial City" (imperatoris civitas).

### ***Governance by the House of Nassau and the Establishment of Modern Administration***

In 1277–78, due to the consistent loyalty of the House of Nassau to the crown, King Rudolf I granted Wiesbaden to Adolf (who later also became King) to counter the influence of Mainz. He bestowed a series of privileges upon Wiesbaden, including coinage rights, silver mining rights, and jurisdiction over the city (including its assets, the mint, and customs). Simultaneously, the city's administrative structure was established. Around 1280, the city government consisted of a mayor appointed by the ruler and seven jurors. By the 15th century, this changed to two mayors and a citizens' assembly acting as a consultative body. In the 18th century, the administration became more sophisticated, with 22 permanent positions responsible for public order and daily administration, such as night watchmen, field wardens, and gate guards.

However, Wiesbaden faced a series of setbacks in the early 16th century. In 1525, the Peasants' War affected the area; citizens rose against municipal authorities, clergy, and rulers, demanding the abolition of forced timber requisitioning and seeking access to water, pastures, and woodlands. After the rebellion was suppressed, Count Philip, the ruler, severely punished the rebels, stripping the city of many privileges and restricting its freedoms. Despite attempts by citizens to restore their original rights over the following decades, they saw little success. Furthermore, two major fires in 1547 and 1561 destroyed almost all medieval buildings. The subsequent outbreak of the Thirty Years' War further deepened the city's destruction.

Wiesbaden finally saw a turning point in 1684 when Prince Georg August of Nassau-Idstein succeeded to the throne. He actively promoted tax incentives to revitalize local commerce and handicrafts, encouraging more people to settle in Wiesbaden. In addition to economic policies, he redesigned urban construction and built a summer palace on the banks of the Rhine in nearby Biebrich, adding a courtly atmosphere and cultural status to the city.

Although increasing taxes sparked dissatisfaction among some citizens, overall, the results of these reforms were significant. Wiesbaden's population doubled within a few decades, gradually emerging from its medieval stagnation to become the capital of the Principality of Nassau-Usingen and later the Duchy of Nassau (1806–1866).

### ***The Glory of an International Spa Town, War, and Modern Transformation***

Moving into the 19th century, Wiesbaden continued to prosper and became an internationally renowned spa city. Even after Prussia annexed the Duchy of Nassau in 1866, Wiesbaden's development did not stall; instead, it moved further toward modernization. As the capital of an administrative district under Prussian rule, Wiesbaden attracted a large number of wealthy rentiers, retired military officers, and high-ranking officials. The cityscape, composed of upscale villas and luxury hotels, reflected the prosperity of the time.

World War I, the subsequent occupation by France and Britain until 1930 (Rheinlandbesetzung), and the Great Depression heavily impacted Wiesbaden's finances and urban development. Even as the city government merged surrounding suburbs to expand the city scale and stimulate the economy, the decline could not be reversed. The glory of this formerly world-famous spa town faded.

After the Nazi regime took power in 1933, Wiesbaden was quickly integrated into its administrative network. Various party and government agencies were established, and campaigns of deportation and persecution against Jews and Sinti were launched. Citizens who opposed the regime, regardless of their political stance, were also suppressed and persecuted.

After the war, Wiesbaden became the capital of the state of Hesse. In addition to government offices, many publishing houses, insurance companies, and film industries chose to settle here. Not only did the city regain its vitality, but as a famous thermal spa destination, Wiesbaden also became one of the most livable cities in the Rhine-Main region.

## The Roman District of Wiesbaden
### ***The Temple of Sirona (Sironatempel)***

Traces of the ancient Roman city can still be found in several places in downtown Wiesbaden. For instance, on Schützenhofstraße (the street where Aldi is located), which is home to the Schützenhofquelle spring, a votive stone dedicated to the Celtic healing goddess Sirona was unearthed, suggesting that a temple dedicated to her may have once stood here. Considering that Sirona was highly revered across the European regions of the Roman Empire, this area was likely the heart of Wiesbaden during the Roman period, much as it is today.

Additionally, a sundial was discovered here, with a design very similar to those found in Pompeii. It was likely placed in front of the public baths near the spring, serving as an ancient clock to remind people of their bathing times.

In short, it’s possible that the place where we buy groceries at Aldi today is the exact same spot where Romans went to the market two thousand years ago!

The Frankfurter Rundschau reported in 2023 on a mural of the goddess Sirona painted on the large staircase of Schützenhofstraße. Interested visitors can also see an information plaque in front of Schützenhofstraße 2.

### ***Thermal Springs***

Wiesbaden is most famous for its thermal springs. Since Roman times, it has been a renowned spa destination. Key springs include the Kochbrunnen, Adlerquelle, and Schützenhofquelle. In addition, there are about 20 other shallow thermal sources. The primary component of the spring water is sodium chloride (table salt).

There are three main theories regarding the origin of the high-salinity hot water in Wiesbaden's springs. The first suggests the salt originates from salt mines at the border of Hesse and Thuringia, with hot water flowing through underground fissures northeast toward the Taunus Mountains. The second theory, based on plate tectonics, states that Wiesbaden sits on a rift zone that is still expanding, allowing hot water to rise from 2,000 meters deep, dissolving rock salt along the way. The third view suggests that even without salt mines or rifts, the rock layers here allow high-temperature circulation of salt-rich hydrothermal systems. Each theory has its merits and they may complement each other to explain the mystery of Wiesbaden’s geothermal springs.

### ***Kaiser-Friedrich-Therme***

When mentioning springs, one must highlight the century-old, artistic Kaiser-Friedrich-Therme in the city center. Built between 1910 and 1913, it is a perfect representation of blending ancient Roman bath inspiration with Art Nouveau (Jugendstil) aesthetics. Upon entering, you are immediately greeted by exquisite wall reliefs, frescoes, and tiles filled with geometric elements throughout the various areas.

The spring water here is approximately 65°C, sourced from the nearby Adlerquelle, and has been a favorite spa destination for Wiesbaden citizens since olden times. Besides bathing, you can try the hot air rooms (Heißlufträume) and the stone steam bath (Steindampfbad) for total relaxation!

### ***The Roman Fort on the Heidenberg (Römerkastell auf dem Heidenberg)***

The Heidenberg heights are located between present-day Kastellstraße and Platter Straße. From the street name Kastell, one can infer that this area was once the site of Roman military facilities. Indeed, during the construction of the new municipal hospital in 1904–1905, multiple trenches were discovered, confirming that four different forts from various periods had been established here, dating from around 85 AD to the early 2nd century AD.

It is estimated that the fort covered about 2.2 hectares, featuring typical rounded corners. Each side had four defensive towers and two gates, with the main gate located on the eastern side. Inside the fort, the western side housed several stone administrative buildings, including the central headquarters and its shrine of the standards (Fahnenheiligtum). The northern and eastern sides contained various workshops and soldier barracks.

### ***The Heathen's Wall (Heidenmauer)***
![Heidenmauer]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Heidenmauer.jpg)
The Heathen's Wall is the only remaining Roman architectural ruin in Wiesbaden today. Research confirms the wall was built in the 4th century AD, originally measuring about 520 meters long, starting from the intersection of Schulberg and Hirschgraben and extending to the Market Church (Marktkirche). Unfortunately, the parts we can see today are limited to a semi-circular tower on Schulberg and two sections of the wall totaling about 80 meters.

The purpose of this wall remains a subject of debate. Some scholars believe it may have been part of a military defense system established by the Roman Emperor Valentinian around 375 AD to strengthen border defenses. Other scholars suggest the wall might be the remains of an ancient Roman aqueduct (Aquädukt), though this theory also lacks strong supporting evidence.

## Marktkirche (Market Church)
![Marktkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Marktkirche.jpg)
Completed in 1862, the Wiesbaden Marktkirche replaced the original Mauritius Church (Mauritiuskirche), which burned down on this site in 1850. Records suggest that the Mauritiuskirche was built during the Carolingian period around 780–790 AD, making it a millennium-old monument at the time of its destruction.

To rebuild the spiritual heart of Wiesbaden, Carl Boos, the architectural advisor to the Duchy of Nassau, was commissioned to design a new church primarily using red brick. As red brick architecture was uncommon in the Hesse region, his design faced significant criticism. Fortunately, due to the affordability, aesthetics, and durability of brick at the time, his design was eventually approved.

The Marktkirche features a Neo-Gothic style with five towers, the central spire reaching a height of 98 meters. From a distance, the church's vertical lines create the impression of a massive wall rising suddenly from the flat ground.

The interior is equally impressive. Exquisite statues of Jesus and the Evangelists stand before the altar, while the vaulted ceiling is adorned with painted star patterns. The church's organ is also one of Germany’s most representative large Romantic-style organs; if you have the chance, be sure to attend a concert here to experience it firsthand.

## Ringkirche (Ring Church)
![Ringkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Ringkirche.jpg)
In 1860, there were approximately 12,900 Protestant believers in Wiesbaden. As the city developed rapidly, this number surged to 42,900 by 1890. Consequently, the city government decided to build a new Protestant church at the western end of Rheinstraße. Initially planned as the Reformation Church (Reformationskirche), it was eventually renamed the Ringkirche because of its location on the edge of the city's ring road. For several years after its completion, the church stood out prominently as there were no residential buildings surrounding it.

The architect of the Ringkirche was Johannes Otzen, a prominent church architect active in Hamburg and Berlin who also designed the Bergkirche in Wiesbaden. In the design of the Ringkirche, he firmly implemented his concept of a centralized congregational church based on the "Wiesbaden Program" (Wiesbadener Programm) proposed by Pastor Emil Veesenmeyer.

This program advocated for a church space centered around preaching, explicitly rejecting traditional long Gothic layouts and the separation of the altar and choir. Instead, it emphasized a design philosophy of unity between the congregation and the clergy, centralizing the altar, pulpit, organ, and choir to present the core of faith through an integrated spatial design.

## Hessian State Theatre Wiesbaden (Hessisches Staatstheater Wiesbaden)
![Theater]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Theater.jpg)
The construction of the Wiesbaden State Theatre began after the Duchy of Nassau was annexed by Prussia and received strong support from Kaiser Wilhelm II. On October 16, 1894, the theatre opened grandly in the presence of the Emperor, becoming a masterpiece of German architectural art of that era. It blends Renaissance and Baroque styles, serving as a model for German theatre architecture.

Stepping into the theatre, you are immediately greeted by a magnificent foyer. Marble columns support the space, golden chandeliers shimmer under soft light, and exquisite carvings are visible all around. The ceiling frescoes are a highlight, featuring Muses and mythological figures symbolizing artistic inspiration, while cleverly integrating local Wiesbaden elements such as the famous thermal springs and the winding Rhine River, tightly weaving art, history, and the city together.

I once had the opportunity to watch Richard Wagner's full "Ring Cycle" here. Four consecutive nights, with each performance lasting 3 to 5 hours, was quite a challenge. However, securing a middle seat in the upper tier provided incredible acoustics—it was a truly exhilarating experience!

## Kurhaus Wiesbaden
![Kurhaus]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Kurhaus.jpg)
The word "Kur" refers to a type of treatment or "cure," originally meaning a method of recuperation based on natural therapies in specialized health facilities to achieve physical and mental relaxation. Since Europeans generally believed in the healing effects of thermal springs, Kurhauses can be found in many spa towns. Originally a building for visitors to these spa resorts, the Kurhaus has evolved over time into a multi-functional event center or cultural facility.

The construction of the Wiesbaden Kurhaus was primarily driven by the large number of tourists visiting the hot springs and the activities of the casino. The old Kurhaus was completed in 1810. To meet growing demand, Friedrich von Thiersch—famous for designing the Palace of Justice in Munich and the Reichstag in Berlin—took on the task of designing a new Kurhaus in 1902. In 1907, the new building was completed on the same site in a similar style and inaugurated in the presence of Kaiser Wilhelm II. The new facility boasts opulent interiors, such as the Wine Salon made of yellow marble; the Shell Hall decorated with frescoes, shells, and pebbles; the Red Pavilion in Louis XVI style; the Bacchus Hall; and the Corner Hall with embossed leather and gilded ceilings. The grand concert hall is decorated with marble columns and mahogany panels, with the entire ceiling adorned in blue and gold lattice carvings—it is truly magnificent.

However, most of these decorations were destroyed in an air raid at the end of World War II. After the war, the Wiesbaden city government restored the building, and a comprehensive renovation and modernization project began in 1983. Based on surviving original plans and old photographs, the building was restored to its 1907 appearance, showcasing the charm of Wilhelmine architecture while meeting modern functional requirements.

Today, the Kurhaus serves as a versatile venue. Perhaps its most famous feature is the Casino Wiesbaden. It is one of the oldest casinos in Germany, having hosted many royals, musicians, and writers looking to try their luck. The Russian author Fyodor Dostoevsky lost his entire fortune here in 1865 and vividly depicted this experience in his novel "The Gambler." Even today, the Russian roulette wheel that Dostoevsky once stood by in high agitation is still on display for visitors.

## Cuckoo Clock
![Clock]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Clock.jpg)
Walking down Burgstraße, one landmark you cannot miss is the one claimed to be the world's largest cuckoo clock. Although this title has since been taken over by the cuckoo clock in Schonachbach, this one in Wiesbaden was the first and only one to officially hold that title back in 1953! Furthermore, it is a movable cuckoo clock mounted on a wall, unlike many other large cuckoo clocks that are integrated into the structure of a house. Rumor has it that its location has indeed been moved before.

As for the origin of this cuckoo clock, it was commissioned by Emil Kronenberger, a souvenir merchant. However, he was more than just a shopkeeper. Before WWII, he was a member of the Jewish community in Wiesbaden, a co-founder of a French underground organization, and a veteran honored by General de Gaulle. After being forced to flee Germany in 1939, he returned after the war and invested his state compensation funds into the construction of this cuckoo clock.

How did it move to its current location? According to a 1954 U.S. military report, the idea came from his nephew: "The Americans will love this!" Consequently, the cuckoo clock was stealthily installed outside the souvenir shop within a single day, allegedly bypassing strict government building codes—a truly fascinating story.

## Unity Man (Einheitsmännchen)
![Einheitsmännchen]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Einheitsmännchen.jpg)
In 2015, to celebrate the 25th anniversary of German reunification, the State of Hesse organized a project titled "Overcoming Borders" (Grenzen überwinden), inviting artist Ottmar Hörl to create a series of sculptural art installations.

For this project, Ottmar Hörl reimagined the famous "Ampelmännchen" (traffic light man) originally designed by Karl Peglau in 1961. He transformed the classic 2D icon into a 38cm tall, 3D monochromatic "Unity Man" (Einheitsmännchen). He gave the figure a friendly smile and an outstretched hand, symbolizing courage, momentum, and hope for the future.

Starting in April 2015, approximately 1,000 of these Unity Man statues embarked on a nationwide tour as cultural ambassadors. They started in Wiesbaden and traveled through Berlin, Hofgeismar, Schwerin, and Stuttgart, finally arriving in Frankfurt for the grand finale of the 25th-anniversary celebrations, sharing their message with people across the country.

While it's hard to find the exact date when the 3.5-meter-tall Unity Man in downtown Wiesbaden was placed there, it is certain that he appeared in front of St. Paul's Church in Frankfurt during the 2015 reunification celebrations.

## Neroberg
![Neroberg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Neroberg.jpg)
If you want a panoramic view of Wiesbaden, you cannot miss Neroberg, located in the northern part of the city. On this hill, approximately 245 meters above sea level, sits Wiesbaden's only vineyard, managed by the Hessian State Wineries of Kloster Eberbach. Additionally, you can find the Nerobergtempel, a small Renaissance-style viewing pavilion that has offered visitors a Mediterranean flair since it was built 170 years ago.

There's plenty to do in the evening as well. Every summer, the "Erlebnismulde" (experience hollow) on the hill becomes the venue for "Impro-Sommer" (Improv Summer) performances on weekends, making it a perfect spot for families and friends to gather.

## Opelbad
If you need to cool off, you can head to the Opelbad, located not far from Neroberg. This is no ordinary swimming pool; it is known as one of the most beautiful scenic pools in Germany! Swimming while overlooking the beautiful cityscape of Wiesbaden elevates the entire experience.

As you might guess from the name, this open-air pool has a connection to the famous German car brand, Opel. After car tycoon Wilhelm von Opel moved to Wiesbaden, he donated 100,000 Reichsmarks to the city and provided additional loans to build an open-air pool on Neroberg. The Opelbad officially opened in 1934 and has since become one of Wiesbaden's landmarks.

## Nerobergbahn
![Nerobergbahn]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Nerobergbahn.jpg)
The Neroberg is not particularly high, and walking up doesn't actually take much time. However, if you'd rather not break a sweat and are looking for a unique experience, then you must try the Nerobergbahn.

Since its opening in 1888, the Nerobergbahn has held its place as one of Wiesbaden's most popular attractions. Stretching 438.5 meters with an ascent of 83 meters and a staggering average gradient of 19.5%, this funicular—the oldest water-ballast-driven cable car in Germany—is a true technological breakthrough!

## Russian Orthodox Church of Saint Elizabeth (Russisch-orthodoxe Kirche der heiligen Elisabeth)
![Russian Church]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Russian_Church.jpg)
On the way to Neroberg, you can spot an Orthodox church with golden onion domes from afar. As you approach step by step along the forest paths, the view gradually clears, and the hidden church finally reveals its full splendor. This magnificent St. Elizabeth's Church is another landmark of Wiesbaden, built between 1846 and 1855. Behind its ornate exterior and interior lies a heartbreaking story.

Elizabeth, Duchess of Nassau (Elisabeth Herzogin zu Nassau), born Elizabeth Mikhailovna Romanova, was the niece of Russian Tsars Alexander I and Nicholas I. She first met her future husband, Duke Adolph of Nassau, in the summer of 1840. It was likely love at first sight, as they immediately considered marriage. Three years later, they met again in Baden-Baden, and after a brief visit by Adolph to Tsar Nicholas I to obtain his consent, the marriage contract was signed in Moscow in December 1843. The contract included a clause allowing Elizabeth to retain her Orthodox faith after moving to the Duchy of Nassau and to have a private chapel in her residence. According to imperial regulations, her dowry amounted to one million rubles, with 4% annual interest at her free disposal for life. The wedding took place on January 31, 1844, in St. Petersburg. Elizabeth was 17 at the time, and Adolph was 26. After the wedding, the couple stayed in Russia until late February before traveling to Nassau via Weimar. On March 26, 1844, all of Wiesbaden held a grand celebration to welcome the newlyweds.

As Duchess of Nassau, Elizabeth dedicated herself to charitable work and founded the Elisabeth-Heilanstalt (Elizabeth Sanatorium). However, less than a year after the wedding, she tragically passed away during childbirth due to tuberculosis; her newborn daughter also did not survive. With the consent of Tsar Nicholas I, Duke Adolph used funds from her dowry to build this church as a funerary memorial for Elizabeth.

The project was led by architect Philipp Hoffmann. He was deeply dedicated, traveling to St. Petersburg and Moscow to study contemporary and Renaissance Russian church architecture, and visiting Italy twice to research interior decorations. He meticulously planned every detail of this Russian Orthodox church. After seven years of construction, the church was consecrated on May 25, 1855. Under the watchful eyes of Wiesbaden residents, the remains of the Duchess and her daughter were moved from St. Bonifatius Church to the newly built sanctuary.

Built of light-colored sandstone, the church features five gilded onion domes in a typical Russian style. The interior is decorated with marble, with a three-story iconostasis directly opposite the entrance. In the annex on the north side, a cenotaph made of white Carrara marble depicts the Duchess in a reclining pose, surrounded by statues of the twelve apostles and four female figures at the corners symbolizing Faith, Hope, Love, and Eternal Life.

Since 1844, this site has been a center for the Russian Orthodox community and Russian visitors. It wasn't until the 1920s, following the Russian Revolution and the Bolshevik rise to power, that a stable local community was established by Russian exiles. Since 1936, the church has belonged to the Diocese of the Russian Orthodox Church in Berlin and Germany. A comprehensive interior restoration took place in the 1990s, and in 2007, on the occasion of the German-Russian "Petersburg Dialogue" summit in Wiesbaden, the domes were re-gilded.

Today, in addition to this church, three streets in the Wiesbaden districts of Biebrich, Delkenheim, and Kastel are named "Elisabethenstraße" in memory of the young Duchess.

## Biebrich Palace (Schloss Biebrich)
![Biebrich]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Schloss Biebrich.jpg)
Biebrich Palace is located on the banks of the Rhine in the southern part of Wiesbaden. It originated as a garden and garden house purchased by Prince Georg August Samuel of Nassau-Idstein around 1700. He loved the location so much that he decided to expand the garden house into a residence in 1701. By the time of his death in 1721, much of the construction remained unfinished. With the extinction of the Nassau-Idstein line, work halted until 1730, when it resumed under the leadership of Princess Charlotte Amalie of Nassau-Usingen.

In 1734, Prince Karl of Nassau-Usingen decided to move the capital from remote Usingen to the banks of the Rhine. Subsequently, stables, residential quarters, a west wing, and riverfront walls were added. By 1750, the palace was largely complete and became the official seat of the government and court. No major changes occurred for several decades thereafter.

In 1829, Duke Wilhelm of Nassau modernized the palace in a classical style. Despite this, he decided to build a new palace within Wiesbaden city center. From 1842, Biebrich Palace ceased to be the seat of government but was retained as a summer residence.

In 1866, Prussia annexed the Duchy of Nassau, and the palace lost its political role. Although nominally still belonging to the abdicated Duke, it was largely uninhabited and received only minimal maintenance. In 1934, the palace was sold to the Prussian government, but planned restorations were shelved due to World War II. In the final stages of the war, parts of the building were bombed, and furniture was stolen or used as firewood, leaving the palace nearly derelict.

Fortunately, starting in the 1960s, the Hessian state government began restoration efforts. Today, Biebrich Palace houses various institutions and frequently hosts celebrations and events. The original chapel and riverside terrace have been transformed into a cafe and restaurant, while the spacious palace park is a popular destination for public walks and relaxation on weekends.

## Mosburg
![Mosburg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wiesbaden/Wiesbaden_Mosburg.jpg)
Walking into the Biebrich Palace Park, one's eyes are often drawn to the Mosburg, a ruin standing quietly by the lake. Its history dates back to the 14th or 15th century. It is speculated to have originally been a moated castle (Wasserburg) built by the House of Nassau. The castle was sold in 1654 and had become a ruin by 1776. After changing hands several times, it returned to the Nassau family in 1804 and was rebuilt into a residential castle with a medieval appearance. It sits on a peninsula in the man-made Mosburg pond (Mosburgweiher) and features its own castle garden.

During the era of Duke Adolph, the sculptor Emil Alexander Hopfgarten established a studio here, creating several important works, including the sarcophagus of Duchess Elizabeth of Nassau and statues for several churches in Wiesbaden. This location also attracted composer Richard Wagner, who for a time considered it an ideal place to live and compose (though this never came to fruition).

In the early 20th century, the Mosburg was converted into a museum, but it closed after being looted during World War II. Subsequently, moisture damage to the roof structure led to rapid deterioration, eventually leaving the building in its current state as a ruin.
