## History of Nuremberg
***Origins and the House of Hohenzollern***

Nuremberg is the second-largest city in Bavaria and the largest in the Franconia region. It boasts a long history dating back to 1050. Although Nuremberg was established later than some neighboring cities, it became a vital trade and cultural hub of the Holy Roman Empire, often referred to as the empire's "unofficial capital."

Early human activity in the Nuremberg area can be traced back to the Bronze Age. The famous Golden Cone of Ezelsdorf-Buch (Goldhut von Ezelsdorf-Buch) is a prime example of the craftsmanship from this era. Records indicate that Celts inhabited the area around 400 BC, followed by Germanic tribes, including the Marcomanni, around 100 BC. Around 800 AD, Slavic peoples settled near what is now the Main Market (Hauptmarkt). These archaeological findings highlight the cultural and demographic diversity of the region during the early Middle Ages.

The exact founding year of Nuremberg remains uncertain, but it is estimated to be between 1000 and 1040, as part of the Holy Roman Empire's central defense system to consolidate links between Saxony, Bavaria, East Franconia, and Bohemia. In 1007, the neighboring town of Fürth first appeared in documents when it was granted to the newly established Diocese of Bamberg (Bistum Bamberg) by the Emperor. Nuremberg first appeared in records in 1050, when Emperor Henry III transferred market, minting, and customs rights from Fürth to Nuremberg to balance Bamberg's influence, laying the foundation for the city's economic rise.

In 1105, Nuremberg was destroyed during a power struggle between Emperor Henry IV and his son, Henry V. To manage royal property more effectively, the Raabs family from Austria was appointed as the Burgraves of Nuremberg (Burggraf). When the Staufer dynasty regained control in 1138, the existing defenses were converted into the Imperial Castle (Kaiserburg). The Raabs family received the hereditary title of Burgrave, and a new settlement was established south of the Pegnitz River, forming what is now the St. Lorenz district.

Frederick Barbarossa continued these plans, expanding Nuremberg Castle into an Imperial Palace (Kaiserpfalz). As an imperial residence, Nuremberg enjoyed privileges such as exemptions from escort, weighing, and market taxes. During this period, the territories of the Counts of Sulzbach to the east reverted to the crown after the line died out, providing a resource base for Nuremberg's growing forging and casting industries.

In 1192, after the Raabs line ended, Emperor Henry VI granted the title of Burgrave to a member of the then-obscure Hohenzollern family (who would later rise to dominate Germany). However, the Burgraves' power was gradually curtailed as administrative and judicial authority outside the castle was transferred to the Imperial Caretaker (Reichsschultheiß), representing the Emperor. The interaction between the Burgraves, the Imperial Caretaker, and the later City Council profoundly shaped Nuremberg's development.

***Imperial Free City and Self-Governance***

In 1219, Emperor Frederick II issued the famous "Great Letter of Freedom" (Großer Freiheitsbrief), expanding Nuremberg's market rights, granting tax collection authority, and declaring the Emperor as the city's sole protector. This made Nuremberg an Imperial Free City, directly subordinate to the Emperor.

Based on these privileges, Nuremberg began to form a self-governing body known as the Patriziat, which functioned similarly to a modern city council. The families eligible for the council held leading political and economic positions, such as the ministerial families who moved to the city around 1250. Meanwhile, the Hohenzollern Burgraves moved their residence to Cadolzburg and occupied much of the surrounding territory. Over time, tensions between the City Council and the Burgraves escalated, eventually leading to the destruction of Nuremberg Castle in 1420.

In 1411, Burgrave Frederick VI of the House of Hohenzollern was appointed Governor of the Margraviate of Brandenburg, later becoming Prince-Elector (Kurfürst) in 1417. To consolidate his other territories, he sold the title of Burgrave and the ruined castle to the Nuremberg City Council in 1427. Although the Hohenzollerns attempted to reclaim Nuremberg during the two Margrave Wars (Markgrafenkrieg), they were unsuccessful.

The influence of the third power, the Imperial Caretaker (Reichsschultheiß), also weakened through the City Council's efforts. Through a series of privileges (such as high jurisdiction granted in 1320), Nuremberg’s autonomy grew. By 1427, when the council acquired the title of Burgrave, the office of the Imperial Caretaker also effectively fell under its control. Thus, the City Council finally achieved absolute control over the city.

***City of Peace and Human Rights***

Nuremberg entered its Golden Age during the 15th and 16th centuries. Thanks to prosperous trade, it was said that Nuremberg’s wealth rivaled that of the entire Kingdom of Bohemia. In 1423, the Emperor entrusted the Imperial Regalia (Reichskleinodien) to Nuremberg for safekeeping, earning the city the reputation of the "Empire's Treasury" and the "unofficial capital."

This era saw Nuremberg become a cradle of art and science, producing figures like the painter Albrecht Dürer, Martin Behaim (creator of the oldest surviving terrestrial globe), and Peter Henlein (inventor of the pocket watch).
However, the city's status declined during the Thirty Years' War, much like other German cities. It rose again in the 19th century as Germany's industrial heart. In 1835, Germany’s first railway opened between Nuremberg and Fürth, sparking industrial growth. Companies like Schuckertwerke (later Siemens) and Maschinenbau-Actien-Gesellschaft Nürnberg (later MAN) expanded rapidly.

Nuremberg is also world-renowned as the "City of Toys." Craftsmen began making delicate toys as early as the Middle Ages. During the industrial era, the production of tin toys thrived, making Nuremberg a global center for model railways. The annual Nuremberg International Toy Fair remains a major cultural highlight.

Due to its historical significance, the Nazi Party (NSDAP) designated Nuremberg as the site for their Nuremberg Rallies (Reichsparteitag) after taking power in 1933. Following World War II, the city hosted the Nuremberg Trials to prosecute Nazi war criminals. To confront its past and embrace responsibility, the city completed "The Way of Human Rights" (Straße der Menschenrechte) in the city center in 1993. It features 27 pillars, each engraved with an article from the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in different languages—a symbol of the city's commitment to peace and human rights.

## St. Sebald Church (Sebaldkirche)
![Sebaldkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Nürnberg/Nürnberg_Sebaldkirche.jpg)
St. Sebald Church is the oldest municipal parish church (Pfarrkirche) in Nuremberg. Its construction began between 1230 and 1240 on the foundations of a previous Romanesque church, while the two magnificent Gothic towers were not completed until 1379, resulting in a Gothic church that retains Romanesque elements.

From the 14th century onwards, St. Sebald served as the Council Church (Ratskirche) for the Nuremberg City Council. Unlike St. Lorenz Church, until the late 15th century, only families of the City Council had the right to donate permanent decorations to the church. Consequently, St. Sebald Church houses a vast collection of precious religious artworks, mostly dating from the pre-Reformation era. The most historically significant piece is the shrine of the city's patron saint, St. Sebald (Stadtpatron Sebald). Since the 14th century, the saint's relics have been kept in an exquisite reliquary. At the end of the 15th century, the renowned brass founders Peter Vischer the Elder and his sons were commissioned to create a bronze casing to protect the reliquary and enhance its artistic value, a masterpiece completed between 1508 and 1519.

Additionally, St. Sebald Church preserves one of the most important medieval stained-glass windows in Germany: the Kaiserfenster (Emperor's Window). Designed by famous artists such as Albrecht Dürer, Hans Süß von Kulmbach, and Veit Hirschvogel the Elder, this exquisite window depicts the three symbols of power in medieval Nuremberg: the Holy Roman Emperor, the Bishop of Bamberg, and the Margraves of the House of Hohenzollern. It is an absolute must-see during your visit!

## St. Lorenz Church (Lorenzkirche)
![Lorenzkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Nürnberg/Nürnberg_Lorenzkirche.jpg)
Located south of the Pegnitz River, St. Lorenz Church began construction around 1250. Beyond its grand architecture, the church interior is home to many exquisite works of art.

Upon entering, visitors are greeted by the "Angelic Salutation" (Engelsgruß), a woodcarving suspended in mid-air. Commissioned by the Tucher family and completed in 1518 by the sculptor Veit Stoß, it depicts the Archangel Gabriel announcing to the Virgin Mary that she will conceive by the Holy Spirit and give birth to Jesus. Notably, although Nuremberg converted to Protestantism after 1525, this artwork remained in its original place. It was briefly removed after Nuremberg was incorporated into the Kingdom of Bavaria in 1806 and reinstalled in 1817. It was taken down again during World War II in 1939 for protection, finally returning to its position in 1952.

The second highlight is the Tabernacle (Sakramentshaus), used for storing the monstrance and the consecrated host (bread) after Mass. Crafted from sandstone, it stands approximately 20 meters high and 3.4 meters wide. Commissioned by the Imhoff family of Nuremberg, it was completed between 1493 and 1496 by the sculptor Adam Kraft at a cost of 700 guilders (Gulden)—a sum that could have purchased two houses in the city center at the time. The tabernacle features many tiers; at the very bottom, various beasts and demons are depicted, while the base is supported by three figures of different ages who appear to be carrying the entire structure. One of these figures is Adam Kraft himself. Above the base, various saints and biblical scenes, such as the Last Supper, are intricately portrayed.

Similar to St. Sebald, St. Lorenz Church features stunning stained-glass windows, mostly sponsored by emperors and prominent families of the era. The central window in the choir (Hallenchor) is also known as the Kaiserfenster (Emperor's Window), donated by Emperor Frederick III. It depicts historical narratives such as Emperor Constantine the Great being baptized by Pope Sylvester I and how Constantine’s mother, St. Helena, discovered the True Cross.

Followers interested in more details can visit the Nuremberg Museums website for further information.

## Church of Our Lady (Frauenkirche)
![Frauenkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Nürnberg/Nürnberg_Frauenkirche.jpg)
The Gothic Church of Our Lady, located on Nuremberg's Main Market Square (Hauptmarkt), is one of the city's three most significant churches. The site was originally occupied by a synagogue that was destroyed in 1349. In 1355, Holy Roman Emperor Charles IV ordered the construction of the Frauenkirche as a royal court chapel. While he initially planned to keep the Imperial Regalia here, those plans were never realized.

In 1509, the church's famous mechanical clock was installed. Every day at noon, the "Männleinlaufen" (Running of the Little Men) performs, showing seven Electors paying homage to Charles IV. This commemorates the Golden Bull of 1356, which decreed that the Holy Roman Emperor must be chosen by seven Electors and that the newly elected emperor must hold his first Imperial Diet in Nuremberg. This regulation remained in effect until the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire in 1806 and is considered one of the empire's most important constitutional documents.

Furthermore, the Frauenkirche serves as the primary backdrop for the Nuremberg Christmas Market (Christkindlesmarkt), where the opening ceremony is held annually, adding a festive atmosphere to the market.

## Imperial Castle (Kaiserburg)
During the Middle Ages, the emperors of the Holy Roman Empire did not reside in a single city but traveled throughout the realm to meet vassals, preside over trials, or attend Imperial Diets. During these journeys, the emperor and his court stayed at various Imperial Castles (Kaiserpfalzen) along the way. The Imperial Castle of Nuremberg is one of the most important medieval imperial palaces in Europe.

Here, visitors can see the Romanesque Double Chapel, a deep well reaching 47 meters, and the towering Sinwell Tower (Sinwellturm). Additionally, the castle museum houses a rich collection, including many medieval weapons—a must-visit for history enthusiasts!

## Heilig-Geist-Spital (Hospital of the Holy Spirit)
![Heilig-Geist-Spital]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Nürnberg/Nürnberg_Heilig-Geist-Spital.jpg)
The Heilig-Geist-Spital is one of Nuremberg's most iconic landmarks. Established in 1339, it is impossible to miss when walking from the train station toward the old town center. From 1423, it served as the repository for the Imperial Regalia of the Holy Roman Empire and the Holy Lance. These treasures were publicly displayed once a year for veneration by the faithful. Before an imperial coronation, the regalia would be transported to Frankfurt and returned to Nuremberg after the ceremony. Today, they are housed in the Imperial Treasury at the Hofburg Palace in Vienna.

When visiting the Heilig-Geist-Spital today, you can find two famous table tombs (Tischgräber) in the infirmary courtyard: those of Konrad Groß and Herdegen Valzner. Representing two of the most influential families in medieval Nuremberg, they were also key patrons of the hospital. As table tombs were often used to commemorate significant religious or secular figures, these monuments not only highlight their status but also reflect their profound contributions to the hospital's development.

## Reichsparteitagsgelände (Nazi Party Rally Grounds)
![Reichsparteitagsgelände]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Nürnberg/Nürnberg_Reichsparteitagsgelände.jpg)
From 1933 to 1938, Nuremberg served as the site for the Nazi Party rallies. Starting in 1934, Hitler’s architect, Albert Speer, designed a master plan for the Nazi Party Rally Grounds in the southeastern outskirts of the city. This included vast squares and the Zeppelin Field (where Zeppelins had landed as early as 1909). The Neoclassical style of the early 20th century was exaggerated to an overwhelming scale here and in other Nazi architecture. Designed to appear sublime, powerful, and awe-inspiring, the structures lack color and decorative elements. Furthermore, 152 anti-aircraft searchlights were installed around the Zeppelintribüne (Zeppelin Grandstand), pointing straight into the sky to create a "Cathedral of Light" and a solemn atmosphere.

This site remains the largest extant complex of Nazi architecture and is home to the Documentation Center (Dokumentationszentrum), which serves as an ideal starting point for exploring the grounds.

## Memorium Nürnberger Prozesse (Memorium Nuremberg Trials)
Courtroom 600, where the Nuremberg Trials took place, is located in the northwestern outskirts of the city. Here, visitors can gain a deep understanding of World War II history, as well as the trial proceedings and their outcomes. The location was primarily chosen for logistical reasons: the Palace of Justice (Justizpalast Nürnberg) was largely undamaged and provided ample space for representatives of the four Allied powers, while the adjacent prison simplified the housing and security of the defendants. Although Nuremberg’s history as the site of the Nazi Party rallies added symbolic weight, it was not the deciding factor for the venue.

The Nuremberg Trials were more than just a judicial proceeding against Nazi war criminals; their significance is monumental. They established the legal foundations for war crimes, crimes against humanity, and crimes of aggression for the first time, making it clear that individuals are responsible for their actions and can no longer hide behind the excuse of "obeying orders." Simultaneously, the trials exposed the atrocities of the Nazi regime, bringing historical truths to light and providing a measure of justice for victims. Furthermore, they symbolized international cooperation and the rule of law, laying the groundwork for the post-war international order. These trials serve as a constant reminder to cherish peace and prevent history from repeating itself—a lesson that remains vital for all of humanity, both now and in the future.

## Spielzeugmuseum Nürnberg (Nuremberg Toy Museum)
![Spielzeugmuseum]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Nürnberg/Nürnberg_Spielzeugmuseum.jpg)
Since the Middle Ages, Nuremberg has been known as Europe's "City of Toys," particularly famous for its exquisite wooden toys and craftsmanship. With the onset of industrialization, its tin toys and model railways also gained worldwide fame. The Toy Museum in the city center displays toys from various eras, reflecting the unique characteristics of different periods as well as social changes and technological developments.

There is a charming story behind the museum: its collection originated from the Würzburg collectors Lydia and Paul Bayer. Their passion for toys was passed down to their daughter, Lydia, who later wrote a thesis on European dollhouses and earned a doctorate in Art History. In 1966, Lydia recognized the value of her parents' collection and began collaborating with the city of Nuremberg, leading to the opening of the Toy Museum in 1971.

Additionally, near the museum on Weißgerbergasse, you can find Nuremberg's largest ensemble of half-timbered houses. No visit to Nuremberg is complete without seeing this area.

## Must-Eat in Nuremberg
***Bratwursthäusle***
![Bratwursthäusle]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Nürnberg/Nürnberg_Bratwurst.jpg)
A legendary hundred-year-old Nuremberg sausage shop that has been grilling right next to St. Sebald Church for 700 years. The flavor is truly extraordinary! The intense charcoal aroma paired with flavorful sauerkraut is incredibly refreshing.

***Nudeln Studio***
   
Still hungry in Germany? Then Nudeln Studio is the place to go.

You can get a free refill of noodles here (as of 2024). Not only that, but the broth is incredibly fragrant and light. You can also add beef slices and chili sauce to dry noodles for a "one-noodle-two-ways" experience—definitely the best choice.

***Schanzenbräu Schankwirtschaft***
   
How can you visit Nuremberg without trying Schäufele? The pork shoulder is incredibly tender, and the skin is so crispy it rivals Cantonese roast meats. It pairs perfectly with the chewy, fragrant potato dumplings (Klöße).

The potato soup was served with dark bread and was a bit salty. The third dish ordered was a beef stew, which also went very well with the pan-fried Klöße.

The menu here is written on the walls, giving the place a lot of character—it's also a great spot to practice your German.
