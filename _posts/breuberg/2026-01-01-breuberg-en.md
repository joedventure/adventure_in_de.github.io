## Breuberg Castle
Breuberg Castle (Burg Breuberg) has stood majestically above the Mümling valley in the Odenwald forest for over 800 years. Having witnessed countless conflicts without ever being fully destroyed, it remains one of the best-preserved medieval castles in southern and central Germany, maintaining its historical character across various eras.

The castle's history dates back to the Staufen period in the 12th century, built as a defensive fortress by the Lords of Breuberg. Its sturdy keep and Romanesque gatehouse still stand today. In 1323, the Breuberg line ended, and the castle was inherited by four noble families. It became a "Ganerbenburg" (joint fief inheritance castle), where each family maintained different sections while sharing management of the 85-meter-deep well to ensure the fortress's self-sufficiency.

At the end of the 15th century, the Counts of Wertheim acquired sole ownership of the castle. To adapt to new forms of warfare and defend against the expansionist efforts of Hesse, Mainz, and the Palatinate, they added four massive defensive towers, artillery platforms, and a dry moat.

Following the extinction of the House of Wertheim in 1556, ownership passed to the Counts of Erbach and Löwenstein-Wertheim. During this period, the castle's role shifted from pure defense to emphasizing residential comfort and noble prestige. The Johann Casimir Building (Johann Casimir-Bau) perfectly illustrates the Renaissance passion for classical art, adorned with the coat of arms of the Löwenstein-Wertheim family and reliefs depicting ancient Greek and Roman deities.

By the 18th century, Burg Breuberg's importance as a seat of government declined, though it remained in the joint possession of the two families until its sale to the German Youth Hostel Association (Deutsches Jugendherbergswerk) in 1919. Today, the castle is owned by the State of Hesse.
