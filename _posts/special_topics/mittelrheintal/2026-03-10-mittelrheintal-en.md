The Middle Rhine Valley, as a UNESCO World Heritage site, certainly deserves a proper introduction.

Extending 65 kilometers from Rüdesheim in the south to Koblenz in the north, the Middle Rhine Valley is dotted with countless historic towns and castles. Not only does it offer breathtaking scenery, but it is also steeped in historical narratives that have deeply influenced intellectuals and artists throughout the ages. Poets like Goethe, in one of his earliest works, bestowed the Rhine with the paternal symbolism of "Father Rhine."

It is estimated that there are approximately 40 castles lining both banks of the river. While taking the train is a fantastic way to explore, the constant transfers might mean it would take months to see every site thoroughly. Fortunately, Rhine river cruises are now an option, allowing travelers to comfortably enjoy the coastal beauty over a few days.

Let’s begin our exploration of the Middle Rhine Valley castles from the southernmost point: Rüdesheim!

## Brömserburg
![Brömserburg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Rüd_Brömserburg.jpg)
Walking from the Rüdesheim train station toward the cable car station, the first landmark you encounter is Brömserburg. Named after the knightly family of Rüdesheim, it was likely originally built to protect the ferries on the Rhine. In 983 AD, the complex was expanded into an administrative outpost by the Archbishopric of Mainz and was granted to local nobles in the early 13th century. By the 14th century, the castle became the core of a larger defensive facility, though it suffered severe damage during the Thirty Years' War.

In 1806, the Imperial Count of Ingelheim (Reichsgraf von Ingelheim) took over the site and transformed it into a Romantic-style residence. During this period, many prominent social figures visited Brömserburg, including Wolfgang von Goethe, Heinrich Heine, the Brothers Grimm, and musicians such as Felix Mendelssohn-Bartholdy and Paganini.

In 1941, the city of Rüdesheim purchased the complex and established the Rheingau Wine Museum here. Although the museum has been closed since 2017 and is no longer open to the public, citizens have still been able to enjoy wine and snacks in the castle gardens on weekends in recent years, soaking in the unique atmosphere created by the ancient walls.

## Boosenburg
![Boosenburg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Rüd_Boosenburg.jpg)
Built between the 10th and 11th centuries, Boosenburg stands side-by-side with the neighboring Brömserburg. In the 12th century, it served as the base for the local noble "Foxes of Rüdesheim" (Füchse von Rüdesheim). In 1474, it passed into the hands of the Boos von Waldeck family, from whom it takes its current name.

After centuries of history, reconstruction of the dilapidated castle began in 1830, including the addition of a wine cellar. Since 1939, Boosenburg has served as the headquarters for the wine company Weingut Carl Jung and has briefly hosted many famous artists. Due to this connection, the castle frequently hosts small private concerts. The composer most deeply associated with this place is Johannes Brahms. He visited Rüdesheim and the surrounding areas multiple times and completed his Symphony No. 3 in the Rheingau in 1883. Consequently, the performance held in 1947 to commemorate the 50th anniversary of his death is the most well-known.

## Rossel Castle Ruins (Burgruine Rossel)
![Burgruine Rossel]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Rüd_Burgruine Rossel.jpg)
Arriving at the Rossel Castle ruins signifies that the hiking portion of the Romantic Tour (for details, please refer to the Rüdesheim page) is nearing its end; the road ahead consists almost entirely of flat, easy-to-walk trails.

These castle ruins were built at the highest point of the Niederwald by Count Karl Maximilian von Ostein in 1787. Records suggest the ruins were built solely to decorate the landscape; they were designed in a ruin-like style from the start and served no defensive purpose.

The view from here is exceptional, offering a panorama of the Mouse Tower (Mäuseturm), the Bingen Hole (Binger Loch), the ruins of Ehrenfels Castle, and the magnificent sight of the Nahe River flowing into the Rhine. It serves as a wonderful reward for the effort of reaching the summit.

## Ehrenfels Castle (Burg Ehrenfels)
![Burg Ehrenfels]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Rüd_Burg Ehrenfels.jpg)
Located halfway up the mountain, Ehrenfels Castle was built between 983 and 1200 AD. It only began to gain significance after becoming a customs station for Mainz in the 13th century. During the conflict between the Staufer and Welf families for the throne of the Holy Roman Empire, the castle underwent its first major expansion, resulting in a 20-meter-high shield wall and two corner towers. In 1224, the Archbishop of Mainz reclaimed this strategic location from local nobles through a royal decree.

With the completion of the famous Mouse Tower in 1270, shipping on the Rhine could be fully monitored. Except for 61 privileged nobles, all ships wishing to pass through had to pay customs duties. However, despite the substantial tax revenue, the finances for maintaining the castle remained tight. The Archbishop of Mainz eventually had to mortgage the castle to Kuno von Falkenstein, the Dean of the Cathedral Chapter (Dompropst), who further fortified it. In 1356, Archbishop Gerlach of Mainz used a ruse to retake the castle, bringing Ehrenfels back under Mainz's control. Even during the bloody Thirty Years' War, the castle miraculously survived.

In 1688, the French occupied Ehrenfels Castle and destroyed most of its buildings during their retreat the following year, causing the customs station to be moved to Bingen. Since then, Ehrenfels Castle has fallen into ruin. In 1945, the State of Hesse became the owner of the site, and maintenance and restoration work only began in the early 1990s.

## Klopp Castle (Burg Klopp)
![Burg Klopp]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Bingen_Burg Klopp.jpg)
Klopp Castle is located in Bingen, directly across from Rüdesheim. Records indicate that its predecessor was built on the site of an ancient Roman fortification around 10 BC. It first appeared in documents around 1105 AD, associated with the legend of Holy Roman Emperor Henry IV (the one who performed the penance at Canossa to seek papal pardon) being imprisoned there by his son.

The current main structure of the castle was built between 1240 and 1249 by Archbishop Siegfried III of Eppstein. Its primary function was to monitor and collect tolls from merchant ships on the Rhine, forming a tight defensive and taxation system with Ehrenfels Castle and the Mouse Tower (Mäuseturm) on the opposite bank. Additionally, it was tasked with defending the mouth of the Nahe River and the residents of Bingen, serving as a vital link in the regional defense network. However, it was ultimately destroyed by French troops in 1689 during the War of the Palatine Succession and completely demolished in 1713.

Nevertheless, the remaining ruins were not forgotten. Around 1853, merchant Ludwig Maria Cron purchased the site and began reconstruction in a predominantly Neo-Gothic style, including the gatehouse and the main tower.

During the same period, the Romantic movement was sweeping across Europe, and Klopp Castle became a source of inspiration for painters and poets. According to visitor logs, famous figures such as Karl Marx, Victor Hugo, and Richard Wagner once visited the site.

In 1897, the city of Bingen acquired Klopp Castle, converting it into a town hall and a folk museum. Although damaged again during World War II, restoration was completed between 1952 and 1953. Today, Klopp Castle houses several municipal offices of Bingen, but many areas are open to tourists. If you have the chance, you can overlook the magnificent scenery of the Rheingau from the main tower. Furthermore, the castle courtyard hosts the international jazz festival "Bingen Swings," attracting many jazz enthusiasts every year.

## Mouse Tower (Mäuseturm)
![Mäuseturm]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Bingen_Mäuseturm.jpg)
The most famous attraction in Bingen is undoubtedly the Mouse Tower. Beyond its historically important strategic location, it is a landmark steeped in historical legends and mythological color.

It is believed that a small fortification existed here as early as the Roman period to monitor river traffic. Following the chaos of political transitions, the structure gradually fell into ruin until Archbishop Hatto II of Mainz became the ruler of Bingen in 968 AD, after which the defensive tower on the site was renovated to serve as a key point for controlling the Rhine.

The name "Mouse Tower" was first recorded in 1516; however, its etymology does not come from mice but from the Middle High German word "musen," meaning to watch or lurk, reflecting its original function as a lookout tower. Like Klopp Castle, the Mouse Tower was completely destroyed by French troops in 1689. It wasn't until the 19th century that the Kingdom of Prussia decided to rebuild this landmark and establish a shipping signal tower to help guide vessels safely.

The Mouse Tower is so famous because of a well-known legend dating back to the 16th century involving Archbishop Hatto of Mainz.

## The Legend of the Mouse Tower
The legend of the Mouse Tower revolves around the greed and ruthlessness of Archbishop Hatto of Mainz and his cold treatment of the poor during a famine, eventually leading to divine punishment. While the legend lacks historical basis, it has linked the name of the Mouse Tower with a somber moral, becoming one of the most widely known cautionary tales in German folklore. There are several versions of the legend; here are three:

Version 1: In the 10th century, Mainz was ruled by Archbishop Hatto. He was a very strict and greedy man who, despite his immense wealth, never wished to give alms to the poor. His barns were full of grain and his vaults overflowing with gold, yet he was never satisfied and continued to oppress his subjects.

One year, a famine struck. A large crowd of starving people gathered outside the Archbishop's castle to beg for food, but Hatto refused, mocking them as lazy and useless peasants. When the starving people refused to leave and continued to plead, he ordered his soldiers to arrest everyone—men, women, and children alike—lock them in an old barn, and set it on fire. As the flames rose, piercing screams were heard, but Hatto laughed heartlessly, saying, "Listen, that's the mice whistling!"

However, immediately after he spoke those words, divine punishment descended. Countless mice emerged from the burning barn, surging toward the Archbishop's castle like a flood. Hatto fled in terror, but the army of mice followed him everywhere. Finally, he fled to a small island in the Rhine in front of Bingen, ordering a high tower to be built and an iron chain hammock to be hung at the top to escape the disaster.

But the next morning, the island was surrounded by mice. Hatto tried to escape by boat, but the mice had chewed it to pieces. In terror, he ran up the tower, closing the doors level by level, and finally huddled on the bed. A few days later, people only found his bones in the tower; the mice had eaten him alive. Since then, the tower has been called the Mouse Tower, and legend says Hatto's ghost can still be seen wandering by the tower at night.

Version 2: Long ago, there was a bishop in Mainz named Hatto, who was cruel and indifferent. A great famine broke out in the Rhine region, and many people starved to death. Although Hatto's warehouses were full of grain, he only sold to the rich and refused to help the poor. As the number of starving people grew, they gathered to beg him to open the granaries, but he ordered soldiers to seize them, lock them in a barn, and burn them to death. When he and his retinue heard the screams of the victims, he actually sneered, "Listen, those are the mice in the barn whistling!"

As soon as he finished speaking, the earth fell into darkness and the sun disappeared. Suddenly, countless mice emerged from the floorboards, walls, and roofs, swarming the palace and eating the food on the tables and the bread in people's hands. Terrified servants scattered, leaving the bishop alone. Hatto fled in haste to the tower in the middle of the Rhine, thinking he could find refuge, but the mice followed him through the water, climbed the tower, and devoured him.

Version 3: In the early 10th century, there was a local man named Hatto, famous for his luxury and cruelty. He later became the Bishop of Mainz. One year, continuous heat and floods caused poor harvests and widespread famine in the Rhine region. Hatto took the opportunity to hoard grain and sell it at high prices for profit. The starving had no way to get food and were full of resentment. Hatto angrily called them lazy beggars, ordered soldiers to arrest and lock them in a barn, then set it on fire, sneering, "Listen, those are the mice in the barn whistling!"

That night, he heard strange noises. Countless mice crawled out from the walls and floors, biting his body. Hatto fled in terror to Ehrenfels Castle, but the mice still followed. Finally, he escaped by boat to a lookout tower in the Rhine and hung himself in an iron bed to escape the mice, but the mice swam across the river regardless, climbed the tower, and bit him to death. It is said that on stormy nights, Hatto's ghost still circles the tower, never finding peace.

Doesn't this story perfectly fit the style of German fairy tales?

## Rheinstein Castle (Burg Rheinstein)
![Burg Rheinstein]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Rheinstein.jpg)
Built in the early 14th century, Rheinstein Castle stands atop a ninety-meter-high rock. When first constructed, the building was known as Vaitzburg, derived from its patron saint, Boniface (Bonifatius). At that time, the castle served primarily as a customs collection point and a defensive stronghold to guard against the increasingly rampant robber knights.

Until the 16th century, Rheinstein Castle mostly belonged to the Archbishopric of Mainz. In 1823, Prince Frederick Wilhelm of Prussia rebuilt the castle in the Romantic style of the Rhine region, which earned it its current name and officially opened it to visitors.

In 1975, opera singer Hermann Hecher purchased the castle and transformed it into a tourist highlight, allowing visitors to experience medieval castle life firsthand. Today, the castle still belongs to the Hecher family, who reside there. If you wish to stay longer at Rheinstein Castle, you can consider spending a night in the castle's guest rooms for a unique castle living experience!

## Reichenstein Castle (Burg Reichenstein)
![Burg Reichenstein]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Reichenstein.jpg)
Reichenstein Castle, located south of Trechtingshausen, is a magnificent and famous castle on the banks of the Rhine. It first appeared in literature in 1213 AD. For several centuries after its construction, it became a stronghold for robber knights until King Rudolf von Habsburg personally led an army to besiege it at the end of the 13th century, ending the robber rule.

In the centuries that followed, due to the castle's unique strategic location, its owners changed constantly, leading to endless destruction and reconstruction. After being destroyed by the French in the 17th century, it gradually fell into ruin.

In 1899, the Kirsch-Puricelli family, who rose from metalworking and became one of Germany's wealthiest industrial noble families, purchased the ruins. Influenced by Rhine Romanticism, the new owners carried out extensive restoration and reconstruction in the Neo-Gothic style, expanding the living quarters and adding a lookout tower.

Today, Reichenstein Castle is a popular tourist destination. It features not only high-end hotels and restaurants but also a museum displaying various art collections, including armor, weapons, furniture, porcelain, and the largest collection of stove plates in Rhineland-Palatinate.

## Sooneck Castle (Burg Sooneck)
![Burg Sooneck]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Sooneck.jpg)
Sooneck Castle is hidden within the Soon Forest (Soonwald), spanning between the two important cities of Bingen and Bacharach.

The castle's original function was to collect tolls from passing ships on the Rhine, but it quickly fell into the hands of robber knights. After being destroyed by war multiple times, it became an important stronghold for the Archbishop of Mainz in the 14th century.

Sooneck Castle was blown up by the French army at the end of the 17th century and remained in ruins until the 19th century, when it was purchased by the then Prussian Crown Prince Frederick William IV and his brothers. Their original idea was to transform Sooneck Castle into a medieval-style royal hunting lodge (Jagdschloss). However, due to the influence of the 1848-49 revolutions, Sooneck Castle was ultimately not used as a hunting lodge. Nevertheless, the interior decoration perfectly showcases the living tastes of the 19th-century Prussian royal family outside the court, and it preserves many original masonry structures, such as the main tower and the main castle building.

Since 1918, Sooneck Castle has been state-owned. After World War II, it was taken over by the State of Rhineland-Palatinate and now houses a museum that fully demonstrates the lifestyle of the king outside the palace.

## Hohneck Castle (Burg Hohneck)
![Burg Hohneck]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Hohneck.jpg)
Hohneck Castle (originally named Burg Haneck), also known as Heimburg, stands above Niederheimbach.

The history of this castle dates back to 1295, built by the Archbishop of Mainz as a response to the provocation of the Count Palatine's illegal occupation of the nearby Sooneck and Reichenstein castles. The castle features a roughly square layout, a sturdy shield wall (Schildmauer) facing the Rhine, and two round towers, the southern one of which was later expanded into a soaring main tower.

Unfortunately, Hohneck Castle was destroyed during the Thirty Years' War and subsequently fell into ruin. From the 17th to the mid-19th century, ownership of the ruins changed hands multiple times, and the name evolved from Haneck to Hoheneck. Between 1866 and 1868, as aristocrats and wealthy citizens became enthusiasts for the charm of Rhine castle ruins, Baron Otto von Wackerbarth carried out the first restoration, integrating the medieval structure and adopting a Neo-Gothic architectural style with battlements and corner towers.

After several changes of ownership, the castle was purchased by Nora Dunlop in 1920 and managed on her behalf by her relatives, the Stinnes family, giants of German industry. During the period of inflation, maintenance work on the castle provided many jobs for local residents. Hohneck Castle served as a representative residence for the Stinnes family and their guests for many years.

The Stinnes family sold the castle in 1965, after which its condition deteriorated. In 1987, the Bachmann-Thurn family took over the castle and invested millions in a comprehensive modern restoration. Despite several name changes and reconstructions, Hohneck Castle remains an important historical landmark on the Rhine today. However, it is currently privately owned and is not open to the public.

## Nollig Castle (Burg Nollig)
![Burg Nollig]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Nollig.jpg)
Nollig Castle is situated above the town of Lorch on the Middle Rhine. Despite its name, Nollig Castle was never considered a true castle; it was essentially part of the Lorch town fortifications, primarily functioning as a lookout tower to protect Lorch from attacks from above.

The castle was built around 1300. Evidence suggests that this defensive structure was originally built in the traditional German timber-frame style before being encased in stone walls. Later, it was protected by a 3-meter-thick shield wall and featured two round towers. It is speculated that there were plans to extend the walls of Lorch along the valley to Nollig Castle; however, this connection was never realized, likely due to unstable shale foundations and a possible shortage of funds.

Nollig Castle remains private property today and is not open to the public. Tourists can mostly view it from the trails below or from the town of Lorch.

## Fürstenberg Castle (Burg Fürstenberg)
![Burg Fürstenberg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Fürstenberg.jpg)
Fürstenberg Castle was once a facility serving both defensive and customs purposes. It was built in the early 13th century by the Archbishop of Cologne to counter the expanding power of the Counts Palatine of the Rhine (Rheinischen Pfalzgrafen).

At that time, Fürstenberg Castle possessed a thick shield wall and a 25-meter-high round main tower, which allowed it to miraculously survive the Thirty Years' War centuries later. However, its strategic position meant that within a century of its completion, it was ruled by three different Electorates, eventually falling into ruin during the War of the Palatine Succession.

In 1993, Gernot Stelter purchased the ruins and worked closely with the heritage protection office to preserve the existing historical remains. They even used chemical analysis to restore the ancient mortar formula and utilized original stones from the ruins to repair the walls. Thanks to their efforts, we can now not only explore the castle ruins deeply but also visit the Fürstenberg Castle Winery (Weingut Schloss Fürstenberg) run by the Stelter family. If interested, you can choose to stay in a holiday apartment overlooking the castle ruins and the Rhine.

## Stahleck Castle (Burg Stahleck)
![Burg Stahleck]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Stahleck.jpg)
The name "Stahleck Castle" is composed of the Middle High German words "stahel" (steel) and "ecke" (corner/edge).

Located on the heights near the famous town of Bacharach in the Middle Rhine Valley, the earliest written record of Stahleck Castle appeared in 1135, associated with the then lord of the castle, Goswin von Stahleck. From an architectural perspective, the massive residential tower (Wohnturm) in the castle courtyard is the oldest part. In front of it is a moat pond (Burgweiher), which is extremely rare for a hilltop castle and was once filled with water. The western side of the castle was once part of the city defense system. Before becoming the residence of the Wittelsbach Counts Palatine—who ruled the area for over five hundred years—it was originally the seat of the Archiepiscopal Bailiffs (Erzbischöflichen Vögte) of Cologne.

Stahleck Castle had a tumultuous fate: it was besieged and occupied by different factions up to eight times during the Thirty Years' War. Its main tower and all residential buildings were blown up by French explosives at the end of the 17th century during the War of the Palatine Succession. The debris from the explosion even destroyed the Gothic Werner Chapel (Wernerkapelle) at the foot of the hill. Due to the extent of the damage, the castle remained in ruins for over two hundred years.

In 1909, the Rhineland Association for Monument Protection and Landscape Preservation (Rheinische Verein für Denkmalpflege und Landschaftsschutz) purchased the site from the Prussian administration and transformed it into a youth hostel in June 1926 after reconstruction work was completed.

During the Nazi era, Stahleck Castle was used as a place to indoctrinate youth with ideology. Between 1940 and 1942, it briefly served as a military hospital and later became a re-education camp for ideologically critical youth.

After World War II, French soldiers were briefly stationed here until 1947, when the castle resumed its previous use as a youth hostel. A new round of restoration and expansion began in 1965 and was fully completed and reopened in 1967. Interested friends can try to book a stay; it is an excellent base for exploring Stahleck Castle and Bacharach!

## Pfalzgrafenstein Castle (Pfalzgrafenstein)
![Pfalzgrafenstein]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Pfalzgrafenstein.jpg)
Whether driving, taking the train, or traveling by boat through the Middle Rhine Valley, you will always see a large ship sailing in the distance at some point. However, upon closer inspection, you realize it is not a ship at all, but a stone castle of the Counts Palatine built on the Rhine. Due to its location, Pfalzgrafenstein is also one of the lowest-altitude medieval castles in the Rhine plain.

Pfalzgrafenstein was built in 1327 by the then Count Palatine and later Holy Roman Emperor Ludwig IV. The project caused protests from the church at the time; even the Pope himself strongly opposed establishing a customs station in the middle of the river, fearing it would reduce the church's vested interests in tolls. However, Ludwig IV ignored the opposition and in 1339 further added a ring wall and defensive passages, upgrading it into a fully functional defensive castle, thereby securing customs revenue between Kaub and Bacharach.

Notably, Pfalzgrafenstein is one of only two castles in the Middle Rhine Valley that were not destroyed by war. During the War of the Bavarian-Palatine Succession (Bayerisch-Pfälzischen Erbfolgekrieg) in 1504, it miraculously survived a 39-day siege unscathed. On New Year's Eve 1813-1814, it became a historic turning point in the Napoleonic Wars when the Prussian Marshal Blücher led the Allied forces to successfully cross the Rhine here, sounding the clarion call for the march into France.

After being incorporated into Prussia in 1867, Pfalzgrafenstein became a popular tourist attraction due to the rise of Rhine Romanticism. After customs collection ceased, it transitioned into a shipping signal station until the 1960s. To preserve its historical appearance, Pfalzgrafenstein was deliberately not modernized after its last renovation in the 1970s; to this day, the interior remains without electricity or toilets.

Today, you can visit Pfalzgrafenstein by taking a small boat from Kaub. From the well-preserved antique furniture, cannons, dungeons, and ancient defensive passages inside the castle, one can still feel the atmosphere of medieval life.

## Gutenfels Castle (Burg Gutenfels)
![Burg Gutenfels]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Gutenfels.jpg)
Gutenfels Castle towers over the rock face two hundred meters above the city of Kaub, Germany, surrounded by vineyards. It represents a classic architectural style from the Staufer dynasty (rough rock surfaces paired with neatly cut edges).

Gutenfels was built in 1222 by the Falkenstein-Münzenberg family and was originally named Cube after the city where it is located. Together with Pfalzgrafenstein, it shared the responsibility of guarding the city of Kaub and ensuring tax revenue.

The most glorious page in its history occurred in 1504, when it successfully withstood the army of Landgrave Wilhelm of Hesse for several weeks. It was because of the exceptional defensive power shown in this battle that the castle was subsequently given the new name "Gutenfels," meaning "Good Rock." Although it suffered heavy damage and even faced demolition during the Thirty Years' War, it was restored in the late 18th century and underwent significant modern renovations.

Today, Gutenfels has been transformed into a tourist destination and features a high-end hotel. If you have the chance, you should try staying there.

## Schönburg Castle (Burg Schönburg)
![Burg Schönburg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Schönburg.jpg)
Located above the city of Oberwesel, the history of Schönburg Castle can be traced back to 911–1166 AD. This fortress, mainly built in the first half of the 12th century, is speculated to have been constructed by the Count Palatine Hermann von Stahleck. From 1166, the Schönburg family became the masters of this site, holding royal fiefs and Rhine customs rights, and ruling Oberwesel as governors for the Emperor. Notably, Schönburg is one of the few historical fortifications to practice "Ganerbenburg" (joint inheritance). This system of equal division among sons allowed the castle to accommodate up to 24 family branches living together at its peak in the 14th century, with a population of about 250. As defensive needs increased, the Archbishop of Trier later added a 20-meter-high shield wall and a vast outer bailey, further strengthening the castle structure.

The most famous member of the Schönburg family was Friedrich von Schönburg, who served the French, Portuguese, and Prussian armies under the name Marshall Schomberg. However, with the outbreak of the War of the Palatine Succession in 1689, French troops burned the castle and the city. The death of Friedrich's son led to the extinction of the family's male line, and Schönburg subsequently fell into a two-hundred-year period of neglect. Nevertheless, its magnificent remains were praised by Victor Hugo as one of the most beautiful ruins in Europe.

The reconstruction of the castle began in 1885 when German-American Major Oakley Rhinelander purchased it and invested heavily in restoration. In the 1950s, ownership returned to the city of Oberwesel, and since 1957, it has been managed by the Hüttl family. In addition to a luxury hotel with medieval flair and a panoramic restaurant, the castle's museum offers an opportunity to gain in-depth knowledge of ancient fortifications.

***There is also a local legend about Schönburg:***

Long ago, seven sisters famous for their beauty and wealth lived at Schönburg, attracting countless knights who came to propose. However, these sisters were arrogant by nature and only toyed with the suitors' feelings by holding tournaments and talent competitions, refusing to make any marriage commitments.

As time passed, the suitors finally lost patience and threatened to besiege the castle if a choice was not made. The sisters feigned compromise, declaring they would decide their fate by drawing lots. However, when the suitors entered the hall the next day with high expectations, they found only seven straw figures dressed in fine clothes sitting on the chairs. It turned out these seven proud young women had already fled down the Rhine by boat, playfully waving and mocking the castle.

But they did not escape far; near Oberwesel, a strange giant wave overturned their boat and swallowed them. Later, seven rocks appeared at the site of the shipwreck, which locals called the "Seven Virgins of Schönburg." They surface whenever the water level drops.

## Katz Castle (Burg Katz)
![Burg Katz]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Katz.jpg)
Katz Castle stands above St. Goarhausen, adjacent to the famous Loreley rock. Its original name is the long and tongue-twisting Burg Neu-Katzenelnbogen, but people prefer to call it Katz Castle, which literally means "Cat Castle." This is not just a simple abbreviation; there is actually a medieval geopolitical anecdote behind it.

As early as 1393, Count Johann III of Katzenelnbogen built this military outpost to defend his family's customs interests on the Rhine while counterbalancing the threatening Mouse Castle (Burg Maus) of the Elector of Trier. The Count once jokingly declared, "The cat will surely catch the little mouse!" Thus, this castle became a giant cat overlooking the valley from the Rhine.

Katz Castle was skillfully built on steep rocky terrain. In the era before artillery became widespread, it was considered an impregnable fortress. The core of the defense is a sixty-meter-high round main tower. The top of the tower served as a lookout for sentries to monitor the surroundings, while the bottom was a dark dungeon. Its unique strategic location allowed the garrison to immediately spot and report any ship bypassing the Loreley bend, ensuring no one escaped the "cat's eye."

However, in 1806, Napoleon's army arrived, and this once-mighty castle fell into ruin under French fire. After nearly a century of silence, architects reconstructed it based on old drawings, preserving the ancient main tower and converting the ruins into living spaces. The new main building features two round high towers facing the Rhine, with magnificent halls and comfortable rooms inside—a perfect location for viewing the river.

Katz Castle briefly served as a boarding school and a welfare institution. Later, it was purchased by a Japanese man who planned to convert it into a hotel specifically for Japanese tourists, but the plan was never realized. Currently, the castle remains private property and is not open to visitors. It is recommended to view it from St. Goar on the opposite bank or from St. Goarhausen.

## Rheinfels Castle (Burg Rheinfels)
![Burg Rheinfels]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Rheinfels.jpg)
Rheinfels Castle is located in the city of St. Goar and was built by Count Diether V of Katzenelnbogen in 1245. Originally, the castle was used to collect tolls on the Rhine, but its astonishing rate of expansion quickly made it the most powerful military facility in the region. As the Katzenelnbogen family rose through political marriages and shrewd financial maneuvering, the castle became an important administrative and trade hub in the 14th century. Together with Katz Castle on the opposite bank, it formed a fearsome blockade on the Rhine.

The most praised aspect of Rheinfels Castle is its legendary reputation for being impregnable. As early as 1256, it successfully withstood a year-long siege by the army of the Rhenish League of Cities (Rheinischer Städtebund), establishing its military status.

In 1479, the castle passed to the Landgraves of Hesse. Around 1570, under the direction of Philipp I, the cold defensive fortifications were converted into a magnificent Renaissance palace. Simultaneously, the outer defenses were expanded to the extreme, making it the largest fortress in the Middle Rhine Valley—even the army of King Louis XIV of France suffered defeat here.

However, this once-mighty fortress was handed over to the French Revolutionary Army in 1794 without resistance and was subsequently ruthlessly blown up, becoming a quarry for the neighboring area. Fortunately, Prince William of Prussia (later Kaiser Wilhelm I) purchased the ruins in 1843, preventing its complete disappearance and preserving about one-third of the walls.

Since 1925, Rheinfels Castle has been owned by the city of St. Goar. Although it is now in ruins (except for the adjacent Schloss Rheinfels resort hotel, converted from the castle's original outer buildings), Rheinfels Castle still retains its medieval majesty. Among the most amazing features are the labyrinthine defensive passages and underground mine tunnels, as well as a 24-meter-long vaulted cellar, considered the largest in Europe. Friends interested in exploring should not forget to bring a flashlight—safety first!

## Mouse Castle (Burg Maus)
![Burg Maus]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Maus.jpg)
Located north of St. Goarhausen, Mouse Castle was built in the mid-14th century by the Archbishop of Trier to consolidate diocesan territory and deter the powerful Counts of Katzenelnbogen. Although its original name was Peterseck or Deuernburg, its widely known title originated from a geopolitical insult full of tension.

At that time, the Count of Katzenelnbogen saw the Archbishop's castle as a thorn in his side. To counter it, he quickly built Katz Castle on a neighboring hill and publicly mocked the smaller Peterseck as nothing more than an insignificant mouse. Unexpectedly, this political verbal war caused the name "Mouse Castle" to circulate for centuries, eventually replacing its original formal name.

Mouse Castle was continuously damaged by war in the centuries following its completion and fell into ruin. In 1898, architect Wilhelm Gärtner from Cologne purchased Mouse Castle and restored it according to the original floor plans, returning it to its medieval appearance.

Mouse Castle is now private property and not open to the public; however, one can visit the falconry center attached to the castle. Many different birds of prey are kept there, and exciting flight shows combined with a medieval atmosphere are offered.

## Sterrenberg Castle (Burg Sterrenberg) and Liebenstein Castle (Burg Liebenstein)
![Burg Sterrenberg&Liebenstein]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Sterrenberg+Burg Liebenstein.jpg)
Here I will share two famous "Feuding Brothers" castles on the Rhine: Sterrenberg and Liebenstein.

As one of the oldest buildings on the Middle Rhine, the history of Sterrenberg can be clearly traced back to 1110 AD and officially became a fief of the Bolanden family in 1190. With its perilous terrain, Sterrenberg became an important stronghold for controlling Rhine customs. The legend of the feuding brothers began with a dramatic conflict in the 13th century.

At that time, the powerful Bolanden family was led by two brothers, Werner and Philipp. Shortly after they divided the family property, Philipp passed away. Werner saw an opportunity and attempted to annex the inheritance, betraying family ties. Although this greedy plunder was briefly blocked, the grudge between the two branches ran deep.

When the controversial inheritance passed to their sister Lukarda, she welcomed a powerful ally: her husband Albrecht von Lewenstein, the eldest illegitimate son of the German King Rudolf I of Habsburg. To protect his wife's property from being taken, Albrecht decided to build his own castle above Sterrenberg. Originally built only as an outer ward of Sterrenberg, this structure eventually expanded into Liebenstein Castle as the family struggle intensified—a castle at a higher altitude capable of rivaling the old one.

In 1320, Sterrenberg fell under the control of the Archbishopric of Trier and became its administrative center on the right bank of the Rhine. Two years later, to guard against the neighbor just steps away, a 2.5-meter-thick "Conflict Wall" (Streitmauer) was built, and the relationship between the two parties hit rock bottom. Even when Liebenstein later became a condominium castle shared by up to ten noble families, the tension between the two sides did not dissipate. Although folklore describes a tragedy of fratricide, historical research indicates that no actual armed conflict broke out between the two parties.

As Sterrenberg's strategic position was replaced by the neighboring Mouse Castle and the bloodlines of both families went extinct in the 15th century, these two castles gradually became ruins. In the 19th century, both castles were owned by Prussia. Sterrenberg came under the management of the State of Rhineland-Palatinate in 1945, and reconstruction began in 1968. Liebenstein remains owned by the descendants of the Baron von Preuschen family, and reconstruction began in the 1970s. Today, both castles have hotels with a medieval atmosphere where you can experience the power games of that era.

**There is another local legend about Sterrenberg and Liebenstein.*** After reading it, I think it sounds like a soap opera plot. Let me share it with you:

Long ago, two brothers from Boppard Castle, Heinrich and Konrad, both fell in love with their childhood friend Hildegard. Unexpectedly, Hildegard's heart belonged to the younger brother, so the honest older brother painfully chose to step aside. To ensure both brothers had a place to live, their wealthy father built two adjacent castles for them on the opposite bank of the Rhine. However, the older brother soon left to join the Crusades to escape the pain in his heart.

To everyone's surprise, after the younger brother got married, he didn't know how to cherish what he had and found life too dull, so he also went abroad. During this time, the older brother returned first. Seeing Hildegard living alone, he lived in the same castle with her to look after her. However, the older brother was a complete gentleman and strictly followed etiquette. Finally, when the younger brother Konrad returned, to everyone's shock, he was accompanied by a Greek mistress! Hildegard collapsed on the spot, and the older brother was so angry he built a high wall between the two castles and challenged him to a duel to the death.

Just as the brothers were about to fight, Hildegard rushed out and shouted, "Stop fighting! Is it enough if I become a nun?" She then entered a convent for the rest of her life. Ironically, the Greek mistress soon ran away with another knight, leaving Konrad with nothing. He eventually died alone of illness.

## Boppard Fortress (Kastell Bodobrica)
![Kastell Bodobric]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Kastell Bodobrica.jpg)
Do you feel that those 10th-century castles aren't old enough? This time, let's try something different and introduce a much older fortification.

Boppard originated as a Celtic settlement named Bodobriga and became Roman territory after Julius Caesar conquered the Germanic regions. To counter the invasion of the Alemanni in the mid-4th century, the fortress Kastell Bodobrica was built in Boppard. After the Roman army withdrew in the 5th century, it miraculously was not abandoned but instead became the core of the city of Boppard.

This rectangular fortress covers an area of 4.7 hectares, with extremely solid outer walls and horseshoe-shaped towers specifically used for mounting powerful ballistae. In addition to military use, the living facilities inside the fortress were very sophisticated, including a bathhouse with a cold and hot pool system. It's not hard to imagine how rich the lives of the Romans were at that time.

Kastell Bodobrica played multiple roles across different centuries. In the 7th century, the Franks left burial sites around the original defensive towers. After the 10th century, parts of the walls were skillfully incorporated into monastery buildings. By the 15th century, it became a multifunctional building for trade and balls. Although only parts of the walls remain today, Kastell Bodobrica is recognized as the best-preserved Roman military site in Germany.

## Marksburg
![Marksburg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Marksburg.jpg)
When mentioning the most beautiful castles in Germany, many people first think of Neuschwanstein. However, I also highly recommend Marksburg on the Rhine!

The earliest record of Marksburg is from 1231 AD. Its predecessor is speculated to have been one of the residences of the local Braubach nobility, subsequently built into a Romanesque castle with the typical triangular design of the Staufer era by the Herren von Eppstein. In 1283, the Count of Katzenelnbogen, the wealthiest in the Rhine region, purchased Marksburg and expanded it extensively in the Gothic style, establishing the current appearance of the castle.

The Landgraviate of Hesse inherited Marksburg in the 15th century and built batteries there, further transforming it into a modern fortress. It was this military upgrade that allowed Marksburg to miraculously survive the Thirty Years' War, making it one of only two miracles in the Rhine Valley that were never captured or destroyed by enemy forces (the other being Pfalzgrafenstein mentioned earlier).

After the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire, Marksburg became part of the Duchy of Nassau and was used as a prison and an infirmary during the Napoleonic era. This inappropriate use indirectly led to the castle being gradually forgotten and neglected. Although the Prussian royal family later took a liking to it and even desired to restore it, actual progress was minimal. In 1900, with the support of Kaiser Wilhelm II, the German Castle Association (Deutsche Burgenvereinigung) purchased the castle for one thousand marks. Architect Bodo Ebhardt carried out large-scale restoration to return Marksburg to its medieval appearance. To this day, Marksburg remains the headquarters of the association.

The interior of Marksburg is also very worth seeing. The entrance has multiple layers of gates, and above the "Schartentor" gate, there are nozzles that could attack enemies with hot oil. It's easy to imagine how difficult it was to attack Marksburg. In the battery area, you can not only see authentic Napoleonic-era cannons at close range but also find the best spot to overlook the Rhine Valley. The interior of the castle accurately restores medieval daily life; the huge kitchen fireplace shows the scale of meal preparation for the garrison, and the records in the wine cellar showing a staggering daily consumption of two to three liters of wine per person make one marvel at the drinking culture of the time. Additionally, the Knight's Hall for banquets, the solemn chapel, and the cold torture chamber all help us reconstruct the former lives of Marksburg's residents.

***Did you know? You don't have to fly to Europe to see Marksburg; there is an original-scale replica in Miyakojima.***

I believe many friends who have visited Miyakojima definitely visited the Ueno German Culture Village. The castle in the park was built according to the model of Marksburg!

But why is there a German castle on Miyakojima? Behind this lies a historical connection between Germany and Japan: in the summer of 1873, the German merchant ship Robertson hit a reef in the waters off Miyakojima due to a typhoon. Local islanders risked their lives to rescue all crew members and took care of them carefully, eventually helping them return home safely. This warm humanitarian act deeply moved Kaiser Wilhelm I, who in 1876 dispatched a warship to the island to establish the "Emperor's Philanthropy Monument," recording this transnational friendship in both German and Chinese. This eventually led to the birth of the German Culture Village in the 1990s.

## Philippsburg Palace (Schloss Philippsburg)
Marksburg has a long-lost brother: Philippsburg Palace, located at the foot of the mountain.

Philippsburg Palace was commissioned by Landgrave Philipp II of Hesse-Rheinfels between 1568 and 1571 and served as the residence for the Landgraves of Hesse-Rheinfels and their successors for many years. Between 1682 and 1689, the Hesse family successfully defended Rheinfels Castle, which also protected Marksburg and Philippsburg from destruction. However, in later years, as it lost its function as an official residence, the building complex gradually fell into neglect.

In 1822, Philippsburg Palace was sold to entrepreneur Johann Christian Heberlein, which led to the originally magnificent building evolving into its current, more modest appearance. With the construction of the railway line on the right bank of the Rhine, the defensive facilities surrounding Philippsburg Palace were gradually demolished.

In 1997, the German Castle Association acquired Philippsburg Palace and established the European Castle Institute (Europäisches Burgeninstitut) here. Thanks to sponsorship from the American Hanika family, after the renovation of the main building was completed, Philippsburg Palace officially reopened to the public at the end of 1999. Thus, Marksburg and Philippsburg, two originally complementary castles, were once again managed as a whole by the same institution.

## Martinsburg Palace (Schloss Martinsburg)
![Schloss Martinsburg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Schloss Martinsburg.jpg)
Starting in 1292, the Archbishop of Mainz obtained a share of the Boppard customs duties with the permission of King Adolf von Nassau. Since Burg Lahneck, which controlled this area, was too far from the Rhine, the Archbishop of Mainz built Martinsburg Palace directly on the banks of the Rhine to ensure the direct collection of Rhine customs.

The oldest parts of Martinsburg Palace include a residential area with two corner towers and the hexagonal foundation of the main tower located on the south side of the building. With the construction of the Oberlahnstein city walls, Martinsburg Palace was also expanded and became part of the city's defense system. As the center of economically prosperous Oberlahnstein, it was permanently staffed by customs officers, guards, and other officials. From the late Middle Ages, it became a temporary residence for the Electors of Mainz, its importance far exceeding that of Burg Lahneck.

In the 18th century, Martinsburg Palace was remodeled several times in the Baroque style. The original moat was filled in during the 19th century and turned into gardens and green spaces. Martinsburg Palace is currently private property and houses several apartments, offices, and an old mint. During recent restoration processes, precious early Neoclassical murals, stucco ceilings, and parquet floors were discovered. The restoration of these cultural assets was made possible through the support of the German Foundation for Monument Protection.

## Lahneck Castle (Burg Lahneck)
![Burg Lahneck]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Burg Lahneck.jpg)
Since we mentioned Martinsburg Palace, we must introduce Burg Lahneck, located at the confluence of the Lahn and Rhine rivers. It was commissioned by Archbishop Siegfried von Eppstein of Mainz between 1240 and 1245. It features an 8-meter-deep moat and a 29-meter-high main tower. For a long time, it served as the northernmost military outpost of the Electorate of Mainz and protected the nearby silver mines.

Burg Lahneck was looted by Swedish troops during the Thirty Years' War, causing its strategic position to gradually decline. During the War of the Palatine Succession, it was burned by the French army, at one point leaving only the main tower. However, its charm provided much inspiration to the great writer Goethe, who wrote the famous poem "Geistergruß" (Spirit's Greeting) here. Additionally, the tragic legend of the Scottish girl Idilia Dubb, who was trapped and died at the top of the tower, adds a mysterious and sorrowful literary color to the castle (although the authenticity of the event is questioned by various parties).

In 1852, Scottish railway entrepreneur Edward A. Moriarty purchased Burg Lahneck and carried out large-scale renovations in the Neo-Gothic style. Today, Burg Lahneck is owned by the Mischke family. The interior of the castle displays precious collections, such as furniture spanning different eras and styles, ancient armor, painting collections, and the famous Gobelin tapestries in the Knight's Hall.

## Stolzenfels Palace (Schloss Stolzenfels)
![Schloss Stolzenfels]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Schloss Stolzenfels.jpg)
Another castle located at the confluence of the Lahn and Rhine rivers is Stolzenfels Palace, situated on the left bank of the Rhine, facing Burg Lahneck.

Its history dates back to the 13th century when Archbishop Arnold von Isenburg of Trier ordered the construction of this military fortress between 1244 and 1248 to counter the Lahneck Castle built by the Archbishop of Mainz. The castle is located at the junction of the territories of the three Electors of Trier, Mainz, and Cologne, making its location highly strategic. Until the early 15th century, it was an important base for collecting Rhine tolls. However, it was destroyed by fire during the War of the Palatine Succession in 1688 and subsequently remained in ruins for over a hundred years.

In 1823, the city of Koblenz gifted the castle ruins to the future Prussian King Frederick William IV. He subsequently hired the master architect Karl Friedrich Schinkel to remodel it into a summer residence. The reconstruction project skillfully integrated the medieval defense tower with an elegant Neo-Gothic chapel and luxurious living spaces, making it a masterpiece of Prussian Rhine Romantic architecture.

The interior of the building contains many precious collection items, including medieval glass art and furniture, murals, curtains, and decorative elements from the 19th century, fully showcasing the world of the Prussian aristocracy during the Romantic period. The surrounding landscape design is also worth seeing. In the nine-hectare landscape park created by the landscape master Peter Joseph Lenné, exquisite pavilion gardens are connected to the castle courtyard, allowing the royal couple to enjoy the view of the Rhine from their bedroom at any time.

By the way, when Queen Victoria and Prince Albert of the United Kingdom first visited Germany, they were invited to stay here, giving this palace a perfect score in both history and beauty!

## Ehrenbreitstein Fortress (Festung Ehrenbreitstein)
![Festung Ehrenbreitstein]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Mittelrheintal/Festung Ehrenbreitstein.jpg)
If we were to find a perfect grand finale for the Middle Rhine Valley castle series, Ehrenbreitstein Fortress is undoubtedly the most suitable choice. This thousand-year-old fortress is located in Koblenz, overlooking the confluence of the Rhine and Moselle rivers.

Around 1000 AD, Ehrenbert of the Conradine family had already built a castle named Ehrenbertstein at this key strategic location, named after himself. It later became the property of Archbishop Poppo of Trier and was continuously expanded by his successors. In the 15th century, Archbishop Richard von Greiffenclau of Trier transformed it into a solid fortress capable of resisting artillery attacks and equipped it with heavy artillery to counter enemy forces. Among them, the most famous—and the largest cannon in Europe at the time—the "Vogel Greif," is still on display here.

Ehrenbreitstein Fortress successfully resisted multiple sieges by French troops. It wasn't until 1799 that the fortress garrison was forced to surrender due to exhaustion of supplies after a years-long blockade by the French army, who subsequently blew it up. However, its story did not end there. In 1815, as the Rhine region was assigned to the Kingdom of Prussia, Koblenz was expanded into a fortress city by order of King Frederick William III. Simultaneously, through the efforts of General Aster and Baron von Huene, Ehrenbreitstein Fortress was rebuilt and equipped with thicker walls, becoming one of the safest and most impregnable fortifications in the world at that time. Its extraordinary military status continued until the end of World War I, when, due to the restrictions of the Treaty of Versailles, it could no longer be used as a military defense facility and became an ordinary barracks.

Since 1970, the fortress has been managed by the cultural heritage department of Rhineland-Palatinate and transformed into a diversified cultural space. It houses the State Museum of Koblenz (Landesmuseum Koblenz), covering archaeological, technical history, and other rich exhibitions. When visiting this monument, besides choosing to arrive on foot, taking the cable car across the Rhine is an excellent experience. Enjoying the magnificent river view and walking through the vast fortress allows you to personally feel its thousand-year history.
