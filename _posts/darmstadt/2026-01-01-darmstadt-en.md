## City History of Darmstadt
Darmstadt is located about 35 kilometers south of Frankfurt and was first mentioned at the end of the 11th century under the name "Darmundestat." At that time, there were two settlement cores: one east of the Darmbach stream and the other surrounding the market square and the city church. Many theories exist regarding the origin of the name Darmstadt. However, since the suffix "-stat" is often combined with personal names, the prevailing explanation today is "the settlement of Darimund," rather than the popular joke referring to it as "Intestine City" (Darm-Stadt).

In the first half of the 13th century, the Counts of Katzenelnbogen built a new castle on the foundation of an old residence to control the northern section of the Bergstraße, a major transport route, and to manage traffic between Heidelberg and Frankfurt. On July 23, 1330, Holy Roman Emperor Ludwig IV (Ludwig der Bayer) granted city privileges to Darmstadt in recognition of the loyal service of Count Wilhelm I of Katzenelnbogen. This meant Darmstadt gained the right to build city walls and hold markets. The walls built in the following decades (remnants of which still exist today) defined the size of Darmstadt until the end of the 16th century.

In 1479, sovereignty over Darmstadt passed to the Landgraviate of Hesse. Subsequently, the castle gradually fell into disrepair, and agriculture and forestry began to decline. It was not until the establishment of the Landgraviate of Hesse-Darmstadt in 1567, when Darmstadt was elevated to the capital of the landgraviate, that the city's conditions improved.

In 1695, Landgrave Ernst Ludwig laid the foundation for the city's westward expansion, creating what is now the city center's Luisenplatz and Luisenstraße. After Napoleon established the Grand Duchy of Hesse in 1806, the New Town in the west emerged to accommodate the growing state administration and court members. The Old Town was gradually marginalized, and the city center shifted to the newly built Luisenplatz.

In the second half of the 19th century, Darmstadt entered a period of rapid industrialization. Well-known companies such as Merck and Schenck were established and continue to provide many jobs in Darmstadt today. Darmstadt has also been actively involved in education and research across various scientific fields. In addition to the TU Darmstadt and the Darmstadt University of Applied Sciences (Hochschule Darmstadt), many research institutes and the European Space Operations Centre are also located here.

At the same time, arts and culture developed rapidly. The last Grand Duke of Darmstadt, Ernst Ludwig, shared a great passion and talent for art with his ancestor Ludwig I. During his reign, the Hessian State Museum was built, and in 1899, the Darmstadt Artists' Colony (Darmstädter Künstlerkolonie) was established. Artists were invited to reside there and showcase a variety of works spanning architecture, arts and crafts, and everyday design, striving to bring art to all levels of society. It was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2021.

## Wedding Tower (Hochzeitsturm)
![Hochzeitsturm]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt_Hochzeitsturm.jpg)
Darmstadt's Mathildenhöhe gathers almost all of the city's landmarks, the most famous of which is undoubtedly the Wedding Tower. It is an Expressionist architectural masterpiece designed by Joseph Maria Olbrich, completed in 1908, and named to commemorate the marriage of Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig of Hesse to Eleonore zu Solms-Hohensolms-Lich.

The most distinctive feature here is the unique five-finger shape at the top of the tower. At nearly 50 meters high, the tower has an excellent observation platform overlooking the city scenery; on a clear day, you can even see Frankfurt. Inside the tower, there are two spacious rooms that still serve as venues for wedding ceremonies today.

Connected to the Wedding Tower is the Exhibition Building, also designed by Joseph Maria Olbrich, which remains an important venue for international contemporary art and exhibitions of art and culture from around 1900. Hidden beneath the exhibition hall is a brick reservoir built between 1877 and 1880. At that time, this was a park on the outskirts of the city, and the reservoir was a vital part of Darmstadt's water supply system, once providing a stable water source for the entire city. Today, it is well-preserved beneath the exhibition hall as a witness to technical and architectural history.

This architectural ensemble was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2021.

## Russian Chapel
![Darmstadt_Russian_Church]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt_Russian_Church.jpg)
Nestled among the buildings on Mathildenhöhe is a magnificent Russian Chapel. It is not part of the Artists' Colony (Künstlerkolonie) but was built independently in 1899 and has deep connections to the Russian Imperial Family.

Alix, the sister of Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig of Hesse, was a granddaughter of Queen Victoria and the wife of the last Russian Tsar, Nicholas II. After marrying into the Russian Imperial Family, she converted to Orthodoxy and took the name Alexandra Feodorovna. To welcome the Tsar's family for visits, this chapel was specially designed by Russian architect Leontij Benua and decorated by the Symbolist master and avant-garde artist Victor Vasnezov.

In the apse of the chapel, we can see a finely detailed mural of the Virgin Mary holding the Christ Child, with floral patterns blending Byzantine and Art Nouveau styles extending to the dome. The golden onion domes, indispensable to a Russian church, still shine brightly, creating a magnificent exotic flair.

## Prince Carl Palace (Prinz-Carl Palais)
![Prinz-Carl_Palais]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt_Prinz-Carl_Palais.jpg)
Prince Carl Palace is the birthplace of Elizabeth Feodorovna. She was the older sister of the last Russian Empress, Alexandra Feodorovna. In Russia, Elizabeth was known for her beauty and her charitable work for the poor; she was later canonized as a saint by the Patriarch of Russia. The former palace now serves as the headquarters for the German Federal Pension Insurance institution.

It's quite fascinating how the nobility used to travel everywhere and marry across borders; who would have thought that a place so far from Russia would have sites related to the Tsar!

## Ludwig Monument (Ludwig Denkmal)
![Darmstadt Luisenplatz]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt Luisenplatz.jpg)
The Ludwig Monument stands alongside the Wedding Tower as one of Darmstadt's landmarks. It was built to commemorate Ludwig I, the first Grand Duke of the Grand Duchy of Hesse, and the Constitution of the Grand Duchy of Hesse promulgated under his reign in 1820.

Initially, the monument was planned for Mathildenplatz, but through the strong advocacy of architect Georg Moller, the final site was changed to Luisenplatz. This decision not only made the monument more prominent but also echoed his vision for the New Town, making it a focal point of the urban landscape.

Construction on the monument officially began in 1840 and was completed four years later. It stands nearly 40 meters high, with the statue itself being 5 meters tall. Inside the monument is a hidden 172-step spiral staircase leading to the observation platform at the top. On specific days, it is open to visitors to climb to the top and overlook the scenery of Darmstadt.

## St. Ludwig's Church (Ludwigskirche)
![Darmstadt_Ludwigskirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt_Ludwigskirche.jpg)
The construction of Darmstadt's first Catholic church since the Reformation is closely linked to the rise of the Landgraviate of Hesse-Darmstadt, the establishment of the Grand Duchy of Hesse, and the subsequent city expansion. Following the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire, the Grand Duchy of Hesse annexed a large amount of secularized territory from the Electorate of Mainz. The government subsequently promoted a policy of religious tolerance and sought to demonstrate the spirit of religious integration by building a grand Catholic church. This concept was realized in 1822 in a meticulously designed church building.

The design of St. Ludwig's Church was inspired by the Pantheon in Rome and was designed by architect Georg Moller. Its most distinctive features are its magnificent domed hall, soft pink exterior walls, and 28 internal marble columns. Upon entering the church, one immediately feels a classical and solemn atmosphere. However, St. Ludwig's Church was heavily damaged during the Great Bombing of 1944. Fortunately, most of its structure was preserved. After years of careful restoration and reconstruction, St. Ludwig's Church was able to regain its former glory.

## Orangerie
![Darmstadt_Orangerie]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt_Orangerie.jpg)
Many European palaces feature an Orangerie. As the name suggests, these buildings were originally used to grow citrus and other fruit trees, functioning like a greenhouse to protect plants from the winter cold. This concept originated in 16th-century Italy and France, with the Orangerie at the Palace of Versailles being a prime example. In addition to being a place for plant cultivation, Orangeries were also important venues for social activities such as banquets and concerts for the nobility. As the capital of the Grand Duchy of Hesse, Darmstadt naturally has an Orangerie as well.

In 1714, Landgrave Ernst Ludwig of Hesse-Darmstadt purchased the Harnischhof estate on this site and transformed it into a French Baroque-style Orangerie. It served various purposes over time. Initially, it was a place for noble social gatherings and feasts. Between 1848 and 1849, the Bessunger Bürgergarde (Bessungen Citizens' Guard) practiced here. From the mid-19th century, concerts and exhibitions began to be held in the building. In 1870, the Horticultural Society held Germany's first rose show here, starting a tradition of large-scale horticultural exhibitions. During the Franco-Prussian War of 1870–71, the Orangerie was converted into a field hospital, and during the two World Wars, it was used for growing potatoes and vegetables.

Between 1945 and 1972, the building in the Orangerie was used as a temporary venue for the Hessian State Theatre, with several annexes added. From 1975 to 1978, restoration work began, and the later additions were demolished to restore the Orangerie as much as possible to its original state. Today, its historical appearance has been largely restored, continuing to showcase its former Baroque elegance.

## Darmstadt Residential Palace (Residenzschloss Darmstadt)
The history of the Darmstadt Residential Palace dates back to the early 13th century, evolving from a moated castle built by the Counts of Katzenelnbogen, which itself was established on the site of an early medieval defensive residence.

The Landgraves of Hesse acquired the castle in 1479 but did not provide much additional management. During the Schmalkaldic War, all wooden structures were burned down until they were later rebuilt as stone structures under Landgrave Ludwig IV.

In 1567, Darmstadt was elevated to the capital of the Landgraviate, and the castle was subsequently expanded into a palace. The walls were reinforced with bastions, and the moat was deepened. Many new buildings and towers were added over the following century. However, after a fire at the palace in 1715, many of these buildings no longer exist. Subsequent restoration and reconstruction work was halted due to financial difficulties. It was not until the early 19th century that the interior decoration of the two wings was gradually completed.

After World War I, the palace became the property of the People's State of Hesse (Volksstaat Hessen). Since 1924, the Palace Museum (Schlossmuseum) has been located in some rooms of the old palace. During the Darmstadt Fire Night (Darmstädter Brandnacht) in 1944, the entire palace complex was burned down. Reconstruction began in 1946 and was completed in the early 1970s. During the reconstruction process, ruins were preserved as much as possible, and the exterior basically maintains its original appearance.

Today, the palace is home to the TU Darmstadt, the German Poland Institute (Deutsches Polen-Institut), and the Palace Museum. There is also a beer garden on the upper floor of the palace—enjoying a drink here on a summer night is undoubtedly a treat!

## Darmstadt State Theatre (Staatstheater Darmstadt)
![Darmstadt_Staatstheater]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt_Staatstheater.jpg)
The Darmstadt State Theatre is located next to St. Ludwig's Church, and the vast green space in front of it is a popular spot for citizens to stroll and chat.

It might be hard to imagine that before the theatre moved here in 1972, this was the site of the New Palace (Neues Palais), the residence of the Grand Duke of Hesse. Even more surprising is that during World War II, the New Palace once served as a branch for the Gestapo.

In 1944, the New Palace was heavily damaged in an air raid and was completely demolished in 1955. Subsequently, in 1961, it was decided to build a modern, fully functional new theatre on this site, eventually resulting in the modern State Theatre we see today.

## Waldspirale (Forest Spiral)
![Waldspirale]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt_Waldspirale.jpg)
The Austrian architect Friedensreich Hundertwasser designed 25 buildings in his lifetime, and the Waldspirale (Forest Spiral) in Darmstadt was his final work.

This imaginative residential complex features 105 unique apartments. Perhaps echoing Darmstadt's historical links with the Russian Imperial Family, the design of the Waldspirale incorporates Oriental styles, featuring golden onion-shaped spires, colorful ceramic columns, and irregularly placed windows that seem to dance. Since its completion in September 2000, this building has become a highlight of Darmstadt.

Regrettably, Hundertwasser did not live to see its completion, as he passed away six months before it was finished. However, his unique architectural philosophy—allowing buildings to grow like living things, with organic forms and vibrant colors, integrating natural elements, and creating a humanized living environment—lives on forever in this work.

## International Convention Center Darmstadtium
![Darmstadtium]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Darmstadt/Darmstadt_Darmstadtium.jpg)
Darmstadt also has a special international convention center called Darmstadtium. Its name is the same as the element with atomic number 110, Darmstadtium (Ds). This is because the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research (GSI Helmholtzzentrum für Schwerionenforschung GmbH) successfully synthesized this superheavy element for the first time in 1994 in nearby Darmstadt-Wixhausen. Interestingly, because 110 is the police emergency number in Germany, Darmstadtium also has the informal nickname "Policium."

Not only Darmstadtium, but the GSI research center has also successfully synthesized various superheavy elements from atomic numbers 107 to 112, making it one of the world's leading heavy ion research institutions.

If you look closely, the various conference halls within the Darmstadtium International Convention Center are also named after different elements, creating a scientific atmosphere that combines science with modern architecture!

## Must-Eat Malatang in Darmstadt
"Mala Gebi" can be considered the most famous restaurant in Darmstadt. I think the Malatang here is better than anywhere else in Germany. A friend of mine actually couldn't find good Malatang in other cities and was moved to tears the first time they ate here! 🤣 So I have to crown it the "ceiling" of German Malatang. 🤭

It's self-service here—pick whatever ingredients you like, pay by weight, and the broth is rich, fragrant, and numbing, with spice levels you can choose yourself. If you don't eat spicy food, there are other broth options available. Following the renovation in 2026, there are also various specialty noodle dishes to choose from!
