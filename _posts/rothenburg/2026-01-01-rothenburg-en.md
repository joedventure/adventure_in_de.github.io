## History of Rothenburg ob der Tauber
Rothenburg ob der Tauber is one of the three remaining towns in Germany that still possess completely intact city walls.

Its history dates back to at least the 11th century, when the Counts of Comburg established a castle in the southwestern part of today's city. Following the extinction of the Comburg line in the early 12th century, the Staufer dynasty took over much of their territory, including the area around Rothenburg. In 1142, King Konrad III of the Holy Roman Empire built a second castle in what is now the Castle Garden (Burggarten), significantly increasing the region's importance. The name "Rothenburg" was first recorded in 1144, meaning "red fortress above the Tauber." On May 15, 1274, Emperor Rudolf I of Habsburg granted the citizens of Rothenburg special privileges, further accelerating the town's development.

In the autumn of 1631, due to the progression of the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648), Rothenburg accidentally became a major battlefield. The town changed hands several times before finally falling to imperial forces led by General Johann T’Serclaes von Tilly. It is said that on the night of October 30–31, the city was authorized to be looted. Citizens reportedly gathered in the Market Square to plead for mercy. At the same time, Tilly likely wished to avoid a repeat of the "Magdeburg Wedding" (Magdeburger Hochzeit)—the brutal massacre and burning of Magdeburg months earlier—and thus spared the city.

According to a legend that emerged around 1770, the former Mayor Georg Nusch saved Rothenburg from destruction by drinking 3 ¼ liters of wine in one gulp upon General Tilly's command. Since 1881, the play "The Master Draught" (Der Meistertrunk) has been performed annually at Pentecost to commemorate this event. In 2016, the play was recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage.

## Plönlein
![Plönlein]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Rotheburg/Plönlein.jpg)
When visiting Rothenburg, how could one not mention its most iconic landmark, the Plönlein? The name means "small square by the fountain," which sounds like something straight out of a fairy tale, doesn't it?

Standing in the center of the square, you are greeted by the square itself, a fountain, half-timbered houses, and two towers. It feels as if you’ve instantly traveled back to the Middle Ages (except for the cameras everywhere! 😂).

Furthermore, this spot has served as the inspiration for medieval settings in many films and video games, including Disney's classic Pinocchio.

## Rothenburg City Walls
![Rothenburg Stadtmauer]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Rotheburg/Rothenburg_Stadtmauer.jpg)
The city walls surrounding Rothenburg stretch for about four kilometers and feature a total of 46 towers. It is said that when looking north toward the Old Town from the Kalkturm, the silhouettes of St. James' Church (Jakobskirche), the Town Hall tower, the Franciscan Church (Franziskanerkirche), and the Castle Gate remind many of the view of Jerusalem's Temple Mount. Consequently, Rothenburg is also known as the "Franconian Jerusalem" (fränkisches Jerusalem). For those who have been to Jerusalem, do you feel a similar vibe?

Featured Gates and Towers:

1. Rödertor: One of the five main gates of Rothenburg’s outer fortifications. Behind the gate, there are two customs checkpoints and guardrooms.

2. Würzburger Tor: Built in 1388. Its second name, Galgentor (Gallows Gate), originates from the gallows that were once located outside the gate.

3. Spitaltor: The Spital district was the last area of Rothenburg's Old Town to be enclosed by walls. At the Spital Gate, we can see the town's most advanced fortifications, including the bastion completed in 1546. Above the gate, Rothenburg greets visitors in Latin: Pax intrantibus, salus exeuntibus ("Peace to those who enter, health to those who leave"). This motto is so fitting that Tokyo Haneda Airport adopted it as its own motto.

4. Markusturm: Built around 1200 along with the first ring of city walls, the Marcus Tower and the nearby Röder Fountain (Röderbrunnen) create a picturesque scene. Nestled next to the tower is the Custom House, built in 1264. For over 500 years, it has provided lodging for travelers and continues to operate today as the family-run Romantik Hotel Markusturm.
